Hyperthermia. You're asked to assess a patient in recovery who has just had a right shoulder replacement. Their core temperature is 33 degrees centigrade. What mechanisms are you aware of by which heat loss may have occurred? This question is testing your knowledge of basic science as well as its clinical application. Heat loss may occur through radiation, convection, evaporation and conduction. Through respiration and through the influence of anesthesia.
0:35
Can I describe radiation? Radiation is the transfer of energy between two objects that are not necessarily touching. A hot object emits radiation with wavelengths in the infrared region. The radiation carries energy from the hot object, thus cooling it down. The degree of heat loss depends on the difference in temperature between the two objects and the surface area exposed. Radiation accounts for 40 to 50% of heat loss during anesthesia.
1:15
Can you describe convection? The air layer closest to the body is warmed up by conduction. As the air temperature increases, the air rises and is carried away by convection currents. This accounts for about 30% of heat loss, but can be accelerated if the body is exposed to high air flow, for example with laminar flow systems. Can you describe evaporation? As the moisture on the body's surface evaporates, it gains the latent heat of vaporization from the body and the body cools down.
1:57
This accounts for 20 to 25% of heat loss. The process is accelerated if there is a large moisture area exposed during surgery, for example major abdominal surgery or major orthopedic surgery. Can you describe conduction? Conduction is where heat is passed between a hot object, which is in direct contact with a cooler object. During anesthesia, this accounts for only 3 to 5% of heat loss, unless the body is touching an efficient heat conductor such as a metal table. Can you describe how heat is lost through respiration?
2:43
During respiration, heat is lost through evaporation and through warming of inspired air. This can account for up to 10% of heat loss, but will be minimized by the use of heat and moisture exchanges. Can you describe how general anesthesia affects heat loss? During anesthesia, peripheral vasodilitation means that heat is initially redistributed from the warmer core to the cooler peripheries. Anesthetic agents can also affect hypotherlamic central heat regulation. The hypotherlamic threshold is related to minimum alveolar concentration, so thermoregulate re-responses and not triggered until the temperature has dropped much further than normal.
3:35
During anesthesia, the patient has reduced metabolic heat production as muscle activity and brain metabolism are both decreased. There is an additional cooling effect from the use of anesthetic gases and intravenous fluids. It is also worth remembering that patients often arrive into the anesthetic room cold from the ward. What are the clinical consequences of hypothermia? Try and classify your answer here. The clinical effects are cardio-respiratory, metabolic and the effects on surgical outcome.
4:16
The cardio-respiratory effects are an increase in oxygen consumption and a decrease in cardiac output. Hypothermia also increases the risk of arrhythmias. The Oxygen Hema-Globin Disassociation Curve is shifted to the left, meaning that oxygen delivery is decreased. Blood viscosity is increased and therefore the risk of intravascular stasis is higher. The metabolic effects are a decrease in metabolic rate of about 6% for every degree fall in core temperature. Enzyme reactions and intramedri metabolism are also affected if core temperature is less than 34 degrees C.
5:03
This has an effect on drug actions which may be prolonged. This particularly applies to neuromuscular blocking agents. Renal and hepatic functions are depressed. The patient may have a diuresis due to the failure of active reabsorption of sodium and water. As glucose utilization falls, the patient may develop hyperglycemia. Hypothermia can also have negative effects on the clotting cascade.
5:36
Hypothermia may affect surgical outcome as immune function is depressed so wound infection rates are increased. Wound healing is also compromised and therefore hospital stay may be prolonged. How would you manage the patient in recovery? Concentrate here on the management of severe hypothermia. Hypothermia of gradual onset is best managed with gradual rewarming at about 1 degrees centigrade per hour. Rewarming can be external or internal.
6:13
External rewarming is using forced warm air heating blankets or radiant heaters. Internal rewarming can be via the use of warm intravenous, intra gastric or intraperator needle fluids or by bladder irrigation via a urinary catheter. The most efficient way of rewarming but by far the most invasive is to put the patient on cardiac bypass. Where have you seen hypothermia being used in a theatre setting? The examiner is looking to see that you have had exposure to a wide range of anesthetic specialties. Hypothermia is used in cardiac surgery when the patient is put on to cardiac pulmonary bypass.
7:00
If hypothermia is used, the patient is typically called to a temperature of 28 to 32 degrees. In more complex cardiac surgery, for example surgery on the aortic root, deep hypothermia is induced, where the patient is called to a temperature of 15 to 22 degrees, which allows periods of low blood flow or circulatory arrest. Do you know of any clinical applications of induced hypothermia? This is asking for any trials you may be aware of in the recent literature. Induced hypothermia has been proven to be beneficial for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest survivors.
7:45
In patients with a return of spontaneous circulation, induced hypothermia has been shown to result in a remarkably more favourable neurological outcome than in patients given standard treatment. As a result, the International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation has recommended that all patients with a return of spontaneous circulation following an out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, where the initial rhythm was ventricular fibrillation, should be treated with induced hypothermia of 32 to 34 degrees centigrade for 12 to 24 hours. Are you aware of any other therapeutic use of hypothermia? There have been trials looking at therapeutic hypothermia in traumatic head injury, and although hypothermia is effective in reducing interest or re-broad pressures, there has been no outcome benefits shown. This is thought to be due to the increased incidence of infectious complications.
8:52
Hypothermia has also been used in newborn hypoxic ischemic encephalopathy, and although there was no benefit seen in those patients with the most abnormal EEGs, there did seem to be some protective effect in patients with intermediate EEG with no increase in complication rate. Other areas where hypothermia has been trialed are in neurosurgery, in-eschemic stroke, and in aortic surgery for spinal protection. Although preliminary studies have suggested some positive effects, no conclusive results have been achieved.

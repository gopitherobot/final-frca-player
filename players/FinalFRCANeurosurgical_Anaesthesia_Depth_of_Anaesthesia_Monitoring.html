What is awareness? There are a few definitions that you should be able to recite and convince the examinists

Awareness can be classified into explicit memory, implicit memory and deliberate awareness. Explicit memory can be defined as the intentional or conscious recollection of experiences. Anesthetic awareness specifically refers to explicit memory of intraoperative events,

which involve spontaneous or conscious recall. The explicit recall may occur with or without the sensation of pain and recollections may be vivid, such as operating room conversation or vague, such as dreams or unpleasant sensations associated with the operation. Implicit memory is perception without conscious recall. The patient denies recall but may remember something under hypnosis or with repeated

It also occurs during some neurosurgical procedures when the patient is woken up to assess where the surgery has affected or will affect important areas. This however is less common now since the introduction of devices to monitor evoked potentials intraoperatively.

Techniques which use a form of EEG monitoring would include pure EEG analysis, cerebral function

Other techniques include the isolated forearm technique, lower esophageal contractility, somatosensory evoked potentials and frontalis scalp EMG. Pure EEG tracings are obtained using a standard 19 electrode method. They are therefore time-consuming, impractical and require an expert to interpret them, which limits their usefulness for monitoring depth of anesthesia.

They also suffer from the fact that different anesthetic agents generate different EEG patterns and that various events such as hypoxia, hypotension and hypercarbia also affect the EEG pattern. Both of these further complicate interpretation making it impractical to use these for routine monitoring. Serial function monitors, frequency domain analysis and compressed spectral array are all monitors which process and modify the conventional EEG to simplify the information.

This is then further analyzed with respect to the frequency distribution, the power contained within a waveform i.e. its amplitude and the relationships between waves of different frequencies. The output is displayed in different ways but overall leads to a trace that is easier to interpret than the raw EEG.

There are two types of smooth muscle contraction that can be detected in the lower esophagus. These are spontaneous contractions and provoked contractions. Both of these reduce in latency and amplitude during a general anesthetic. The provoked contractions result from a sudden distinction of the esophagus which can be

There is renewed research particularly in auditory vote potentials for use as a depth of anesthesia monitor. Evoked potential monitors measure electrical activity in certain areas of the brain in response
11:53
to stimulation of specific sensory pathways. A super maximal stimulus is applied to a peripheral nerve whilst a recording scalp electrode is placed over the appropriate sensory area. Most anesthetic agents increase the latency and decrease the amplitude of these responses in a dose dependent manner. Visual evoked potentials are less reliable than auditory evoked potentials.
12:21
When using auditory evoked potentials a repetitive auditory stimulus at 6 to 10 hertz is played to the patient through headphones. A response waveform can then be plotted against time. The repeated sampling enables the signal to be identified from the background EEG. The waveform formed consists of three waveform peaks. Firstly, the brain stem response.
12:50
Secondly, the mid-cautical response. And finally, the late-cautical response. The brain stem and late-cautical responses do not correspond with depth of anesthesia. However, the mid-cautical response does. There is a decrease in amplitude and an increase in latency of waves in a dose dependent manner. By processing the raw waveform, the auditory evoked potential index can be calculated,
13:18
which gives a number which might be more meaningful to interpret than the raw waveform. A value of greater than 80 would indicate an awake patient, and a value of less than 50 would indicate an anesthetized patient. There is a sudden increase in the AEP index when a patient changes from being asleep to being awake. Therefore, it can be easier to distinguish between an asleep patient and an awake patient than with other monitors, such as the BIS.
13:51
However, there is interpatient variability for the index value at which this change takes place. Therefore, it is difficult to define a sensitive and specific cutoff value, whilst in an individual patient, the pattern is very reproducible with awakening repeatedly occurring at the same index values. What do you know about BIS monitoring? Are you aware of any recent studies into the use of BIS?
14:16
There are two or three reasonably important recent studies that you will probably be expected to have heard of, even if you don't know the details well. The BIS monitor displays a real-time EEG trace from the fronto temporal electrodes, which is then processed into a number on a scale of 0 to 100. A 100 represents normal cortical activity, 0 represents complete absence of cortical activity, and a value of less than 60 is thought to represent a value below which there is a very low
14:53
probability of post-operative recall. There are several limitations to BIS monitoring. Firstly, there is no gold standard to compare BIS 2. It is therefore difficult to evaluate its effectiveness. The values themselves tend to have significant interpatient variability, whilst one patient is asleep at a BIS value of 75, another may still be awake at a value of 70.
15:20
The BIS values show a continuum from awake to asleep, and therefore there is no clear cut point, unlike auditory evoke potentials, where transition is made from asleep to awake. BIS values demonstrate a dose response relationship with inhalational and hypnotic intravenous agents, which is independent of the agent used and correlates with clinical assessments of level of consciousness. The one exception to this is when using ketamine, which causes EEG activation and therefore complicates BIS interpretation.
15:57
Studies have shown that BIS values decrease when muscle relaxation drugs are given, suggesting that the patient is at a deeper level of anesthesia, whilst the values do not change when opiate drugs or nitrous oxide are given, suggesting no change in depth of anesthesia. These findings are obviously conflicting with what we know from clinical experience, and should therefore make the anesthetist cautious if delivering an anesthetic to a targeted number. There are two major recent trials assessing the effectiveness of BIS monitoring.
16:33
The first dimension is the B-aware trial, published in the Lancet in 2004. This was a large, prospective, randomized, double-blind, multi-center trial. The trial group were high-awareness risk patients undergoing a variety of procedures, including cardiac, trauma and obstetric surgery. They were randomly assigned to receive standard general anesthetics or a general anesthetic with BIS monitoring. The patients were blinded to the protocol they received, as were the observers who assessed

them for awareness at two to six hours, 24 to 36 hours, and 30 days. The overall outcome from the study suggested that BIS guided anesthesia reduced the incidence of awareness with recall approximately 82%. They also incidentally reported that the use of BIS led to a reduction of the administration of volatile gases. The group, therefore concluded by recommending the use of BIS in high-risk patients. It was estimated that if we were to routinely use awareness monitoring for most patients in the United Kingdom, it would cost approximately £30 million annually.

One of the most recent trials published was the B-Unaware trial, which was published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2008. This trial was based in Washington, the United States. Once again all the patients included were considered to be at high risk of awareness under anesthesia. There were various inclusion criteria which were based on factors such as previous history of awareness, history of difficult intubation, ASA grades 4 or 5, Aortic stenosis or end-stage lung disease. The patients were due to have a general anesthetic with a volatile agent, with or without

The second group used entied agent monitoring targeting max of between 0.7 and 1.3. Alarms were set to alert when values were outside of these ranges. Awareness was assessed using questionnaires, a well-accepted method, at 24 hours, 72 hours, and 30 days. Patients and investigators were unaware of the groups to which they had been assigned. There were two definite cases of awareness in each group. The trial did not reproduce the

Can you tell me what entropy is? Entropy can be defined as being a quantitative measurement of disorder in a system. Entropy monitoring for depth of anesthesia relies on assessing the degree of irregularity in the EEG signals. The amount of irregularity decreases with increasing
19:55
brain levels of anesthetic drugs. The monitor itself is similar to a BIS monitor in that it consists of EEG electrodes placed on the patient's forehead. The monitor processes the data collected and produces two different numbers. These are response entropy, RE, and state entropy, SE. The difference between these relates to the frequency band pass used. The response entropy looks at higher frequency waves and this enables a faster response time from the monitor. Studies have confirmed that entropy scores do relate to depth of anesthesia.

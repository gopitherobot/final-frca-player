Describe the arterial blood supply to the spinal cord.
A
  • The arterial supply to the spinal cord consists of the anterior and posterior spinal arteries.
  • The single anterior spinal artery lies in the anterior median fissure of the cord.
  • It is formed from branches of each vertebral artery at the level of the furamine magnum.
  • It runs the whole length of the spinal cord.
  • The anterior spinal artery supplies the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord.
  • The posterior spinal arteries arise from the posterior inferior cerebellar artery at the
  • level of the furamine magnum.
  • They lie both anterior and posterior to the dorsal nerve roots.
  • There are important contributions to the spinal arteries from regular branches at each
  • spinal level.
  • The anatomy of this is variable.

  • anterior regular artery of a damk of it.

  • aspastic paralysis with decreased pain and temperature appreciation below the level of

  • There is relative sparing of the senses of proprioception and vibration, as the posterior
  • columns are usually preserved.

A

  • the cordal canal.

What are the features suggestive of a post-dural puncture headache?
A
  • The headache may be severe, usually over the frontal and occipital areas, sometimes radiating
  • to the neck and shoulders.
  • It is often worsened by an upright posture and neck movement and relieved by lying down.
  • It may be associated with neck stiffness, photophobia, tinnitus and visual disturbances.
  • It is seen more commonly after-dural puncture, recognized or otherwise, with needles of
  • larger calibre and with cutting tips.
  • Parturients are more likely to experience post-dural puncture headache.
  • It is less frequent in the obese.
  • There may not have been recognized breach of the juror's sack, but symptoms usually occur
  • within 48 hours and last about two weeks.
  • Post-dural puncture headache can lead to cranial nerve pauses, convulsions or even intra cranial
  • hemorrhage and death.
  • There should be a full assessment of the headache and a neurological assessment performed.
  • Other causes of headache should be considered.
  • MRI or CT scanning can demonstrate CSF leaks, but in practice is not often used.
Q7
How should a post-dural puncture headache be managed?
A
  • Patients with significant symptoms can initially be managed conservatively with bedrest,
  • analgesia, anti-emetic drugs and hydration.
  • There are a number of pharmacological agents that have been recommended to treat post-dural
  • puncture headache.
  • Caffeine, 500 mg orally or intravenously, once or twice a day, has found to be an effective
  • treatment.
  • It is thought that it may act by vasoconstricting dilated cerebral vessels.
  • The Tryptan and ACTH have also been used in the past, but there is no evidence to support
  • their use.
  • Epidural blood patch is regarded as the definitive treatment and has a success rate of between
  • 70 to 98% if carried out more than 24 hours after a general puncture.
  • If the symptoms persist after the epidural blood patch, the procedure may be repeated.

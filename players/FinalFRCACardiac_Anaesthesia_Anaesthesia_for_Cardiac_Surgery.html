A
  • Stenosis may be mild, moderate, severe, or critical and can be classified according to the
  • mean pressure gradient across the valve and valve area. It is the valve area that is the key
  • factor determining flow. A normal aortic valve has a cross-sectional area of 2.5 to 3.5 cm squared.
  • Mild stenosis has a mean gradient of less than 25 mm of mercury and a valve area of 1.3 to 2 cm squared.

  • A valve area of less than 0.7 cm squared is critical and any demand for an increased cardiac output
  • will result in symptoms of angina, syncopy, and sudden death.

Q9
Describe how you would induce anesthesia for this patient.
A
  • Here it pays to visualize what you do in practice and talk it through step-by-step.
  • An examiner will not expect you to have extensive first-hand experience of cardiac anesthesia,
  • but knowledge of the principles of managing patients with severe cardiac disease will be required.
  • All anaesthetic and resuscitation drugs, equipment and staff should be ready before the patient
  • arrives in the anaesthetic room. Ina tropes, vasopressors and vasodilators must be prepared in advance.
  • Prior to induction of anaesthesia, continuous ECG monitoring using a CM-5 configuration,
  • oxygen saturations probe and invasive arterial monitoring should be established.
  • It may be appropriate to monitor central venous pressure,
  • insert a pulmonary artery catheter or use non-invasive methods of cardiac output monitoring at this stage.
  • At least one large bore intravenous cannula should be inserted whilst oxygen is administered
  • and the patients should be pre-oxygenated. If sedatives are administered as pre-medication
  • on the ward, oxygen therapy should commence at this early stage.
  • The choice of anaesthetic agents is governed by the preference of the anesthetist and the
  • requirement for cardiovascular stability. Intravenous induction may be performed using a combination
  • of high-dose opioid such as 10-15 micrograms per kilo of fentanyl and a carefully
  • titrated intravenous induction agent such as propifol or thio pentone, while carefully monitoring
  • the arterial pressure. It is important to obtain the presser response to endotracheal intubation,
  • avoid myocardial depression and sudden decreases in systemic vascular resistance.
  • Any adverse hemodynamic changes should be treated promptly. A muscle relaxant is given after
  • loss of consciousness and mask ventilation with oxygen performed with anesthesia being maintained
  • with a volatile agent such as civa fluorine or an infusion of propifol until neuromuscular blockade
  • is adequate for intubation. Once the airway has been secured with a tracheal tube,
  • mandatory ventilation is continued whilst a central venous catheter is inserted to provide
  • venous pressure monitoring and access for inotropic agents. Routine cannulation of the internal
  • jugular vein with a large bore sheath to allow flotation of a pulmonary artery catheter
  • introducer is advocated in many centres. Prior to surgery, the patient should have a urinary
  • catheter and a nasopharyngeal temperature probe inserted. A temperature probe positioned to
  • measure core temperature should be used. Capnography should be used as per the association guidelines.
  • Some centres advocate cerebral perfusion and processed EEG monitoring during cardio-bulmonary bypass
  • as awareness during anesthesia is a recognised risk. Transysophagial echo is now used in most centres.
  • Maintenance of anesthesia can be provided using a volatile agent or a total intravenous technique
  • with propifol and remyventinil. Increments of neuromuscular blockade and longer acting analgesics
  • will also be required. Nitrous oxide is best avoided because of cardiovascular depression
  • and there is a risk of expanding air bubbles within the circulation.

Q19
What are the complications associated with cardiopulmonary bypass?
A
  • The complications of cardiopulmonary bypass relate to the circuit and to perfusion.
  • Circuit-related complications include obstruction of the cannulae, failure of the oxygenator,
  • inadequate anti-coagulation causing embolism and aortic dissection.
  • Air embolism is also a risk and hemorrhage can also occur during the surgery or postoperatively.
  • Complications associated with perfusion include hypothermia, fluid overload,
  • myocardial stunning and coagulopathy which occurs particularly with pump times greater than 2 hours.
  • Prolonged cardiopulmonary bypass can lead to release of cytokines and a systemic inflammatory
  • response resulting in organ dysfunction. This may clinically manifest as confusion,
  • renal dysfunction and failure, ischemic gut, thrombocytopenia and impaired gas exchange due to
  • ventilation perfusion mismatch. Hypothermia can occur and careful temperature management
  • and active rewarming are essential prior to coming off bypass.
  • Electrolite and acid-based disturbances may also occur and should also be corrected prior to
  • coming off bypass. Seribrovascular events occur in between 1 and 5% of patients ranging from a
  • transient postoperative cognitive dysfunction to a major disabling stroke.

A
  • Myocardial preservation or protection aims to preserve myocardial function and prevent cell death.
  • Cardioplegic solutions are used to achieve this. They are administered to the myocardium
  • to cause a diastolic electro-mechanical arrest. A cold solution can be used to reduce metabolic rate
  • and cardioplegia solution is stored at 4°C then injected into the coronary arteries or coronary
  • sinus. Cooling is also achieved with cardioplegia at 4°C poured into the pericardium and chambers
  • to achieve a uniformly cooled heart at between 10 and 12°C. Further doses of cardioplegia
  • are required every 20 minutes or when electrical activity returns. The core body temperature
  • is allowed to fall to a target value of between 28 and 32°C. Modifications of this technique

A
  • There are a number of different formulations. These are crystalloid or blood-based solutions
  • to which a number of ingredients are added. Ringer's lactate, dextrose and saline dextrose

Q22
What events occur when coming off bypass?
A
  • There must be clear communication between the surgeon, anesthetist and perfusionist.
  • The patient's temperature should be returned to 37°C, the potassium should be 4-5mm per litre
  • and the hematocrit should be greater than 24%. A sinus rate of 70-100 beats per minute is desirable
  • and if sinus rhythm does not return spontaneously, DC cardioversion or an internal defibrillator
  • or temporary pacing wires may be needed. The venous pump is gradually restricted,
  • so allowing venous return back into the right atrium. As cardiac activity returns,
  • blood begins to circulate into the pulmonary vasculature and artificial ventilation with
  • oxygen and an inhalational volatile agent is recommended. Once the ventricles are contracting well,
  • the circulating volume is returned to the heart. Protamine, 1mg per 100 units of heparin,
  • is administered by slow infusion to reverse the effects of heparin, but only when surgically
  • indicated and at this point the perfusionist must turn off the suction.
  • Protamine may precipitate systemic hypotension, pulmonary vasoconstriction and anaphylaxis.

What are the important features of post-operative care?
A
  • The patient should be looked after in a critical care environment,
  • with invasive monitoring and appropriately experienced nursing and medical care.
  • The patient will usually require a period of sedation and ventilation post-operatively.
  • The length of time will depend on which anesthetic drugs and analgesic drugs are used
  • and whether the patient is stable cardiovascular. Fast-track cardiac anesthesia,
  • meaning excavation within eight hours after cardiac surgery, has been established as routine
  • in many cardiac centres in the world. The patient should be normothermic,
  • hemodynamically stable and have any acid-base or electrolyte disturbances corrected prior to
  • excavation. Most patients will require volume expansion as they warm up, and are high
  • urine output as a result of the filtration of the pump prime can lead to hypocholemia.
  • Bleeding from the chest or the pericardial drains in the post-operative period may need
  • investigating, necessitating the administration of blood, platelets, fresh frozen plasma,
  • or more protamine. If bleeding continues, then surgical exploration may be required.
  • Once exhumated, analgesia can be maintained using patient-controlled opioid analgesia
  • and regular paracetamol. High thoracic epidural anesthesia provides good hemodinamidum
  • and anachystability throughout surgery, superior analgesia, facilitating respiratory movements,
  • and adequate muscle tone, all necessary criteria for safe early excavation.
  • Centres using this technique claim that immediate excavation at the end of surgery is possible.
  • Extubation is not contraindicated if the patient is dependent on moderate doses of ionotropes.

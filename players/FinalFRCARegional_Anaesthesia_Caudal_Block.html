Codal block, adult compared to children.
Q1
Tell me about Codal analgesia.
A
  • Codal analgesia is produced by injection of local anaesthetic
  • into the Codal canal to block lumbar and sacral nerve roots.
  • It's mainly used in pediatric anesthesia.
Q2
Tell me about the anatomy.
A
  • Draw a sagittal section diagram.
  • The Codal space is the lowest part of the epidural space
  • contained by the sacrum.
  • The sacrum consists of five fused sacral vertebrae
  • and articulates with the fifth lumbar vertebrae superially,
  • but coxics inferiorly and the ilia, laterally.
  • The dorsal roof has a median crest in the midline
  • from fusion of the sacral spina's processes.
  • Failure of fusion of S5 and occasion S4
  • leaves the defect called the sacral hiatus.
  • This is covered by the sacro-coxagule membrane.
  • On either side of the sacral hiatus are sacral corneua.
  • The sacral canal is accessed via the sacral hiatus.
Q3
What does the sacral canal contain?
A
  • The terminal part of the duressac, which
  • finishes at approximately S1 to 2 in adults
  • and S3 to 4 in infants.
  • The cordic quiny, which consists of five sacral and coxagule nerves,
  • the phyllum terminale, the venous plexus,
  • and epidural fat.
Q4
What operations can cordal analgesia be used for?
A
  • In adults, cordals are mainly used for operations
  • on the perineum.
  • In children, it is used more extensively
  • for operations below the umbilicus.
  • These include an urnia repair, hypospadius repair,
  • orchid pexy, and lower limb surgery.
Q5
Why is it used more extensively in children than in adults?
A
  • The fat within the sacral canal is loose in children,
  • allowing the spread of local anaesthetic
  • making the block much more predictable.
  • In adults, the connective tissue forms a fibrus,
  • closed mesh, which leads to an unpredictable spread
  • of local anaesthetic.
  • The volume of the sacral canal also varies greatly in adults.
  • The sacral corneua are more easily identifiable in children.

Q7
What dose of local anaesthetic do you use?
A
  • In adults, 20 to 30 mills of bupivocane,
  • 0.25 to 0.5%, depending on the size of the patient.
  • The volume of the sacral canal is normally 30 to 35 milliliters.
  • For children, the volume depends on the level of block required.
  • For a sacral block, use 0.5 mills per kilogram
  • of 0.25 percent bupivocane.
  • For a lumber block, use 1 mill per kilogram
  • of 0.25 percent bupivocane.
  • Or for the racco lumber,
  • 1.25 mills per kilogram of 0.19 percent bupivocane.

Q9
How long do cordal blocks generally last?
A
  • There's a lot of individual variety.
  • If you don't know, then guess.
  • Giving information about additives
  • looks like you've thought about how to prolong your block.
  • They normally last four to eight hours.
  • They can be extended by using a catheter technique
  • or by using additives.
  • These include clonadine, 1 microgram per kilogram,
  • opioids such as diamorphine,
  • at 30 micrograms per kilogram,
  • and preservative-free ketamine.
Q10
Any problems with these additives?
A
  • We must ensure that they are preservative-free
  • as preservatives may be neurotoxic.
  • Clonadine and opioids can cause sedation.
  • Opioids may cause respiratory depression
  • and urinary retention.
  • I don't use additives for day-case procedures.

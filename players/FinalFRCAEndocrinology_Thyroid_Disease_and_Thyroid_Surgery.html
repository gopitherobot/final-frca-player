thyroid disease and thyroid surgery. You were asked to assess a 65-year-old woman for sub-total thyroidectomy.
Q1
Describe your preoperative assessment.
A
  • To answer this question, you should use the same approach as for the preoperative assessment of
  • any patients, namely by taking a full history, examining the patients and obtaining appropriate
  • investigations. Your specific priorities in this case are to elicit evidence of the patient's
  • thyroid status and any evidence of airway compromise as a result of their thyroid disease.
  • I would begin by taking a history from the patient. In the history, I would try to elicit symptoms
  • of hypothyroidism such as lethargy and intolerance to cold, or hyperthyroidism such as agitation,
  • intolerance to heat and weight to loss. I would find out the reason for the surgery and any
  • medications prescribed for the thyroid problem. It is vitally important to establish any symptoms
  • of airway compromise resulting from the thyroid pathology. This includes asking about shortness
  • of breath, swallowing difficulties and noisy breathing.
  • Exasperation of symptoms of dyspnea or stride or on lying flat is an important indicator of a
  • potentially difficult airway. In addition to these specific points, I would take a full anaesthetic,
  • medical and surgical history. My examination would focus on a listing sign suggestive of hypothyroidism.
  • These may include bradacardia, coarse skin and hair and non-pitting edema in hypothyroid patients,
  • and tetacardia, atrial fibrillation, tremor and eye involvement in patients with hyperthyroidism.
  • I would perform a careful examination of the airway and neck to assess the size of any thyroid
  • swelling, detect obvious deviation of the trachea and establish any retrasternal extension of the
  • mass. Appropriate investigations in this patient include blood tests, full blood count,
  • urea and electrolytes, thyroid function tests and corrected calcium. A chest x-ray may show
  • deviation of the trachea, and this can be further investigated with a thoracic inlet x-ray or
  • ideally a CT scan. The latter allows the precise localization of tracheal narrowing.
  • A preoperative nasal endoscopy is useful for two reasons. It gives some indication of the view
  • of the larynx likely to be obtained during direct larynxoscopy and allows assessment of
  • vocal cord function prior to surgery. This lady has a large goita with evidence of retrasternal
  • extension. She is uncomfortable to breath on lying flat. She is clinically and biochemically
  • euthyroid. A CT scan of her thorax shows a marked deviation and a narrowing of the trachea
  • at the level of the upper sternum. Describe your options for technique of induction of anacesia
  • in this patient. There are no absolute right and wrong answers to a question like this,
  • but your answers should demonstrate that you are aware of the potential problems with the case
  • and have given thought to how to avoid or address them. This lady has clinical and radiological
  • evidence of airway compromise due to her thyroid swelling. I anticipate her airway may be difficult,
  • both in terms of a potentially difficult view at larynxoscopy, and also difficulty passing an
  • endotracheal tube past the narrowing. I would keep the patient in a semi-soupine position
  • to minimize airway obstruction and ensure pre-oxygenation for three minutes.
  • Potentially suitable techniques include an inhalational induction or awake fiber optic
  • intubation. An awake tracheostomy, whilst attractive in terms of controlling the airway,
  • can be potentially hazardous with excessive bleeding from the vascular thyroid tissue.
  • In view of the fact that insertion of the fiber optic scope into the narrowed airway
  • may produce complete obstruction, I would use an inhalational technique and intubate
  • under deep inhalational anacesia. Topical local anaesthetic applied to the airway helps to
  • reduce airway reflexes. A reinforced endotracheal tube is needed, ensuring that a range of
  • sizes are available to pass beyond the tracheal narrowing. In the event of a failure to secure the
  • airway after inhalational induction, ventilation may be possible via a rigid bronchoscope.
Q2
Aside from the airway, what are the other perioperative concerns with patients having thyroid surgery?
A
  • Careful eye protection should be used in patients with eye involvement, since it may not be
  • possible to close their eyes fully. There is the potential for significant blood loss and limited
  • access to cannulas in the patient's forearms due to the extended position of the arms under
  • the surgical drapes. A large bore cannula with an extension should be available to allow the
  • rapid administration of fluid in the event of hemorrhage.
Q3
How would you extubate this patient?
A
  • I would extubate the patient sitting up, fully awake,
  • having insured adequate reversal of neuromuscular blockade. You're called to the ward to see this
  • patient three hours after her surgery. She's acutely distressed with oxygen saturation of 78%.
Q4
How would you proceed?
A
  • I would approach this problem with an airway breathing and circulation
  • approach. Whilst making an initial rapid assessment of the situation, I would administer 100% oxygen
  • via a non-rebreathing mask. The potential problems I need to consider are bleeding, causing a
  • hematoma around the surgical site, laryngeal edema, and damage to the recurrent laryngeal nerves.
  • The most likely cause is a hematoma and an urgent transfer back to theatre should be organized.
  • The wound can be opened on the ward to relieve the pressure on the airway in severe cases.
  • A further rare cause of post-operative airway compromise in patients with a long-standing
  • goiter is tracheomalacia. These patients should be re-intubated quickly and are likely to require
  • further management with a tracheostomy. What's the consequences of damage to the recurrent laryngeal
Q5
nerves?
A
  • The recurrent laryngeal nerves can be affected by compression, ischemia, or direct injury
  • at the time of surgery. The injury can be unilateral or bilateral and partial or complete.
  • The recurrent laryngeal nerve supplies all of the intrinsic muscles of the larynx except
  • cryothyroid. Complete transection of the nerve results in the vocal cord on the affected side,
  • adopting a partially abducted or cadavaric position.
  • Partial nerve injury is potentially more worrying as the muscles controlling
  • abduction of the cords are affected to a greater extent than the adductors and the cord lies in
  • the midline. Bilateral partial nerve injury can therefore result in the vocal cords overlapping
  • in the midline, producing complete airway obstruction. Name another major post-operative complication
  • of thyroid surgery aside from airway complications. Inadvertent removal of parathyroid tissue at the time
  • of thyroidectomy can produce high muscle twitching, tingling around the mouth and tetanine
  • in extreme cases. Specific clinical signs can be elicited, such as trusose sign, whereby
  • carpapedal spasm is provoked by inflation of a cuff around the limb. Ochoestec sign, where tapping
  • over the course of the facial nerve produces twitching of the facial muscles.
  • Corrected calcium levels should be checked routinely 12 to 24 hours after surgery,
  • and earlier if there is clinical suspicion of hypocalcemia. Calcium supplementation can be
  • all in mild cases or intravenous if the corrected calcium is less than 2 millimoles per litre.
  • Appropriate intravenous calcium supplementation is 10 ml of 10% calcium gluconate given over
  • 3 to 5 minutes.

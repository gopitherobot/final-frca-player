You are presented with a 56 year old obese smoker for an elective large inguinal hernia repair. He also says to yourself as an obstructive sleep apnea, which was diagnosed two years ago. Q1
How common is sleep apnea?
A
  • About 3% of middle aged adults have clinically significant sleep apnea, with the male to female ratio of 2 to 1.
Q2
What are the causes of sleep apnea?
A
  • Try and classify your answer since the examiner has not yet said obstructive sleep apnea.
  • Sleep apnea can be caused by either central or obstructive factors.
  • Obstructive sleep apnea is characterized by persistent effort without airflow whilst in central apnea effort is absent.
  • Central sleep apnea is due to inadequate breathing because of reduced or absent respiratory effort due to disorders of ventilatory control or neuromuscular function.
  • Patients have reduced ventilatory capacity that is sufficient when awake, but insufficient when asleep, as the ventilatory drive decreases and compensatory mechanisms fail.
  • Causes of central sleep apnea include neuromuscular diseases such as polio or muscular dystrophy, central nervous system problems such as stroke, surgery and head injury,
  • and excessive respiratory load such as chyphoscaliosis.
  • Obstructive sleep apnea is due to pharyngeal collapse that occurs during sleep.
  • Vibration of the flaccid structures causes snoring which persists until sleep is interrupted and muscle tone restored.
  • Obstructive sleep apnea is associated with obesity, pharyngeal abnormalities and conditions such as hypothyroidism.
  • Sedative drugs and alcohol can precipitate the condition.
Q3
What are the symptoms of sleep apnea?
A
  • Try not to focus your answers solely on the respiratory symptoms.
  • The symptoms of sleep apnea are heavy snoring with occasional waking and excessive daytime somnolence.
  • Poor memory, mood changes and headaches may be described.
  • Gastroosophageal reflux disease, impotence and nocturnal epilepsy are also associated with sleep apnea.
  • The patient's partner may describe witnessing apneas and sudden awakenings with choking.
  • Children with sleep apnea may have behavioral problems and frequent colds.
Q4
What are the signs of sleep apnea?
A
  • The clinical signs of sleep apnea may be divided into airway, respiratory, cardiac and other systems.
  • The airway signs include maxillary hypoplasia, retronatia, nasal obstruction, increased nexocumference greater than 44 cm,
  • changes to the soft tissues of the palate and decreased oropharangial dimensions.
  • The respiratory signs include cyanosis, hypoxia, CO2 tremor, bony and muscular chest wall abnormalities.
  • The cardiac signs include hypertension, arrhythmias and right-sided heart failure, raised JVP, pulsatile liver and peripheral edema.
  • Other systems that might indicate diseases associated with sleep apnea are the neurological and endocrine systems with diseases such as stroke, hypothyroidism or acromegally possibly being present.
Q5
And what are the potential consequences of sleep apnea?
A
  • As usual, folks, try and classify your answer here.
  • The sequelia of sleep apnea can be divided into neurological, cardiovascular, pulmonary, hematological, endocrine and gastrointestinal.
  • Neurological consequences include reduced memory and cognition, headaches, anxiety and depression and intracranial hypertension.
  • Cardiovascular include hypertension, ischemic heart disease, cerebral vascular disease and right heart failure.
  • Respiratory problems include hypoxemia, hypercapnia and pulmonary hypertension.
  • Long-term sleep apnea leads to desensitization of respiratory centers with increased reliance on hypoxic drive and eventually type 2 respiratory failure.
  • The hematological changes include polycythemia.
  • The endocrine changes include reductions in growth hormone and testosterone levels and predisposition to diabetes.
  • With regards to the gastrointestinal tract, the patient is at risk of developing gastroesophageal reflux disease.
  • Finally, sleep apnea is associated with poor wound healing.
Q6
How is obstructive sleep apnea diagnosed?
A
  • The diagnosis of obstructive sleep apnea is made after taking a full history and examining the patient.
  • The gold standard of investigation is overnight polysomnography.
  • This involves videoing a patient sleeping and recording measurements such as the EEG to stage the sleep cycle, ECG, pulse oximetry, mouth and nasal airflow, chest and abdominal movement, snoring level and apneas.
  • Apnea capposodes are described as 10 seconds or more of total cessation of airflow despite continuation of respiratory effort against a closed glottis.
  • More than five of these in an hour or 30 during a night are considered clinically significant.
  • It's possible that you might be sharing a set of sleep studies.
  • Stick to the basics, describe what you see. There may be a number of different variables as described above, so try and link them together.
Q7
What are the treatment options for obstructive sleep apnea?
A
  • Treatment options are dependent on the severity of the condition and the preference of the patient.
  • Initial measures include weight loss, reduction of alcohol or sedative consumption and cessation of smoking.
  • CPAP is the treatment of choice for patients with moderate to severe sleep apnea.
  • It acts by splinting open the upper airway, reduces secondary complications and improves mortality rates.
  • More severe cases may require by-pap devices.
  • Some cases of obstructive sleep apnea may require upper airway imaging to guide whether surgical intervention will be of benefit.
  • Correction of nasal obstruction, tonselectomy and even tracheostomy has all been performed for sleep apnea patients.
Q8
What would be the general anesthetic considerations for this man?
A
  • Sleep apnea poses a number of challenges to the anesthetist.
  • Firstly, the altered respiratory drive, secondly, the potential difficult ventilation and intubation,
  • and thirdly, the multi-system effect of the sleep apnea.
Q9
How would you assess the patient?
A
  • I would take a full history from the patient and his partner, paying particular attention to the sleep apnea and any systemic effects that may have occurred.
  • I would also want to discuss the inguinal hernia and the indications for the operation.
  • My examination would focus on assessing the patient's airway, respiratory and cardiovascular systems.
  • I would also want to see a recent full blood count and use an ease to assess for polycythemia and possible effects of anti-hypertensive treatment on renal function.
  • I would require an ECG looking for evidence of right heart failure and ischemic heart disease.
  • An echocardiogram and sleep studies might be indicated depending upon the severity of the patient's condition.

Q11
How I induced anesthesia would depend on my findings at the preoperative visit.
A
  • If the airway appeared difficult, I would consent the patient for an awake fiber optic intubation.
  • Otherwise, I would consider performing a rapid sequence induction due to the risks of gastroesophageal reflux disease.
  • Patients with sleep apnea can become harder to mask ventilate once the muscle relaxant has been administered.
  • Have a higher incidence of difficult intubation and airway complications such as luringus basm.
  • I would therefore request senior anaesthetic assistance for this case.
  • Considerations during maintenance of anesthesia would include judicious use of opiates and any sedative medication since this might lead to post-operative respiratory failure.

A
  • The patient will require his CPAP in the post-operative period. A high dependency bed should be available.
  • The patient should be nursed sitting and not in the supine position, and care should be taken to monitors oxygen saturation, respiratory rate and conscious level.
  • Patients with sleep apnea are at higher risk of developing deep vein thrombosis, and so I would consider starting clexane early with dead stockings and pneumatic foot pumps.

Anathes easier of cardiac patients for non-cardiac surgery. A 75-year-old man presents on the general surgical list for an AP resection for invasive sigmoid tumour. He had an MI two years ago and has since had a coronary artery bypass graft. He gets angina on severe exertion and a recent coronary angiogram showed us the notice of one of the diagonal arteries, not amenable for stenting or surgical treatment. He is hypertensive for which he takes an ACE inhibitor. He is also in atrial fibrillation, which is rate control with the joxen and is taking warfarin.

Q5
How would you assess his functional capacity?
A
  • Functional capacity is based on exercise tolerance and can be assessed by history.
  • The patient's maximal oxygen consumption is estimated and expressed in metabolic equivalence
  • or METS, where 1 METS is equal to approximately 3 mils of oxygen per kilo per minute.
  • Poor functional capacity would be between 1 and 4 METS and the patient's activities
  • would be limited to lighthouse work, walking slowly on the flat and taking care of themselves
  • for example, showering or dressing.
  • Moderate functional capacity is between 4 and 7 METS and the kind of activities required
  • to produce this are climbing a flight of stairs without stopping, walking briskly on the
  • flat or light gardening.
  • More than 7 METS represents excellent functional capacity and this would include activities
  • such as digging in the garden, lifting heavy furniture or more strenuous sporting activities.
  • Patients with poor functional capacity and patients who are limited by other conditions
  • should have more detailed investigations prior to surgery.
Q6
Can you describe cardio pulmonary exercise testing?
A
  • The examiners want a basic description of the test and important findings.
  • Cardiopulmonary exercise testing is a non-invasive technique that allows maximal oxygen consumption
  • to be calculated.
  • The patients are asked to exercise on a treadmill or bicycle with continuous ECG monitoring
  • and breath-to-breath determination of oxygen uptake and carbon dioxide consumption.
  • This allows calculation of the anaerobic threshold which is the amount of oxygen being
  • consumed at the point where aerobic metabolism starts to be supplemented with anaerobic
  • metabolism.
  • An anaerobic threshold of 11 mls per kilo per minute or more tends to be associated with
  • a good outcome following surgery.

Q9
Are there any other optimisation strategies you're aware of?
A
  • This is testing your awareness of the literature and may provoke some debate as there are no
  • right answers.
  • The problem with the optimization strategies have been discussed in the literature targeting
  • tissue oxygen delivery.
  • Patients are admitted to the intensive care unit preoperatively, arterial and central
  • lines are inserted and are means of measuring cardiac output.
  • Fluid therapy and dopexamine are titrated to achieve a tissue oxygen delivery of 600
  • mil per minute per meter square of body surface area.
  • Unfortunately, the studies to date have not shown a survival benefit from this strategy
  • and it is costly in terms of resources.
  • There has also been some discussion in the literature about the use of beta blockers
  • and statins in the perioperative period.
  • The Pauise study looked at the use of metoprolol 2-4 hours before surgery, continuing for 30 days
  • afterwards, and they found that although the risk of cardiovascular complications was reduced,
  • the risk of death, stroke and significant hypotension were increased.
  • Until more studies are done looking at the types of beta blocker and duration of use,
  • their routine use in high risk patients cannot be recommended, there is a growing body of
  • evidence that suggests that patients who are taking statins prior to major surgery have
  • better outcomes.
  • There are also suggestions that stopping statins in patients who are taking them can
  • worsen outcome.
  • There is no data however on whether starting a statin in a patient in the perioperative period
  • will improve the outcome for that particular patient.
  • There are also potentially serious side effects such as rabdo-myelisis associated with statin
  • use, so their routine use in high risk patients is not without risk.
  • Until further evidence is available, patients on statins should continue them in the perioperative
  • period, but patients should not be started on statins day-novo.

How would you manage this man's anaesthetic?
A

  • agent.

  • intra-optively, again, to attenuate the stress response to surgery and to maintain hemodynamic
  • stability.
Q11
Are there any other considerations during surgery?
A
  • This one is for bonus marks.
  • Other factors I would want to pay attention to are temperature control and glucose control.
  • I would aim to maintain normal thermia during the procedure, as there is a higher instance
  • of cardiac morbidity in patients who become hypothermic.
  • I would keep the patient warm using warmed IV fluids, a warm air blanket, and by wrapping
  • the patient's head to minimize heat loss.
  • Tight glucose control is associated with a reduced morbidity and mortality, so I would
  • check a B.M. at the start of the case and every hour during the procedure.
  • If the blood sugar starts to rise, I would run an insulin infusion.
Q12
How would you manage the patient in the post-operative period?
A
  • Think about why, where, and how to manage the patient.
  • The 72 hours following surgery is when most perioperative infarcts occur, as the period
  • after surgery is associated with a hyper-coagulable state and increased circulating cataclylamine
  • levels.
  • This patient would be best managed in a high dependency area post-operatively.
  • He should have humidified oxygen for 48-72 hours, and his analgesia well managed.
  • He will need thrombo-prosylaxis and anemia should be avoided.
  • His normal cardiac medications should be restarted as soon as possible.

Q1
anatomy of central venous access. What are the indications for central venous access?
A
  • This is a straightforward question and having done the procedure several times,
  • you should be able to answer the question, then easily move on.
  • Short term central venous access is performed mainly to administer drugs which are irritant
  • if given via peripheral vein. For hemodialysis, when peripheral access is difficult,
  • for transvenous cardiac pacing and cardiac catheterisation, and for the measurement of central
  • venous pressures, long term access is mainly for total parental nutrition and chemotherapy.
Q2
What are the common sites used for central venous access?
A
  • Internal jugular, subclavian and femoral vein. Internal jugular is the preferred site
  • due to the low risk of complications. However, the subclavian vein is the site which
  • carries the least risk of infection. The femoral vein carries the highest risk of infection

Q4
along with the trachea and esophagus. What are the complications of central venous access?
A
  • They can be divided into immediate and delayed complications. Immediate complications include
  • bleeding from the site, carotid arterial puncture, which may be associated with hematoma,
  • pneumothorax, hemothorax, chylothorax, but only on the left side,
  • air embolism, foreign body embolism, e.g. guidewire or catheter fragment,
  • arrhythmias and complications associated with unrecognized catheter misplacement,
  • such as intriplural infusion. There have been reports of cardiac tampernard.
  • Long-term complications include infection, line-related sepsis and thrombus formation.
Q5
How can these complications be minimized?
A
  • The risk of pneumothorax and hemothorax are minimized by using a high approach.
  • An ultrasound guided technique may also reduce the risk of arterial puncture and catheter misplacement.
  • Performing a chest extra post-insertion to ensure appropriate catheter tip position may reduce
  • the risk of thrombosis and also the risk of cardiac tampernard. One should avoid threading the
  • guidewire into the right atrium and have continuous ECG monitoring available to detect arrhythmias,
  • with drawing the guidewire should arrhythmias occur.
  • Position the patient head down to reduce the risk of air embolism.
Q6
How can the risk of infection be minimized?
A
  • Some clavian vein cannulations are associated with a lower risk of infection.
  • A full aseptic technique with sterar gown, gloves, hat, mask and drapes should be used.
  • The chosen site should be prepared with a 2% aqueouschlorhexidine gluconate solution.
  • It has been shown to have lower bloodstream infection rates compared with site preparation
  • with 10% povodyneidine or 70% alcohol.
  • Teflon or polyurethane catheters have been associated with fewer infectious complications than
  • catheters made of polyvinyl chloride or polyethylene. Use a central venous catheter with the minimum
  • number of ports or lumens essential for the management of the patient.
  • Use an antimicrobial or antiseptic impregnated CVC in adults whose catheter is expected to remain
  • in place for greater than five days. The type of dressing used can be transparent or with
  • gauze, however the site, dressing and need for central access should be reviewed daily and each
  • patient treated on an individual basis. Cap all stop-cocks were not in use and replaced
  • administration sets including secondary sets and add-on devices no more frequently than at 72
  • hour intervals unless catheter related infection is suspected or documented. Replace tubing used
  • to administer blood, blood products or lipid emulsions within 24 hours of initiating the infusion.
  • There's no agreed time frame and routine for change of catheters and it's not recommended unless
  • ongoing infection at the site or systemic sepsis is suspected or proven. There should be local
  • protocols and care pathways implementing the above together with staff education and monitoring of infection rates.

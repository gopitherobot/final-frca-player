Q1
How is this ion regulated in the body?
A
  • This is a core topic that you should remember from the primary FRCA.
  • Potassium intake is approximately 50 to 150 millimoles per day.
  • Potassium is predominantly an intracellular ion present in concentrations 20 to 30 times
  • higher than in plasma.
  • The plasma potassium concentration is 3.5 to 4.5 millimoles per liter.
  • A cute regulation of plasma potassium levels is achieved by the actions of insulin which
  • promotes uptake of potassium into cells.
  • Chronic regulation of plasma potassium levels is achieved by the kidney.
  • Normally, all of the filtered potassium is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and then actively
  • secreted in the distal tubule and collecting duct.
  • Ourosterone acts on the sodium potassium ATPase pump in the distal convoluted tubule to increase
  • potassium excretion.
  • Its release is stimulated by hyperkalemia and via the renin angiotensin pathway.
Q2
What are the causes of hypochalemia?
A
  • Don't forget folks, try and structure your answer whenever possible.
  • Hypochalemia may be due to intercompartmental shifts, increased potassium loss or reduced
  • potassium intake.
  • Intercompartmental shifts may be due to alkylosis, insulin, beta-2 agonists and hypothermia.
  • Increased losses may be caused by diuretics, mineralocorticoids, renal tubular acidosis,
  • isleal conduit, diurea and vomiting, sweating and dialysis.
  • Reduced intake may be due to lack of potassium in IV fluids or diet.
Q3
What are the ECG changes of hypochalemia?
A
  • This question may be posed in the context of an abnormally ECG.
  • The ECG changes include T-wave flattening and inversion, U-waves, ST depression, a prolonged
  • PR interval and increased QT interval.
  • There may be progression to arrhythmias and possibly acistyle.
Q4
What are the effects of hypochalemia?
A
  • A reduction in myocardial contractility can lead to heart failure.
  • Generalized skeletal muscle weakness may be a feature along with Ilius and Polyurea.
Q5
How can we treat hypochalemia?
A
  • Treatment is aimed at replacement of potassium, which can be oral or intravenous depending
  • on the severity of the condition.
Q6
What are the anaesthetic implications of hypochalemia?
A
  • Electives with hypochalemia are at risk of developing arythmias.
  • Elective operations should be cancelled if the potassium level is below 3 millimoles
  • per litre.
  • Emergency procedures may warrant more rapid correction with ECG monitoring in a high
  • dependency environment.
  • The maximum rate of potassium administration via central-venous access is 40 millimoles
  • per hour.
  • Thermal levels would need to be closely monitored and I would aim to achieve a level of 4 to
  • 5 millimoles per litre.
  • This is particularly important for patients taking digoxin due to an increased risk of
  • toxicity if the level is below 4 millimoles per litre.
Q7
What's the causes of hyperchalemia?
A
  • Can't say enough folks, try and structure your answer.
  • They can be split into inter-compartmental shifts, reduced excretion and increased intake.
  • Causes of inter-compartmental shifts include acidosis, aryptomyelitisis, trauma, malignant
  • hypothermia, succcemethonium use in patients with burns and spinal injury.
  • Reduced excretion may be due to renal failure, adrenal cortical insufficiency and drugs such
  • as ACE inhibitors or potassium-sparing diuretics.
  • Increased intake might be due to excessive intravenous potassium or as a consequence of a
  • massive blood transfusion.
Q8
What are the ECG changes of hyperchalemia?
A
  • The ECG changes include peaked T-waves, wired QRS and prolonged PR intervals which may
  • progress to a loss of P-waves, ST depression, ventricular fibrillation and a cystily.
  • These changes are patensuated by hypocalcemia, hyponatremia and acidosis.
Q9
What are the effects of hyperchalemia?
A
  • A patient may present with muscle weakness or gastrointestinal symptoms of nausea, vomiting
  • and diarrhea.
  • Furthermore, they may present with palpitations or cardiovascular collapse.
Q10
How can we treat hyperchalemia?
A
  • A potassium level of greater than or equal to 6.5 millimoles per litre or ECG changes
  • consistent with hyperchalemia require urgent treatment to avoid deterioration and cardiac
  • arrest.
  • 10 mills of 10% calcium chloride given intravenously will provide some cardiac protection by acting
  • as a physiological antagonist to the potassium.
  • 15 units of insulin in 100 mills of 20% dextrose infused over 30 minutes or any other similar
  • raisemen will derive the potassium into the cells and therefore reduce the plasma potassium
  • level.
  • Additional emergency treatment might include beta-2 agonists such as Soutbutamol which will
  • also move potassium into the intracellular space and dialysis to remove potassium from the
  • plasma.
  • Less acute treatment of hyperchalemia may include calcium razonium either orally or rectally.
Q11
What are the anesthetic implications of hyperchalemia?
A
  • Hyperchalemia will predispose the patient to arrhythmias which may be fatal.
  • It is therefore essential to treat symptomatic hyperchalemia prior to induction of an anesthetic
  • as I described earlier.
  • Considerations during anesthesia would then involve avoidance of succcemethonium which would
  • transiently increase the potassium level by 0.5 to 1 millimoles per liter and may be fatal.
  • Acidosis and hypothermia would also worsen the hyperchalemia by encouraging the shift
  • of potassium from the intracellular space into the plasma.
  • Controlled ventilation would allow potential correction of pH via optimization of carbon
  • dioxide levels.
  • An arterial line would allow regular monitoring of electrolyte and acid-based status.
  • Neuromuscular blockade may also be prolonged and therefore should be monitored.

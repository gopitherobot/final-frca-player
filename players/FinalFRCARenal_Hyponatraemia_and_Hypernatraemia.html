Hypernatremia and hypernatremia. What are the causes of hypernatremia? Hypernatremia is defined as a plasma sodium level of greater than 145 millimoles per liter. This may be due to sodium excess, water depletion or sodium deficiency with proportionally greater water loss. Causes of sodium excess include hyper-aldrosterinism, cushing's disease, and excessive administration of hypertonic saline. Water depletion may be due to nephrogenic diabetes in sypedus, inadequate water intake and burns.

Water depletion greater than sodium loss might be caused by renal dioresis, secondary to osmotic diuretics, for example, manitol, glucose or urea. How does hyper-aldrestonearism, corms disease or cushing syndrome lead to hypernatremia? Excessive mineralocorticoid levels lead to activation of aldosterone receptors in the distal convoluted tubule within the kidney. This causes increased sodium retention with potassium loss. Cushing's disease is caused by excess glucocorticoid excretion.

Diabetes in sypedus is due to impaired water reabsorption from the kidney. It may be cranial or nephrogenic in origin. Cranial diabetes in sypedus is caused by reduced ADH secretion from the posterior pituitary,

Nephrogenic diabetes in sypedus may be due to infection of the kidney, hypokalemia or drugs such as lithium or domestic cyclin. Investigations to confirm diabetes in sypedus should reveal hypernatremia with high plasma osmolality and low urine osmolality. A water deprivation test should confirm the diagnosis. The patients may present with effects of dehydration, including thirst, nausea, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion and lethargy. Central nervous system effects may also include seizures, muscle spasms, hyperreflexia and possibly intracranial hemorrhage. The patient may be hyperthermic. Hyponatremia is defined as a plasma sodium level below 135 millimoles per litre. This may be due to water excess, sodium redistribution, water excess disproportionate to sodium excess, or water depletion with greater sodium deficiency. Water excess may be further split into causes of excess administration of hypotonic IV fluids, drinking too much water or TUR syndrome,

Unbalanced sodium and water deficiency is predominantly due to renal compromise due to diuretics, renal tubular acidosis, or hypodrenalism, but may be due to diarrhea and vomiting or pancreatitis. Finally, redistribution of sodium into the intracellular space may be found in patients with hypoglycemia. What are the effects of hyponatremia? Can you tell me more about hypotonic saline? Hypotonic saline is indicated when several symptoms develop or serum sodium levels fall below 120 millimoles per liter. Ideally, sodium levels should rise by 1 millimole per hour and not more than 20 millimoles over 48 hours.

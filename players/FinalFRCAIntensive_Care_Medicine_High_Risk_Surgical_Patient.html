The high-risk surgical patient. This topic would be most likely to appear as either a long or short clinical case, as it allows ample opportunity to discuss common clinical problems. It could follow on from some relevant basic science, for example, the stress response to surgery.
Q1
Have you any idea of mortality rates after surgery in the UK?
A
  • Data from NC Pod puts 30-day mortality somewhere between 0.7 and 1.7 percent for all operations.
  • Using a sample of over 4 million operations between 1999 and 2004 from ICNARC and CHKS,
  • overall 30-day mortality after elective surgery is approximately 0.5 percent and 5.5 percent after
  • emergency surgery. This data also showed that a small group of patients accounted for the majority
  • of deaths. About 1 in 8 patients were in this high-risk group that had a crude mortality rate
  • after all surgery of 12.5 percent. Interestingly, in absolute terms, this group accounted for over 80
  • percent of the total number of deaths, but fewer than 15 percent of them were admitted to an ICU.

  • and major plus procedures, laparotomies for example. These operations are generally longer

A
  • Goal-directed therapy is a term used widely in anesthesia and critical care that usually refers to
  • more or less protocolized care aimed at achieving a set of predominantly hemodynamic goals that have

Q11
What is the principle behind goal-directed therapy?
A
  • It was first noted over 40 years ago that patients who could not increase their cardiac output
  • postoperatively had a higher mortality, and in the 80s this observation was again made by Schumaker
  • in trauma patients. It was thought that cardiac ischemia and organ failure was caused by low
  • cardiac output and reduced oxygen delivery, so it was hypothesized that increasing cardiac output
  • and oxygen delivery to the levels attained by survivors would lead to reduced mortality.
  • In a landmark trial, he was able to show that achieving oxygen delivery goals with oxygen,
  • fluids and ionotropes mortality was reduced, but there are some methodological concerns.
  • The modern conception of goal-directed therapy also concentrates on oxygen supply demand
  • imbalance but less on cardiac ischemia. The stress response to surgery imposes the extra
  • oxygen demand for adequate mitochondrial generation of ATP required primarily for wound healing.
  • And the postoperative inflammatory response. This is met by increasing cardiac output and oxygen
  • extraction. Patients with an inability to respond to this increase in demand are those who are at
  • risk of complications as mitochondrial function is affected by prolonged inadequate oxygen delivery.
  • On this view, complications other than cardiac ischemia and macadial infarction are accounted for
  • as mitochondrial dysfunction leads to immunosuppression and is thought to underline multi-organ failure.
  • It also explains why heart failure is so significant,

Q15
gold-direct therapy?
A
  • Yes. As with any intervention, there are risks and benefits.
  • There are risks associated with the insertion of intravascular catheters,
  • with the fluids and drugs used, and with the use of cardiac output monitoring.
  • The main problem seen with the drugs is tachycardia, which increases mycardial oxygen demand,

  • output monitoring, particularly with the pulmonary artery catheter, that the measurement technique
  • and interpretation are done badly, so that the numbers produced are unreliable.

more patients had strokes or died. So when do you think beta blockers should be used?
A
  • Patients who are already taking beta blockers should remain on them, and those with an
  • independent indication should start, preferably well in advance of their operation.
  • Those with a high risk of cardiac complications, or undergoing high risk surgery, are more difficult.
  • At present, there is not enough evidence to make firm recommendations, but there may well be
  • groups of patients who will benefit from beta blockers. In the emergency setting, a cause for
  • tachycardia should be sought and corrected before even cautious use of beta blockers is considered.
Q19
Could you combine beta blockers and gold-direct therapy? Should you?
A
  • There is a practical problem in that patients taking beta blockers will be less susceptible to
  • the effects of Ina tropes, although there may still be beneficial beta two effects.
  • There is no other reason why they could not be combined, and arguably they should,
  • as high risk patients may have multiple pathologies and benefit from multiple therapies.
  • Beta blockers are used for different indications than gold-directed therapy.
  • Withdrawal of beta blockers is also associated with significant morbidity and mortality.
  • For patients stable on beta blockers, the cardiac adaptation of increased stroke volumes at
  • lower heart rates are ideal for the perioperative period, where an increase in cardiac output
  • by increased stroke volume is associated with less ischemia than by increased heart rate.
  • Overall, the risks and benefits are likely to be related to the hemodynamic effects of beta blockers
  • especially if used acutely. More fluid may be required and greater attention to detail,
  • but that is no bad thing. There could be a role for new Ina tropes like Livo's cemendum.
Q20
What about statins?
A
  • Statins have a number of effects, independent of their lipid lowering properties,
  • that are advantageous in the perioperative period. They are anti-inflammatory,
  • they reduce thrombosis, endothelial activation, platelet activation, and ischemia re-perfusion injury,
  • and enhance fibrolysis and vasodilitation in the micro-circulation.
  • Several cohort studies show increased cardiac morbidity and mortality risk if statins are
  • stopped perioperatively. A single small randomised control trial showed a reduction in death or
  • cardiac complications after vascular surgery in a group given a torver statin.
  • Altogether, there is sufficient evidence to test the utility of statins in high risk patients,
  • but not enough yet to recommend initiation in those not already taking them.

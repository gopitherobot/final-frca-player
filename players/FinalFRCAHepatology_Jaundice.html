Jondis, define Jondis. What are the causes of Jondis?
A

  • Jondis is a yellowish discoloration of the sclerone and skin due to subcutaneous deposition of
  • bilirubin. Normal bilirubin levels are exceeded and at greater than 60 micromole per liter,
  • the yellowish discoloration becomes apparent. Jondis can be pre-hypatic, hepatic, or post-hypatic.
  • The pre-hypatic causes could be because of impaired excretion of bilirubin, for example in

Q2
How is bilirubin metabolized in the body?
A
  • Bilirubin is a byproduct of the breakdown of hemoglobin in the tissues.
  • It is transported to the liver bound to albumin. It dissociates in the liver and is bound to
  • cytoplasmic proteins. It gets conjugated to form bilirubin di-glucuronoid.
  • This water soluble form of bilirubin is then transported via the bile canicule eventually to
  • the bile ducts and secreted into the intestine. Due to the action of colonic bacteria,
  • it is further metabolized to Euro-Biliner Gen and then to Sturco-Bilin.
  • Some of the Euro-Biliner Gen is reabsorbed and undergoes entrohapatic resurculation.
  • A small amount is absorbed into the general circulation and is excreted in the urine
  • giving it the characteristic straw colour. The Sturco-Bilin gives feces its brown colour.

Q4
How does liver failure affect pharmacokinetics?
A
  • Liver failure is associated with decrease in liver blood flow and this will decrease the first
  • past metabolism of drugs. This is further compounded by hypoalbumemia which increases the
  • amount of free drug present in the plasma. The active renein and jatensen system causes
  • retention of sodium and water. This increases the volume of distribution of drugs.
  • In early liver disease enzymes are induced and drugs metabolized more rapidly.

Q6
Describe the preoperative assessment of a patient with jaundice.
A
  • Preoperative assessment should always include history, examination and a review of the investigation.
  • A good history is paramount to ascertain the cause of jaundice. Younger patients are likely to have
  • viral, drug or alcohol induced hepatitis. Older patients have mechanical obstruction to the flow of
  • bile either due to gallstones or tumour. Rapid onset associated with nausea and anorexia is likely
  • to be due to viral hepatitis. Slow insidious onset with preritus is usually colostatic.
  • Associated symptoms of fever and rygores would suggest colangitis, whereas severe pain
  • could suggest acute pancreatitis either alcohol or gallstone related.
  • A history of hemolytic inherited blood disorders,
  • social history including history of alcohol consumption, sexual contacts, drug abuse,
  • history of recent travel and history of chemical or rodent exposure should also be elicited.
  • On examination look for signs of chronic liver disease. These are scratch marks, clubbing,
  • purer, bruising, palma erothema, leukonicia, spider-nevi, muscle wasting,
  • dupertrons contractor, loss of pubic and auxiliary hair, adema and testicular atrophy.
  • Signs of decompensation are fetal hepaticus, hepatic flap and confusion, which would suggest
  • hepatic encephalopathy. Also look for signs of portal hypertension like acytes,
  • spenomegaly and caput meduzai. The investigations that should be carried out include
  • a full blood count which may show anemia secondary to himolosis. A high leukocyte count
  • with predominantly polymorphs occurs in viral hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis and colangitis.
  • Coagulation is likely to be abnormal with a prolonged pro thrombin time and INR.
  • Pro thrombin time is a good indicator of liver synthetic function.
  • Eurea and electrolytes will often show low urea due to impaired production
  • and abnormal electrolytes due to diuretics. Glucose levels can be low due to impaired storage
  • and utilization of glucose. Arterial blood gases may show hypoxia secondary to interpulmonary
  • shunting. Billet rubin levels may be high. Alkaline phosphatase is raised to three times the
  • normal level. In the absence of pageants disease, this strongly suggests cholestasis.
  • Alonine amino transferase and the spartate amino transferase are non-specific and can be elevated
  • in hepaticellular injury. Gamma glutamyle transpeptidase is non-specific and is elevated in
  • colistasis and hepaticellular jaundice. Albune levels are decreased with a normal or raised
  • globuline level in patients with chronic liver disease. Hepatitis screen, which includes viral
  • markers, serum ferritin levels, serum seruloplasmine levels, autoimmune profile and alpha-feeta
  • proton levels should all be measured. Abdominal ultrasound may be used to distinguish between
  • obstructive and non-obstructive jaundice. Endoscopic retrograde, Kalangio pancreatography
  • is commonly used to investigate obstructive jaundice.

The Anatomy of the Cranial Nerves is rather dry and cumbersome to remember. The following podcasts aim to give a concise description.
Q1
Describe the Anatomy of the Optic Nerve.
A
  • The Optic Nerve is the second cranial nerve and mediates vision.
  • It is a nerve mainly consisting of afferent fibers which originate from the innermost layer
  • retina called the stratum opticum.
  • The fibers are axons of the cells in the retina which eventually receive myelin sheaths
  • and form the optic nerve.
  • The optic nerve runs through the optic canal, enters the cranial cavity and joins the optic
  • chiasma.
  • The optic chiasma is a flattened bundle of nerve fibers situated near the floor of the
  • third ventricle.
  • In the optic chiasma, the fibers from the nasal half of the retina cross the median plane
  • and enter the optic tract of the opposite side.

Q3
Describe the anatomy of the trideminal nerve.
A
  • The trideminal nerve is the fifth cranial nerve and provides sensory and motor innovation
  • to the face.
  • It has three sensory and one motor nucleus.
  • The first sensory nucleus lies lateral to the motor nucleus in the upper ponds and
  • receives fibers transmitting touch sensation.
  • The second nucleus receives proprioceptive fibers and lies in the midbrain.
  • Pain and temperature are perceived via the spinal tract of the trideminal nerve.
  • The motor nucleus is situated in the upper part of the ponds.
  • The trideminal ganglion, also called the semilunaganglion due to its shape, lies near the apex
  • of the petrous temporal bone.
  • The sensory fibers pass through the ganglion whereas the motor fibers pass below the ganglion.
  • The three main divisions of the nerve arising from the trideminal ganglion are the ophthalmic
  • division, V1, the maxillary division, V2, and the mandibular division, V3.
  • The ophthalmic division of the trideminal nerve divides into the lacrimal, nasosylary
  • and frontal branches just before the superior orbital fissure.
  • The frontal nerve further divides into the supraorbital and supratrochlear nerves.
  • The ophthalmic division also transmits some sympathetic and parasympathetic fibers.
  • In summary, the ophthalmic division supplies the skin of the nose, the forehead, eyelids, scalp, conjunctiver,
  • lacrimal apparatus, and the globe.
  • The maxillary division runs below the ophthalmic and leaves the base of the skull of either
  • ferramin rotundum.
  • It gives the superior dental nerve, zygomatic and sphenopalatine branches.
  • Eventually, it leaves the infraorbital ferramin as the infraorbital nerve.
  • The maxillary branch provides sensory supply to the skin over the temples, lower eyelids,
  • part of the cheek, and the upper lips.
  • The mandibular division has both sensory and motor routes.
  • The sensory division leaves the skull of either ferramin ovale and gives the lingula,
  • ariculotemporal, buckle, and mental nerves.
  • The motor route supplies the muscles of massacation, namely the masseter and the lateral
  • terrogides.
Q4
Describe the anatomy of the facial nerve.
A
  • The facial nerve is the seventh cranial nerve and has both sensory and motor routes.
  • The motor nucleus lies in the deep part of the ponds and is ventrometial to the spinal
  • tract of the trigeminal nerve and it is the origin of the motor route.
  • The motor route lies close to the abducine's nucleus and eventually emerges at the cordal
  • border of the ponds between the olive and cerebellipaduncles at the cerebellopontine angle.
  • The sensory nucleus is the upper part of the tractors solitarius of the medulla oblongata.
  • It receives fibers from the sensory route.
  • The routes of the facial nerve pass literally and forward towards the internal auditory
  • meetus.
  • In the internal auditory meetus, the motor route lies in a groove on the upper anterior
  • surface of the vestibulococlear nerve.
  • The sensory route lies between them.
  • The facial nerve then enters the facial canal and descends to the stylo-mastoid foramen.
  • The geniculate ganglion is situated at the point where the nerve bends sharply before it
  • emerges from the stylo-mastoid foramen and runs forward into the parotid gland.
  • At the neck of the mandible, it divides into its five branches.
  • The greater patrosal nerve, consisting of taste fibers, arises from the genicula ganglion
  • and also contains pre-ganglionic parasympathetic fibers which are travelling to the terrogopalatine
  • ganglion.
  • The branches of the facial nerve in the facial canal are the nerve to the stupedius and the
  • cordotimpony.
  • As the nerve leaves the stylo-mastoid foramen, it gives the posterior aricular, digastric and
  • stylohyoid branches.
  • On the face it gives the temporal, zygomatic, buckle, marginal mandibular and cervical branches.
Q5
Describe the anatomy of the vagus nerve.
A
  • The vagus nerve has the most extensive course of distribution compared to any other cranial
  • nerve.
  • It consists of motor and sensory fibers.
  • They are connected to four nuclei in the medulla oblongata.
  • These four nuclei are the dorsal nucleus of the vagus, nucleus ambiguous, nucleus of the
  • tractus solitarius, and the spinal nucleus of the trigeminal nerve.
  • The roots derived from these ganglion unite to form the large cord of the vagus nerve and
  • it exits the skull via the jugular foramen.
  • Before it exits the foramen, it forms the superior ganglion and on its exit forms another
  • swelling called the inferior ganglion.
  • The vagus nerve also gets some twigs from the accessory nerve and its fibers are distributed
  • via the farongial and recurrent larongial branches of the vagus nerve.
  • The vagus nerve passes vertically down the neck, invested in the corroded sheath and
  • lying between the internal jugular vein and internal corroded artery.
  • At the upper border of the thyroid cartilage it lies between the internal jugular vein
  • and the common corroded artery.
  • On the right side the vagus nerve lies posterior to the internal jugular vein and crosses
  • the first part of the subclavian artery.
  • It enters the thorax and lies to the right of the trachea and posterior medial to the
  • brachiocafalic vein and the superior venocaver.
  • The right pluror and right lung are lateral to the nerve.
  • It then passes behind the right main broncus to reach the posterior aspect of the root
  • of the right lung.
  • Eventually it forms the right posterior pulmonary plexus and the posterior esophageal plexus.
  • Continuing further down the posterior esophageal plexus receives branches from the left vagus
  • nerve and descends into the abdomen via the diaphragm and is now called the posterior vagal
  • trunk.
  • It then divides into the small gastric and a large celiac branch.
  • The gastric branch supplies most of the stomach and the celiac branch gives off branches
  • to the various abdominal plexi.
  • On the left side the vagus nerve enters the thorax between the left common corroded artery
  • and left subclavian arteries.
  • It descends into the thorax and crosses the left side of the archiveaorta.
  • At this point it lies posterior to the left frenic nerve.
  • It then passes behind the root of the left lung and forms the left posterior pulmonary
  • plexus.
  • From this plexus two branches descend to the anterior of the esophagus and with a twig
  • from the right posterior pulmonary plexus form the anterior esophageal plexus.
  • Some branches continue further down and enter the abdomen as the anterior vagal trunk,

  • It passes upwards through the pharynmin magnum.

  • The hypoglosal nerve supplies the motor supply to all of the tongue muscles except the

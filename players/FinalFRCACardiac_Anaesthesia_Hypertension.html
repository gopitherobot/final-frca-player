hypertension. You've been asked to see a patient in the pre-operative assessment clinic prior to surgery on a colonic tumour. The nurse reports their blood pressure is elevated at 170 over 90. This is likely to be a question on the risks of hypertension and appropriate therapy.

What else would you like to know?
A

  • including any previous medical history and medications. I would focus my history to look for any symptoms
  • of cardiac disease or if the patient had known hypertension. I would then perform a full examination
  • and request appropriate tests. I'm looking for potential causes of hypertension and also
Q2
any evidence of end organ damage. What causes of hypertension are you looking for?
A
  • One of the commonest causes in a hospital clinic is white coat hypertension, which should be excluded.
  • Another common cause is essential hypertension. Prior to diagnosing essential hypertension,
  • it is important to exclude secondary causes of hypertension. Tell me what you mean by white coat hypertension.
  • White coat hypertension, which can be induced by both doctors and nurses, is defined as a
  • persistently elevated clinical arterial pressure in combination with a normal ambulatory arterial
  • pressure. It is usually a benign condition. Before a diagnosis of hypertension is made,
  • patients should have several blood pressure readings at different times of the day. Obviously,
  • this is not always possible in the hospital clinic, but some efforts towards this,
  • such as repeated readings followed by some rest, are possible.

A

  • endocrine or renal causes. Common endocrine causes include cushing syndrome, cons syndrome,
  • hypothyroidism and fiochromocytoma. renal causes include renal artery stenosis, glomerulone
  • fritus and polycystic kidneys. If any of these diagnoses are suspected, refer

A

  • itself. The principal effect to concern ennecetists should be developing heart failure,
  • ischemic heart disease and renal failure. These have been associated with an increased

A
  • Ischemic heart disease can be identified based on a history of angina that has
  • hopefully been investigated using exercise tests and possibly and geography. It may also be
  • evident on a preoperative ECG. Heart failure can again be identified from the history,

Q6
So are you willing to ennecetize this patient? What is your threshold of blood pressure that you would accept?
A
  • This is a controversial area and each decision should be based on a variety of factors relating to both
  • the patient, their condition and the urgency of the operation. There is little doubt that
  • this patient needs blood pressure control, but as this surgery is for cancer, I would proceed with the case.
  • The threshold I would accept for urgent surgery is a systolic blood pressure of less than
  • 180 millimeters of mercury or a diastolic blood pressure of less than 110.
Q7
What if you base those figures on?
A
  • The American Heart Association and American College of Cardiology have produced extensive
  • recommendations on the perioperative management of hypertension. They have suggested that a blood
  • pressure of less than 180 over 110 is not associated with an increased perioperative risk.
  • This finding is supported by a meta-analysis and review article published in the British
  • Journal of Anesthesia that suggests that whilst there may be a small increased risk with
  • hypertension of less than 180 over 110, it is not clinically significant.
  • Obviously, these numbers should not be used in isolation, but should form part of your
  • comprehensive preoperative assessment.

If this patient was undergoing non-urgent surgery, what would you do?
A
  • In that situation, the patient has a blood pressure of greater than 140 over 90,

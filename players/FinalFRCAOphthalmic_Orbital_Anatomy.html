Orbital anatomy. Describe the anatomy of the orbit. This is a complicated subject, but don't get put off by the details. A description relevant to the blocks we do should see you through comfortably. The orbit is a bony cavity containing the eyeball, nerves, muscles and blood vessels which are necessary for the functioning of the eye.
0:27
The orbit can be thought of as a pyramid, the base of which is situated on the anterior part of the face. The roof is formed by the frontal bone and to the greater wing of the sphenoid. The floor is formed by the maxilla and the zygoma. The medial wall is formed by the maxilla, lacrimal bone, sphenoid and the ethmoid bone. The lateral wall is formed by the continuation of the greater wing of the sphenoid and the zygoma. The superior, inferior, medial and lateral recti muscles form a cone.
1:07
The apex of which is a fibrotendinous ring surrounding the optic canal and the medial part of the superior orbital fissure. Anteriorly, the muscles are inserted onto the scleror. The extra-conal muscles are superior oblique, inferior oblique, levator palpabri superioris and orbicularis ocule. The nerves and blood vessels reach the globe via the superior orbital fissure, inferior orbital fissure and the optic canal. The superior orbital fissure transmits the superior ophthalmic vein, ocular motor, abducins, troclier, lacrimal, frontal and nasosylary nerves. The inferior orbital fissure transmits the inferior ophthalmic vein and in-for-orbital artery and nerve.

The optic canal contains the ophthalmic artery and optic nerve. Describe the nerve supply to the orbit. The ocular motor nerve supplies all of the extra-ocular muscles except the superior oblique and lateral rectus, which are supplied by the troclier and abducins nerve respectively. The orbicularis ocule is innovated by the facial nerve and the levator palpabri superioris has a sympathetic innovation. The sensory nerve supply to the periorbital skin is via the superorbital super-troclier, infraorbital and lacrimal nerves, which are branches of the trigeminal nerve. The external layer, formed by the scleror and cornea, followed by the middle layer, divided into an anterior part consisting of the iris and ciliary body, and posterior part, namely the coroid layer. The iris is a coloured disc situated between the cornea and the crystalline lens, and it divides the anterior part of the eye into the anterior and posterior chambers. In the centre of the normal iris is the pupil, which behaves like a camera shutter and helps focus the beam of light onto the retina. The aqueous humour fills the anterior and posterior chambers, and is responsible for maintaining normal intracular pressure.

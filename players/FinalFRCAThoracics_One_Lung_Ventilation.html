Q1
What are the indications for one lung anesthesia?
A
  • You must have a structure to any question of this nature.
  • These can be divided into absolute and relative indications.
  • The absolute indications can be further classified into protection of the healthy lung from
  • pathology in the contralateral lung, including massive hemorrhage, infection or other fluid
  • such as lung lavage, or preventing ventilation to one lung, for example with a large bronchopluor
  • or fistula, or if there is a risk of rupture such as with a large bullet.
  • As well as these clinical indications, some surgical procedures such as video assisted
  • thoracoscopy are essentially impossible without one lung ventilation.
  • The relative indications are to improve surgical access, as opposed to lung retraction by the
  • surgeon.
  • These are indicated by the surgical approach and include nomenectomy, low-bectomy, thoracic
  • aneurysm repair, issoffagectomy and spinal surgery.

Q3
Can you think of advantages and disadvantages to the use of bronchial blockers?
A
  • Hopefully this type of question means the virus going well, and they are exploring your
  • knowledge a little further.
  • Bronchial blockers are helpful when there is abnormal airway anatomy, and it becomes difficult
  • or impossible to pass a double lumen tube.
  • They can be used for lower isolation.
  • In patients already intubated with a single lumen tube, the use of a bronchial blocker
  • avoids the need to change the tube at the beginning or the end of the case.
  • The disadvantages are that they give less reliable isolation, are more likely to become dislodged
  • intraoperatively, and they don't give easy access to the deflated lung, for example,
  • for suctioning.
Q4
Would you have a preference between a left or a right-sided double lumen tube?
A
  • This is hopefully straightforward.
  • Unless a right-sided tube was essential, for example, surgery on the left main bronchus,
  • I would always prefer to use a left-sided tube.
  • This is because the adult distance from the carina to the left upper lobe bronchus is
  • about 5cm, versus 2.5cm on the right.
  • So a left-side tube is much less likely to cause upper lobe obstruction.

Q8
Do you know what depth to insert a double-lumen tube?
A
  • For adults 1.7 to 1.8 meters tall, the depth is usually 29cm to the incisors.
  • The depth is meant to increase or decrease by 1cm for every 10cm in height difference.

Q13
What are the physiological consequences of one lung ventilation?
A
  • This usually takes place in the lateral de-cubitus position.
  • The lower dependent lung is ventilated.
  • Gravity will aid perfusion, but may also lead to compression and pooling of secretions
  • causing hypoxemia.
  • The upper non-dependent lung, which is either the surgical site or facilitating surgical
  • access, is not ventilated.
  • Any blood flow to this lung or to areas of alveolar collapse in the ventilated lung leads
  • to a shunt.
  • The shunt fraction is estimated to be between 20-40%.
  • Surgical retraction should help reduce any shunt by direct compression of vessels.
  • Hypoxic vasoconstriction should also reduce shunt, but only by 50%.

Q15
How would you adjust ventilation when the non-dependent lung is deflated?
A
  • I would maintain an inspired oxygen fraction of at least 0.5, increasing this if necessary.
  • Though the effects of the shunt mean that increasing this is unlikely to have a major impact,
  • I would keep the tidal volumes the same as for too long ventilation, up to a maximum
  • of about 10 mils per kilo, while trying to maintain peak airway pressures below 30 centimetres
  • of water.
  • And I would adjust the rate to try to maintain normal catnia.
Q16
How would you deal with hypoxemia?
A
  • Depending on the severity, there are a variety of strategies which can be grouped into those
  • addressing the dependent or non-dependent lung.
  • Increasing the dependent lung can be increased while avoiding excessive pressures and volumes.
  • Increasing the inspired oxygen concentration may be of use.
  • Succiting may help remove poor secretions.
  • If there is time, it may be possible to recheck the tube position with a broncuscope.
  • In particular, to check the upper-low broncus is not occluded.
  • Cautious application of positive end-expiratory pressure may improve oxygenation if a
  • telectasis is the main problem, though it may cause a worsening of any shunt by increasing
  • blood flow through the non-ventilated lung.
  • If ventilation appears adequate, there might be a problem with perfusion that is treatable
  • with fluids and or vasopressors.
  • With the non-dependent lung, the strategy is to try to decrease the shunt.
  • This could be via insufflation of oxygen, for example through a suction catheter.
  • In the discussion with the surgeon, CPAP could be applied.
  • If the operation is a new monectomy, the surgeon can clamp the pulmonary artery.
  • Failing these measures, it may become necessary to ventilate the non-dependent lung.

Q1
Can you classify anti-coagulants?
A
  • Remember, as with everything, you should classify the drugs if possible, as you will be less
  • likely to forget any.
  • Anti-coagulant drugs can be classified into those that inhibit platelet function, those
  • that affect the clotting cascade, and those that lead to the breakdown of a clot once
  • it is formed.
  • Let's start by talking about anti-platelet agents.
Q2
Can you tell me about all the drugs you know of that inhibit platelet function?
A
  • There are several drugs that affect platelet function, all of which work in slightly different
  • ways.
  • It's helpful to have an understanding of platelet function to understand how these drugs
  • work.
  • Platelets are activated when they are exposed to the subendothelial matrix when a vessel
  • wall is damaged.
  • Activation leads to degranulation of the platelets, and then a process of aggregation where platelets
  • stick to each other and clot formation.
  • Von Willibrand factor, a component of factor 8, is essential for the initial phase of platelets
  • sticking to the vessel wall.
  • The degranulation process leads to release of thromboxane A2, which increases platelet
  • aggregation, and also causes localized vasoconstriction of the vessel.
  • More distant vessel walls release prostocycline, which has the opposite effect to thromboxane
  • A2, preventing platelet aggregation and leading to vasodilitation.
  • This aims to localize the clotting process.
  • Thrombin, produced as part of the clotting cascade, increases the activity of platelets
  • by increasing the production of glycoprotein 2B and 3A receptors.
  • These receptors are required for binding of fibrinogen to platelets, leading to cross-linking
  • of platelets, and strengthening the clot formed.
  • Adenosine diphosphate, ADP, is also released from the platelets in the degranulation process.
  • This has a positive feedback effect on the platelets by binding to an ADP receptor on
  • the platelet surface.
  • This increases platelet activity by enabling the glycoprotein 2B3A receptor to transform
  • into its active form and therefore facilitate binding of fibrinogen to the platelets.
  • Drugs that affect the platelet function include non-steeroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, such
  • as aspirin.
  • These work by inhibiting cyclooxygenase enzymes, therefore reducing production of thromboxane
  • A2 from a racodonic acid, thus inhibiting platelet aggregation and preventing localized vasoconstriction
  • of the vessel.
  • Clopidogrel, this works by irreversibly preventing ADP from binding to its receptor and thus
  • reducing platelet activity and inhibiting the glycoprotein 2B3A receptor.
  • Diperidomol, this inhibits platelet phosphodiesterase, reducing platelet adhesion to vessel walls.
  • Prostocyclin, as previously mentioned, this is a Prosteglandin produced endovascularly,

Q4
Can you describe with the help of a diagram if you wish exactly how anti-inflammatory drugs work to inhibit platelet function?
A
  • Always worth drawing a quick diagram if you can, but make sure you make it a big, clear
  • diagram.
  • Membrane phospholipids are broken down to a racodonic acid by phospholipase A. This is
  • then converted to cyclic endoperoxidases by cyclooxygenase enzymes.
  • These are then converted to thromboxane A2 in platelets, prostocycline PGI2 in the vascular
  • endothelium and various prostocyclins PGE2, PGF2 alpha, PGD2 at other sites.
  • There is an alternative pathway where a racodonic acid is converted to lucotrains by lipooxygenase
  • enzymes.
  • This is not affected by non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs.
  • It is thought that a relative excess of lucotrains, due to an inhibition of cyclooxygenase,
  • may be the cause for the wheeze precipitated by non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs.
Q5
What is the difference between aspirin and other NSAIDs with regards to duration of action?
A
  • Aspirin leads to an irreversible inhibition of the cyclooxygenase enzyme whilst other
  • non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs lead to reversible inhibition.
  • This therefore means that the effect of aspirin lasts for the lifetime of the platelet, whilst
  • the other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs only have an effect whilst the drug is
  • in the system in its active form.

Q10
Finally, can you tell me about the Fibreinolithic agents?
A
  • The Fibreinolithic agents are Plasminogen activators.
  • During the process of clot breakdown, inactive Plasminogen has to be converted to the active
  • Plasmin, which can then break Fibrein down to Fibrein degradation products, thus dissolving
  • the clot.
  • Fibreinolithic agents initially form a Fibreinolithic Plasminogen activator complex.
  • This then goes on to convert further Plasminogen molecules to active Plasmin.
  • There are many examples of Fibreinolithic including streptokynaes and outer plays.
  • They are generally given to dissolve clot in a life-threatening situation, such as a
  • myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolus.
  • They carry with them the risk of serious bleeding, and so there are situations when the
  • risk of administration outweighs any potential benefit of treatment.

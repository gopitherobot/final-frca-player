Q1
Errithmiers. Can you define and classify Errithmiers?
A
  • Errithmiers is defined as an abnormal heart rhythm. They can be broadly classified as
  • tacky Errithmiers or bratty Errithmiers depending on the rate. An alternative classification
  • is based on the pathophysiology of the Errithmier and is discussed further in the next few minutes.
Q2
Why do Errithmiers occur?
A
  • Errithmiers can occur due to disorders of impulse formation or auto-maticity, and this explains
  • sinus node arrest, premature beats, atrial fibrillation and sick sinus syndrome.
  • Abnamalities of impulse conduction lead to heart blocks and establishment of reentry circuits.
  • Reentry is the underlying mechanism for paroxysmal superventricular tachycardia and atrial flutter.
  • Triggered activity occurs when abnormal electrical activity persists after repolarisation
  • and reaches a threshold level required to trigger a new depolarisation. This can occur in ventricular
  • tachycardia in the prolonged QT syndrome. Apart from the above mechanisms, there are several predisposing
  • factors that lead to Errithmiers. They are genetic abnormalities, myocardial ischemia, acquired
  • structural heart disease, hypoxia, hypercarbia, electrolyte abnormalities, hormonal imbalance
  • like phyrotoxicosis and drugs which alter conduction, auto-maticity and refractory period.
Q3
Can you classify bradycardia and explain their management?
A
  • Sinus bradycardia is defined as a heart rate less than 60 beats per minute with normal PQRS
  • and T waves on ECG. This is normal in fit and young people in athletes and during sleep.
  • Pathological causes are hypothyroidism raised intracranial pressure and intense
  • vagal stimulation such as stretching the peritoneum. If he modynamically stable, these patients
  • do not require treatment. However, if the rate is less than 40 beats per minute, they should be treated
  • with anti-muscarinic drugs or beta agonists. Heart blocks occur due to atroventricular
  • conduction abnormalities. They can be classified as first degree heart block,
  • diagnosed by a prolonged PR interval of greater than 200 milliseconds. They usually require
  • no treatment. Second degree heart blocks are classified as MOBITS type 1 and MOBITS type 2.
  • In MOBITS type 1 block, the PR interval increases with each successive beat
  • until a QRS complex is dropped. When it occurs in young and fit patients with a high vagal tone,
  • it is usually benign. In MOBITS type 2 block, there is an intermittent failure of conduction
  • with a 2 to 1 or 3 to 1 pattern. These patients are at a risk of sudden death and need pacing
  • preoperatively. In third degree heart block, there is complete interruption of atroventricular
  • conduction. The ventricular rate bears no relation to the atrial rate. The P waves are not followed
  • by QRS complexes. In the initial management of patients with bradicaridia, give 100% oxygen,
  • record a 12 lead ECG and look for adverse signs which are a systolic blood pressure less than 90
  • millimeters of mercury, a heart rate less than 40 beats per minute, signs of heart failure,
  • and ventricular arrhythmias. If any of these are present, give a dose of 500 micrograms of
  • atropine intravenously. If there is an unsatisfactory response or there are risk factors of A
  • systole, for example a MOBITS type 2 block, ventricular pauses of greater than 3 seconds,
  • recent ASIS-tally or complete heart block, then further doses of 500 micrograms of atropine
  • can be given up to a maximum of 3 milligrams. In addition, adrenaline 2 to 10 micrograms per minute
  • can be administered and transcutaneous pacing instituted. These are only holding measures and
  • expert help should be sought and transvenous pacing arranged as soon as possible.
  • Other drugs that can be tried in the interim are isoprenoline, aminophiline,
  • glycopyralate instead of atropine and glucagon if beta blocker or calcium overdose are suspected.
Q4
What are the indications for preoperative pacing?
A
  • Symptomatic bradiarythmia,
  • trifocicular block or MOBITS type 2 or complete heart block.
Q5
Can you classify tachyarythmias and explain their management?
A
  • They can be classified broadly as superventricular and ventricular in origin.
  • Sinous tachycardia is defined as a heart rate of greater than 100 beats per minute
  • and it occurs with fever, emotions, exercise, pain, anemia, hyperthyroidism, shock and alcohol abuse.
  • Superventricular tachycardias are narrow complex with a QRS duration of less than 120 milliseconds.
  • They include atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, atrial ventricular functional tachycardia,
  • multifocal atrial tachycardia and paroxysmal reentrant tachycardia.
  • Initial management includes the administration of 100% oxygen, venous cannulation,
  • continuous heart rate measurement and blood pressure measurement, and the recording of a 12 lead
  • ECG and rhythm strip. Adverse signs include reduced conscious level, a systolic blood pressure
  • less than 90mm of mercury, chest pain and heart failure. In the event of these adverse signs,
  • patients will need to be electrically cardiaverted using a synchronized by-phasic DC shock of 150
  • jewels. Remember, cardiaversion will require sedation or general anesthesia, depending on the
  • fasting status of the patient. If the patient is diagnosed as having new AF, i.e. of onset
  • of less than 48 hours, then 300 mg of amyodeurone as a loading dose can be given,
  • followed by maintenance dose of 900 mg over 24 hours.
  • Narrow complex tachycardia, with a regular rhythm, should be treated with vagal maneuvers in the first
  • instance. Failure to return to sinus rhythm should be treated with incremental doses of intravenous
  • adenosine, starting with 6 mg up to a 12 mg bolus. If the patient reverts to sinus rhythm,
  • the probable cause is a paroxysmol-superventricular tachycardia, and a 12 lead ECG should be recorded,
  • and appropriate anti-arhythmic prophylaxis instituted. Failure to return to sinus rhythm may
  • indicate atrial flutter. Give beta blockers, continue to provide supportive treatment,
  • and seek further expert advice. In all of the above situations, look for reversible causes
  • and treat these appropriately. Ventricular tachycardias are broad complex tachycardias.
  • If adverse signs are present, treat as per narrow complex tachycardias. If the patient is stable
  • and the heart rate is regular or the rhythm is uncertain, give 300 mg of amyodeurone,
  • followed by 900 mg over 24 hours. If the patient is known to have a superventricular tachycardia
  • with a bundle branch block, give adenosine as per the narrow complex tachycardia protocol.
  • Broad complex tachycardia with irregular rhythm is more complicated and expert advice is required.
  • The possibilities include atrial fibrillation with a bundle branch block, which should be treated
  • as a narrow complex tachycardia. Other causes are pre-excitation atrial fibrillation
  • and polymorphic ventricular tachycardia or torsade de poire. Polymorphic ventricular tachycardia
  • should be treated with magnesium 2g over 10 minutes.

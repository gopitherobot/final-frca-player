Q1
What is a Carotid Endar Terectomy?
A
  • Carotid Endar Terectomy is a vascular surgical procedure for treating Carotid Sternosis.
  • It involves removing plaque from the lining of the internal Carotid Atory.
  • Disease Intima and a portion of the media may be removed.
  • It is aimed at reducing the stroke rate in high-risk patients.
Q2
What are the indications for surgery?
A
  • Carotid Endar Terectomy is a prophylactic operation, so careful evaluation of the risks and benefits
  • is required. There are two large randomized controlled trials,
  • providing evidence to guide which patients should undergo surgery for Carotid Atory Sternosis.
  • They are the North American Symptomatic Carotid Endar Terectomy Trial, Nasset and the European
  • Carotid Surgery Trial, ECST. The Nasset Trial demonstrated that for every six patients treated,
  • one major stroke was prevented at two years for symptomatic patients with 70 to 99% Sternosis.
  • Smaller benefit was demonstrated with patients with 50 to 69% occlusion,
  • with a number needed to treat of 22 at five years.
  • NICE Guidance published in July 2008 advises patients with non-disabling strokes or TIAs
  • receive Carotid Imaging within a week of symptom onset. Sternosis is measured according to
  • the criteria from either of the trials mentioned. Symptomatic Sternosis of 50 to 99% according to
  • Nasset criteria or 70 to 99% according to ECST criteria,
  • warrants referral and assessment for Carotid Endar Terectomy with the procedure being undertaken
  • within two weeks from symptom onset. Discuss the risks involved in Carotid Endar Terectomy.
  • The risk can be classified into those associated with the patient comorbidities
  • and those associated with the anaesthetic and the procedure itself.
  • Carotid Endar Terectomy is a high-risk procedure. The risks can be classified into those associated
  • with the patient's comorbidities and those related to the surgery itself.
  • Patients presenting with symptomatic Sternosis commonly have widespread atherosclerotic disease.
  • Therefore they are often elderly, hyper-tensive, smokers with diabetes,
  • ischemic heart disease and or COPD. Comorbidity adversely affects the outcome,
  • so patients with multiple medical problems have a higher postoperative mortality and morbidity
  • and hence benefits less from the procedure. The procedure itself carries considerable risk.
  • Perioperative combined mortality and major stroke risk is 2-5%.
  • Other than stroke, the procedure carries a risk of TIAs, myocardial infarction and dyschemia.
  • Hypo or hyper-tension may occur, hemorrhage can be potentially life-threatening and hematomas
  • can cause tracheal compression and laryngeal edema. Damage to surrounding structures
  • such as the recurrent laryngeal nerve or hypoglossal nerve can occur during surgery.
  • Another rare but potentially serious complication is the hyper-perfusion syndrome
  • due to the sudden increase in perfusion of the vasculature distal to the sternosis.

Q6
Describe the technique for performing a deep cervical plexus block.
A
  • I would prepare the patient, as I have previously mentioned, and position the patient supine with
  • some head up tilt and the head turned away from the side of the block. I would identify the landmarks,
  • the posterior border of the sterniclider mastoid muscle and feel for the interscaling groove
  • behind the lateral border of the sterniclider mastoid at the level of the thyroid cartilage C4.
  • Then, using a septic technique, I would use some local anesthetic to the skin and
  • insert a 5mm needle towards the contralateral elbow, that is in a medial, cordal and dorsal direction
  • until the patient feels parasthesia or the bone of C4 transverse process is contacted.
  • After careful aspiration, I would inject 8-10 mils of 0.25% bupivocane.

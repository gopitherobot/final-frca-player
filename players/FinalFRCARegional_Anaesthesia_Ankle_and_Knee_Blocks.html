Ankle and knee blocks. This topic will be well suited to the clinical sciences' viather, but could easily incorporate features of physiology, pharmacology and physics, and therefore also be found in the basic sciences viather.
Q1
What nerve supply the structures below the knee?
A
  • The nerve supply to structures below the level of the knee is derived mainly from the
  • sciatic nerve.
  • Although the saffonous nerve supplies a variable portion of the medial calf as far as the medial
  • malleolus, the sciatic nerve derived from L4 to S3 nerve roots divides approximately
  • 6cm above the popliteal skin crease into the tibial and common perineal nerves.
  • The tibial nerve subsequently divides into the medial and lateral plantar nerves beyond
  • the medial malleolus and supplies the plantar surface of the foot.
  • After passing around the neck of the fibula, the common perineal nerve divides into the
  • deep and superficial perineal nerves.
  • The majority of sensation on the dorsum of the foot is supplied by the superficial perineal
  • nerve, whilst the deep branch supplies a small area of skin webbing between the first and
  • second toes.
  • Motor innovation of the ankle is derived from the common perineal nerve which causes dorsiflexion
  • and aversion whilst the tibial nerve results in plantar flexion and inversion.
  • Finally, the churl nerve is formed from fibres of both the common perineal and tibial
  • nerves and supplies sensation of the lateral foot and calf.
Q2
What methods of nerve identification can be used in knee and ankle blocks?
A
  • Identification of the relevant nerves in an ankle block relies primarily on knowledge
  • of anatomy applied to a landmark technique.
  • Although in the past, parathesia has been used to suggest correct placement, these blocks
  • are now mostly performed under general anesthesia.
  • In addition, as few of the ankle nerves have a motor element, the use of the nerve stimulator
  • would not be helpful.
  • Increasingly, the availability of high quality ultrasound devices can aid identification
  • of these nerves even at the ankle.
  • At the knee, a nerve stimulator can be combined with ultrasound to aid identification, although
  • some anaethetists will use ultrasound alone.
Q3
How do nerve stimulators use the identification of peripheral nerves? Different from those used for assessing neuro muscular blockade and why?
A
  • Due to the wide variation of tissue and hence impedance encountered as a nerve is approached
  • by a stimulating needle, devices used for identification of peripheral nerves must
  • be constant current generators.
  • In addition, the delivered output should be clearly indicated on a digital display.
  • The current duration, termed Cronaxes, should be shortened to around 100 milliseconds
  • in order to preferentially stimulate a alpha motor fibers and not a delta pain fibers.
  • These devices use an insulated needle to maximize and pinpoint delivered current whilst minimizing
  • peripheral current loss.
  • Incorrectly attaching the positive electrode to the needle will result in a cone of hyperpolarisation
  • around the nerve and hence reduce effectiveness.
Q4
How would you perform a popliteal nerve block and what are the causes of suboptimal intraoperative analgesia?
A
  • There are two main approaches to blocking nerves of the popliteal fossa, a posterior and a
  • lateral approach.
  • Both approaches target the tibial and common perineal nerves after they have divided from
  • the sciatic nerve above the popliteal fossa.
  • I am most familiar with the lateral approach and feel that it has advantages over the posterior
  • approach as the patient is able to remain supine during placement.
  • After inducing anesthesia, checking all relevant equipment and using sterile precautions,
  • I would insert a 5cm insulated nerve stimulating needle approximately 7cm above the lateral
  • femoral epicondyle between the groove formed by biceps femoris and vastus lateralis.
  • After contacting the femur, I would redirect the needle posteriorly until a motor response
  • of the ankle is elicited at a current above 0.5 mA.
  • After negative aspiration, I would inject 30 mA of 0.5% bupivocane in 5 mA.
  • Suboptimal analgesia can result from a poorly working block or a poorly selected block.
  • A nerve block may fail for a number of reasons.
  • Most commonly, the local anesthetic will have been deposited in the wrong location.
  • Introvascular injection would similarly result in a poor block.
  • Depending on the location of surgery, the nerve block selected may not cover an adequate
  • area.
  • For example, surgery on the medial surface of the foot may require an additional saffonous
  • nerve block to ensure adequate analgesia.
  • In addition, using an above knee tornakei would require additional analgesia or a more
  • proximal nerve block.
  • You perform a pop-till block using a nerve stimulator on a patient with long-term type 1 diabetes.
  • One week later, you are told that she has paracetesia over the outer aspect of her upper
  • thigh.
Q5
What are the potential causes?
A
  • The causes of paracetesia can be divided into those related to anesthesia, those related
  • to surgery and other causes.
  • Considering those related to anesthesia, although the pop-till block will often be blamed
  • for such problems, the pattern of injury makes this very unlikely.
  • The distribution of paracetesia suggests involvement of the lateral cutaneous nerve of the
  • thigh at a point above where the previous block was performed.
  • Damage to the lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh can often be traced to poor interoperative
  • positioning, especially in people with conditions increasing chances of neuropraxia such as diabetes.
  • Poor positioning results in neuropraxia due to a combination of direct nerve compression,
  • ischemia and nerve stretching.
  • The lateral causes would include direct nerve trauma, although in this case, tornocade
  • damage would be more likely.
  • Finally, the paracetesia may be unrelated to recent events and represent a mononuropathy
  • from diabetes alone.
Q6
What are the advantages of a peripheral nerve block over those placed more centrally?
A
  • There are advantages to the technique itself and advantages to the patient.
  • In general, the more peripheral the nerve is blocked, the more superficial it can be found
  • and therefore landmarks become simpler.
  • Additionally, superficial nerves are often more amenable to ultrasound imaging and possibly
  • easier to identify with a nerve stimulator.
  • Turning to advantages for the patient, central nerve blocks tend to be placed closer to
  • vascular and neurological structures and thus the risks of peripheral blocks tend to be less.
  • In addition, blocking nerves more peripherally will enable one to avoid some of the central
  • effects of nerve blockade, including urinary retention and a dense whole limb weakness.
Q7
Should a popletyl nerve block be performed in a patient with a distal tibia fracture?
A
  • There are advantages and disadvantages to using peripheral nerve blocks.
  • A traumatic tibial fracture is known to carry a risk of compartment syndrome which persists
  • even after surgical fixation.
  • Some may argue that performing a long-lasting peripheral nerve block would make identifying
  • a compartment syndrome difficult.
Q8
However, even when a nerve block has been successful, the use of a tornake will often induce
A
  • intraoperative physiological changes consistent with pain.
  • There have been studies showing that this ischemia type of pain, common in compartment syndrome,
  • will break through even a perfectly working peripheral nerve block.
  • It has also been shown that good postoperative analgesia and hence early mobilization can
  • reduce the incidence of compartment syndrome.
  • It could be argued that if surgeons are so concerned about the risks of compartment
  • syndrome to avoid using a peripheral nerve block, then a different surgical technique or postoperative
  • compartment pressure monitoring should be used.
  • Overall, the decision to use a peripheral nerve block in these circumstances should be
  • made jointly between the surgeon and the anesthetist and would be influenced by the safety
  • of administering systemic analgesia to the patient in question.

Strabismus. You've been asked to anesthize a four-year-old child for correction of a squint.
Q1
What factors should be considered in the pre-operative assessment?
A
  • In addition to the general assessment of the child, it is important to consider whether
  • this is an isolated strabismus or whether it is secondary to a muscular disorder. Squints
  • are associated with rare conditions such as cruisons and apert syndrome and also with
  • malignant hyperthermia, so it is important to elicit whether there is a family history
  • of this condition.
  • Muscle disorders associated with malignant hyperthermia include central core disease,
  • myotonia congenitor, and douchens muscular dystrophy. It is also important to establish
  • whether there are any other congenital abnormalities or whether the child has learning difficulties.
  • General considerations for pediatric anesthesia include a history of medical conditions such
  • as asthma, recent respiratory tract infections, epilepsy, or a history of prematurity. Other
  • factors to consider include previous anesthetic history, family history of anesthetic problems,
  • medications, allergies, and period of starvation.

Fine. What would you use for intraoperative and postoperative analgesia for squint surgery?
A
  • This type of surgery is not particularly painful, and opioids should be avoided to decrease the risk
  • of postoperative nausea and vomiting. A combination of fentanyl or alphentanyl with a
  • Volterol suppository at a dose of 1 milligram per kilogram and intravenous paracetamol at a dose
  • of 15 milligrams per kilogram is usually adequate. Supplemental local anesthesia should also be used,
  • and subconjunctival local anesthetic has a longer lasting effect than topical eye drops.
  • Some surgeons also apply 0.5% dichylophanec eye drops on the muscle insurgents before closing the
  • conjunctival. Peribolba blocks, whilst producing effective analgesia and reducing the incidence of the
  • ocular cardiac reflex, are inadvisable in children due to the risk of globe perforation and retrobolba
  • hemorrhage. The patients should be given regular ibuprofen at a dose of 5 milligrams per kilogram
  • and paracetamol at a dose of 15 milligrams per kilogram to be taken four times a day for three

days postoperatively. You mentioned postoperative nausea and vomiting. Is this usually a problem?
A
  • Yes, it can be. Untreated, the incidence of postoperative vomiting in squint surgery is very high
  • and can be greater than 50%. Actually, it is not as high as that with resection and recession
  • and is closer to 25 to 30%. It is much higher with the fade-in operation, which involves the placement
  • of a posterior fixation suture, however this is no longer performed in the United Kingdom.
  • Postoperative nausea and vomiting is also worse with bilateral operations.
  • Its precise mechanism is unknown, but may be due to an ocular emetic reflex involving the
  • ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve under the vomiting centre in the medulla. It can be
  • prevented by prophylaxis with intravenous endanceratron and dexamethasone, a combination of which is
  • recommended by a recent association of pediatric anesthesiokide line in 2008 on the prevention of postoperative
  • vomiting in high-risk children. Maintaining adequate hydration is also important, and all children
  • should receive 20 mils per kilo of intravenous fluids. It is possible to reduce the incidence of
  • vomiting to less than 10% when a multimodal approach is used. Also, if not, is that the predisposition
  • to postoperative nausea and vomiting persists for 48 hours postoperatively.

Feeding.
Q1
Tell me about entral nutrition.
A
  • The best nutrition has obtained when food is chewed, swallowed and digested, where possible
  • the entral route should always be used.
  • The entral route has benefits over and above the supply of nutrition, playing a role in
  • maintenance of gut integrity and protecting against bacterial translocation across the
  • gut wall.
  • Entral nutrition can be administered via a nasogastric or nasogedginal feeding tube,
  • or via a percutaneous gastric or percutaneous gedginal feeding tube.
  • The most commonly used route is the nasogastric route, but it relies on adequate gastric emptying.
  • The nasogedginal route is less commonly used, as it is more difficult for the tube to
  • be positioned correctly.
  • It often requires an endoscope, but it is thought to be advantageous in patients with
  • severe pancreatitis, and some suggest that it may decrease the rate of pneumonia.
  • Percutaneous feeding tubes are useful when entral feeding is likely to be required long
  • term, such as in patients with cerebral palsy, head injury or after major maxural facial
  • surgery.
  • It has the advantage of avoiding the discomfort of a nasal tube.
  • Successful entral feeding requires the use of feeding protocols.
  • These will vary from centre to centre, but in general most protocols insist on a gastric
  • residual volume of less than 200 ml, following a four-hour period of continuous feeding.
  • If the volume is greater than 200 ml, pro-kinetics are often indicated to aid gut motility.
Q2
What different types of entral feeds are available?
A
  • The majority of entral feeds are polymeric and contain one kilocalorie per mil.
  • Special feeds are available, which are fibroenriched, such as Jevety Plus.
  • Nepro is available for renal patients who are fluid restricted as it contains two kilocalories
  • per mil.
  • Elemental feeds are available for patients with gastrointestinal problems, such as short
  • bowel syndrome and pancreatitis.
  • Polymercare contains a higher proportion of protein and fat and less carbohydrate, and
  • can be beneficial in patients with carbon dioxide retention, as it reduces the amount of carbon
  • dioxide produced.
Q3
What are the complications of entral feeding?
A
  • Entral feeding can be impeded by a number of mechanical problems.
  • The lumen of the feeding tube can become obstructed or displaced.
  • The tube can cause discomfort and in extreme cases ulceration.
  • Lureusophageal reflux occurs more commonly with a nasogastric tube and can lead to aspiration
  • and pneumonia.
  • Entral feeding can cause various metabolic problems, including dehydration or over-hydration,
  • hyperglycemia, hypercatenia and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Blood glucose should be monitored and an insulin-sliding scale started where appropriate.
  • Intestinal feed can be increased if the carbon dioxide is seen to rise, especially in feeds
  • with a high carbohydrate load.
  • Electrolite imbalance may occur, particularly in patients who have been without a food
  • source for a prolonged period, where refeeding syndrome should be suspected and treated aggressively.
  • Although in testinal complications of entral feeding include gastric retention, nausea,
  • vomiting, diarrhea and bloating.
  • Prokinetics can be helpful in some cases of gastric retention and feeds with high fiber
  • can help limit diarrhea.
  • Alternative causes for all of the above symptoms should be sought prior to attributing them
  • to the feed.
  • In particular, patients with diarrhea should be screened for clostridium difficeule.
Q4
What prokinetics can be used in critical care to improve entral nutrition?
A
  • Metaclopromide and erythromycin are the most commonly used prokinetics on critical care.
  • Metaclopromide works by improving gastric emptying, but has no effect on pneumonia or mortality.
  • Erythromycin improves short-term tolerance of entral nutrition and reduces gastric
  • residual volumes, but its use is limited by concerns over development of antibiotic
  • resistance.
  • Near stigmean is occasionally used to improve tolerance of entral feeding, but it is not
  • licensed for this.
Q5

A
  • The use of total parental nutrition or TPN is generally reserved for those patients in

A
  • TPN is usually formulated as a lipid emulsion and contains medium branched amino acids.
  • The nutrient solution consists of water and electrolytes, glucose, amino acids and
  • lipids.
  • Essential vitamins, minerals and trace elements are added or given separately.
  • TPN is hyperosmolar and irritant to veins, and therefore usually given through a central
  • vein via a continuous infusion, although some formulations do exist that can be administered
  • via peripheral access.
  • It is imperative to check electrolytes daily and the levels of trace elements should be adjusted
  • accordingly.
  • This is especially important where the patient is at high risk of refeedings in-drome.
  • Patients receiving TPN are at high risk of line infection.
  • Meticulous asepsis is therefore required when connecting and disconnecting feeds and
  • the infusion line should only be broken when absolutely necessary.
  • In recent years, 24 hour-leabags of TPN have been introduced to keep disconnections to
  • a minimum.
  • In some circumstances, it may be appropriate to administer TPN and entral feeds simultaneously.
  • In these special cases, it is essential to have the input of a dietician.
Q7
What is refeeding syndrome?
A
  • Refeeding syndrome consists of a number of metabolic disturbances that occur as a result of
  • re-institution of nutrition to a patient who has been starved or severely malnourished
  • for a prolonged period.
  • It usually occurs within four days of starting feeding and is characterized by hypophosphatemia
  • accompanied by a multitude of life-threatening complications such as cardiac and respiratory
  • failure, coma, seizures, raptomylicis and hematological dysfunction.
  • The underlying cause is a shift from fat to carbohydrate metabolism.
  • This results in a sudden increase in insulin levels, which in turn increase cellular uptake
  • of phosphate causing a precipitous fall in extracellular phosphate.
  • Levels of potassium, magnesium, glucose and thymine also fall.
  • The shifting of electrolytes and fluid balance increases cardiac workload and heart rate,

which can cause acute heart failure.
A
  • Oxygen consumption is also increased, which strains the respiratory system and can result
  • in worsening respiratory failure.
  • It can be fatal if it is not recognised and treated properly.
  • Electrolite levels should be checked daily until they are stable.
  • Treatment includes starting feed slowly and aggressive correction of electrolyte deficiency
  • via the intravenous root.
  • In those thought to be at risk of thymine deficiency, supplements should be prescribed.
Q9
What are immune enhancing diets?
A
  • The dietary supplementation of glutamine, arginine, fish oils and antioxidants such as vitamin
  • C and E.
  • In the critical care setting, selenium has been the focus of much research in the last decade.
  • Unfortunately, the exact role of this immunonutrition is inconclusive and randomised clinical trials
  • of immune enhanced feeds have failed to show a clear benefit in critically ill patients.
  • Glutamine seems to have the largest body of evidence behind it and benefits have been
  • shown in certain circumstances.
  • Arginine has been shown to be beneficial in cancer patients, but may be harmful in the
  • critically ill as increasing arginine levels leads to an increase in nitric oxide production.
  • Each use is therefore not recommended in critical care.
  • Fish oils such as omega-3 fatty acids have limited evidence to suggest a survival benefit
  • in acute lung injury, but more research is needed.
  • Selenium is the most promising antioxidant to have been studied, but again, more research
  • is needed to confirm benefits in critically ill patients.
  • Only more about glutamine supplementation.
  • Glutamine is the most abundant, non-essential amino acid.
  • It is synthesised and stored in skeletal muscle.
  • It has a central role in nitrogen transport, urea genesis, ammonia genesis and regulation

  • conditionally essential amino acid, and patients with severe trauma, sepsis, major surgery
  • and chemo-oradiotherapy often have low levels of glutamine.
  • This can lead to a reduced capacity of immune cells to proliferate and an impaired immune
  • function.
  • Evidence so far suggests some theoretical benefits of glutamine administration.
  • Notably, it is thought to improve nitrogen balance and preserve glutamine levels in skeletal
  • muscle.

Q11
What special considerations are there for feeding patients with liver failure, renal failure and pancreatitis?
A
  • Patients with liver failure may have marked pre-existing electrolyte abnormalities, and
  • may have been on fluid-restricted diets to minimize a site's formation.
  • Patassium, magnesium and zinc losses are common in these patients, and they may have profound
  • hyponatremia despite having high total body sodium concentrations.
  • The sodium levels should be corrected slowly to avoid the risk of central pontine myelinolysis.
  • In patients that have developed n-cafolopathy, feeds with increased branched chain amino
  • acids and reduced sulfur-containing amino acids should be administered.
  • These limit further accumulation of ammonia, which is responsible for the n-cafolopathy.
  • In patients with renal failure, the feeding regime will need to be tailored to the type
  • of renal replacement therapy being received.
  • In fluid-restricted patients, low volume, low sodium feeds should be used.
  • In patients with chronic renal failure, nitrogen intake will need to be reduced to between
  • 0.5 and 0.8 grams of nitrogen per kilo per day.
  • In patients on peritoneal dialysis, glucose levels should be monitored, as the dialysis
  • fluid usually contains glucose.
  • Feeding regimes in acute pancreatitis have been much debated.
  • It was traditionally thought that TPN should be used and the pancreas should be rested.
  • But more recent evidence suggests that this may not be associated with improved outcomes.
  • Most pyloric feeding has been advocated, but it has been shown to stimulate pancreatic
  • enzyme synthesis and secretion as much as gastric feeding.
  • Some studies suggest that resting the bowel may exacerbate the inflammatory response and
  • worsen the catabolic process of pancreatitis, thus increasing protein deficiency.
  • There is therefore increasing support for entral polymeric feeding in severe acute pancreatitis.

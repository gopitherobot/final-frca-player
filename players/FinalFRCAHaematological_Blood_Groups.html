Blood groups. What is a blood group and how are they classified? Blood groups are defined as inherited character of the red cell surface, which are detected by a specific alloantibolly. The main system in use is the ABO system. There are, however, over 285 blood group antigens. Examples of other systems are the MNS, the RISIS, KEL, and Duffy systems. Blood group antigens may be proteins, glycoproteins, or glycolipids,

which form an integral structure of the red cell membrane. Adults have A or B, or both antigens, and naturally occurring antibodies to those ABO antigens they lack. Describe the antigens, antibodies, and patterns of inheritance of the ABO blood groups. The system has two antigens, A and B. These are products of the A and B allials. There is a third allial O that produces no antigen and is recessive to A and B. A, B, AB, and O are the phenotypes that result from the genotypes A or AO, BB or BO,

AB from AB and O and O from OO. In the UK, the commonest phenotype is O, 47%, followed by A, 42%, B, 8%, and AB, 3%. Anti-A antibody is present when the A antigen is absent, and anti-B antibody is present when the B antigen is absent. The antibodies in adults are most commonly IgM, however IgG and IgA may occur. In neonates, the antibodies are usually IgG inherited from the mother and begin to reach adult levels by 5 to 10 years of age. Are you aware of any recent changes to the transfusion guidelines?

Every patient should have an appropriate pre-operative assessment within an assist involved and be optimised if required prior to surgery that is likely to involve blood transfusion. Normal hemoglobin post-operatively is an unnecessary as transfusion is not devoid of risks. It is necessary to have written and readily available guidelines on blood transfusion. European law advocates the permanent recording of every transfusion and the track and trace system should be in place in all departments.

The reason for transfusions should be clearly recorded in the patient's notes. All hospitals should have a transfusion committee with an anaesthetic representative. Blood loss of 30% may be treated effectively with colloids or crystalloids. The main function of the hemoglobin is oxygen carriage and delivery. Post-optive patients are likely to be less mobile and the oxygen demand will not necessarily exceed the supply. Hence, patients should not be transfused if the hemoglobin concentration is greater than 10 grams per desi-litre.

However, if these patients become symptomatic, they should be transfused. Patients with hemoglobin concentrations of less than 7 grams per desi-litre are likely to need transfusion. And those with a concentration less than 5 grams per desi-litre should certainly be transfused. How can the need for allergenic blood transfusion be minimized? Appropriate pre-optive assessment is the key to minimizing post-operative transfusion.

Elective surgery in anemic patients should be postponed until they have been appropriately investigated and treated. Patients on anti-coagulants should have their medications stopped for the prerequisite time before surgery. Early hematological advice should be sought in patients with bleeding and coagulation disorders. Autologous blood transfusion is the collection of blood from a patient and re-transfusioned to the same patient. Three techniques are commonly described. Pre-operative autologous transfusion, where the patient donates blood pre-operatively, it is stored appropriately and then transfused back to the patient if required.

The next technique commonly used is cell salvage, where blood collected from surgical sites and drains is washed, filtered and transfused, and then back to the patient. A technique less commonly used is the acute normovalemic hemodilution, where blood is collected just before surgery and is repaced with colloidal crystalloid. Once hemostasis is achieved, the blood is re-transfused. Blood loss may be minimized by using a hypotensive anesthetic technique, regional anesthesia, the use of tornocase and local infiltration of vasoconstrictors. Pharmacotherapy involves the use of trinocaminic acid and desmopressin, post-operatively the oxygen consumption may be decreased by elective ventilation of the patient and avoiding hyperthermia.

Alternative oxygen delivering fluids also commonly referred to as blood substitutes include perfluoro-carbon and modified hemoglobin solutions. These have been under investigation for many years and there is no indication that these components will become available for routine use. What are the risks of blood transfusion? Have a systematic approach to the answer and most complications should be covered. A system-wise approach is used here however, alternative classifications such as immediate and delayed may also be used. Immunological reactions occur in 2% of transfusions.

IgE-mediated anaphylaxis, IgM-mediated hemolysis, and IgG-mediated hemolysis and hemolytic disease of the newborn may occur. Other immune-mediated complications are delayed hemolysis, transfusion-related acute lung injury, graft-facestus-host disease in immunocompromised patients, and fibroial-erticarial reactions. Metabolic complications include hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, citrate toxicity and alkylosis. Fluid overload and cardiac failure may occur in susceptible patients. Impaired coagulation may occur due to lack of clotting factors and platelets impact red cell transfusions. Embellism, microaggregates and ex-travisation are possible during transfusion.

Rapid transfusion of cold blood may lead to hypothermia. Due to the left shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve installed blood tissue oxygen delivery is impaired. Infective risks associated with blood transfusion include transmission of hepatitis B, hepatitis C, HIV, HTL, malaria, cytomegalovirus, brusolosis, bacterial contamination and prion diseases.

Q1
Describe the anatomy of thisophagus.
A
  • The esophagus is a muscular tube connecting the pharynx to the stomach.
  • The upper third of the esophagus consists of an outer longitudinal and an inner circular layer of striated muscle.
  • The lower two-thirds consists of smooth muscle.
  • The inner lumen consists of stratified squamous epithelium.
  • There are two esophageals fictors.
  • The first, the cryocrypharangius muscle, acts as the functional upper esophageals fictor
  • and has a high resting intra-luminal pressure of 6.7 to 13.3 kilopascals.
  • The second, the lower esophageals fictor, is a functional entity rather than an anatomical one.
  • It results from thickened, tonically contracted smooth muscle at the lowest 2 to 4 cm of the esophagus
  • and it has a resting pressure of 2 to 3.3 kilopascals above the gastric pressure, which prevents gastroesophageal reflux.
Q2
What is the barrier pressure and what is its significance?
A
  • Barrier pressure is the lower esophageals fictor pressure minus the intra-gastric pressure.
  • The significance is that the lower esophageals fictor pressure is reduced by pregnancy under drugs,
  • such as atropine and succimethonium, and that the intra-gastric pressure is increased in the fed state.
Q3
When one encroaches on the other, barrier pressure fails and reflux is possible. Describe the anatomy of the stomach.
A
  • Functionally, the stomach is made up of the fundus, the body and the antrum.
  • Each muscle layer forms a syncytium, acting as a unit.
  • Between the stomach and the duodenum is the pyloric sphinctor, a junction formed by thickened circular smooth muscle.
  • The stomach has an intrinsic and extrinsic nerve supply.
  • The extrinsic nerve supply is from the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems.
  • The sympathetic supply via the celiac plexus inhibits motility.
  • The parasympathetic supply via the vagus nerve stimulates motility.
  • The intrinsic nerve supply is responsible for parastalsis and is formed by mysna's plexus, which is sub-mucosal,
  • and the our backs plexus, which lies between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers of the stomach.
Q4
How is gastric emptying normally controlled? And what are the cause of delayed gastric emptying?
A
  • As always with this sort of question, a classification will aid your answer and impress the examiner.
  • The physical state and chemical composition of a substance affect the speed at which it is emptied from the stomach.
  • Liquid's emptying more rapidly than solids.
  • The rate of emptying of solids depends on the rate at which crime is broken down into small particles.
  • Increased gastric volume produces distension, which provokes vagal reflexes leading to increased gastric emptying.
  • Both gastric distension and high protein content of food stimulate gastric secretion, which enhances gastric emptying.
  • Nutrients in the duodenum activate chemoreceptors which inhibit gastric emptying, allowing time for further digestion and absorption in the small intestine.
  • Hypertonicity, fatty acids and hydrogen ions activate the secretion of colycysticinin, secretin, and gastric inhibitory peptide, which inhibit gastric emptying.
  • Many factors cause delayed gastric emptying and can be classified as physiological, pathological, and pharmacological.
  • Physiological causes include pain, anxiety, pregnancy and age, pathological causes include gastrointestinal obstruction,
  • electrolyte abnormalities, diabetes, gastritis, raised intracranial pressure and migraine.
  • Pharmacological causes include opioids, anticholinergics, sympathetic medics, alcohol and dopamine ergics.
Q5
What is gastric acid and what stimulates its production?
A
  • To help you answer the second part of this question, a simple diagram of a parietal cell, including the three main receptors and the hydrogen ion pump, will impress the examiner and will aid you if a further question about drug actions is asked.
  • Gastric secretions include hydrochloric acid, pepsin, gastrin, mucus and intrinsic factor.
  • The parietal cells secrete hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor. The cells are located in the body and fundus of the stomach.
  • A pH of 1 to 1.5 is needed for optimal pepsin activity as well as providing a degree of antimicrobial activity.
  • Pepsin initiates protein digestion, intrinsic factor is necessary for vitamin B12 absorption in the terminal isleum and mucus is essential for protection of the mucusal cells.
  • The three most important factors that act on the parietal cell to stimulate hydrochloric acid production are histamine, via H2 receptors, acetylcholine, via M1 muscarinic receptors and gastrin-vigastrin receptors.
  • These receptors are located on the basalateral membrane of the parietal cell.
  • The apical membrane contains an ATP-dependent hydrogenine pump.
  • The three receptors on the basalateral membrane utilize second messenger systems.
  • Acetylcholine and gastrin increase intracellular concentrations of calcium via ionocyte-altryphosphate activation.
  • The histamine receptors activate adenyl cyclase via g-stimulatory proteins.
  • This results in an increase in cyclic AMP levels.
  • Increased calcium and cyclic AMP causes activation of specific protein kinases which increase the activity of the hydrogen ion pump.
  • Hydrogen ions are produced by the reaction of carbon dioxide with water under the influence of carbonic and hydraise.
  • The hydrogen ions are then actively transported into the lumen by the hydrogen potassium ATP-dependent pump.
  • By carbonate ions pass across the basalateral membrane in exchange for chloride ions via an anti-port mechanism.
  • The logical control of gastric acid production may be considered in terms of chivalic, gastric and intestinal factors.
  • Chivalic factors such as anticipation, sight, smell and taste are mediated via the vagus nerve.
  • This occurs before food ingestion and can be responsible for up to 50% of gastric acid secretion.
  • The gastric phase is initiated by the entry of food into the stomach.
  • Acid secretion is stimulated in several ways.
  • Distention of the stomach activates mechanoreceptors, stimulating vagal reflexes, gastrin release from g-cells and acid secretion from parietal cells.
  • The acidic pH enhances pepsinogen secretion mediated by local reflexes.
  • The intestinal phase is initiated as chime enters the duodenum, stimulating further gastrin release.
  • But this is of minor importance compared with chivalic and gastric factors.
Q6
What factors predisposed of vomiting or regurgitation and aspiration of gastric contents?
A
  • Vomiting is an active process.
  • As it is more likely to occur during the lighter planes of anesthesia, it is more common during induction of and emergence from anesthesia.
  • It should not occur during maintenance of anesthesia.
  • Regurgitation is a passive process, therefore it may occur at any time and is not always immediately apparent.
  • Because regurgitation usually occurs during deep anesthesia, laryngeal reflexes are absent and the risk of aspiration is high.
  • The most common factors are non-elective surgical procedures, light anesthesia, acute or chronic upper or lower gastrin-tastinal pathology, obesity, opioid medication, impaired conscious level, lyphotomy position,
  • physical tearway or intubation, gastrointestinal reflux and hiatus hernia.
  • A patient with a known hiatus hernia presents for an emergency laparotomy.
Q7
What drugs would you use pre-operatively to reduce the risk of regurgitation and aspiration?
A
  • My aims in this case would be to promote gastric emptying, reduce gastric acid production and neutralize gastric acidity.
  • To promote gastric emptying I would give metaclopromide.
  • Its prokinetic actions are mediated by antagonism of peripheral dopaminergic receptors and selective stimulation of gastric muscarinic receptors.
  • This results in an increased lower esophageal sphincter tone and relaxation of the pylorus.
  • To reduce gastric acid production I would use either a histamine 2 receptor antagonist such as renitidine or a proton pump inhibitor such as a meprizol or lanzoprizol.
  • Renitidine is a competitive antagonist of H2 receptors at parietal cells, the gastric pH is raised and the volume of secretions is reduced.
  • It does not affect the lower esophageal sphincter.
  • A meprizol reversibly blocks the hydrogen ion pump in the apical membrane of the parietal cell, the final common pathway of gastric acid secretion.
  • Again, it does not affect lower esophageal sphincter tone.
  • Proton pump inhibitors have a greater potential to achieve absolute inhibition of acid secretion compared with H2 receptor antagonists.
  • I would use an antacid such as sodium bicarbonate to raise the pH of gastric contents immediately before induction.
  • 30 millimoles of sodium citrate just prior to induction is another alternative.

Control of intraocular pressure. This is a common topic since understanding of intraocular pressure has implications for ophthalmic anesthesia, in particular penetrating eye injury.
Q1
What is a normal IOP?
A
  • It looks good if you can define what they are asking for and then give a range of normal
  • values.
  • Intraocular pressure is the tissue pressure of the globe contents on the scleror.
  • The normal range is 10 to 20mm of mercury.
Q2
What determines intraocular pressure?
A
  • As ever, classify your answer.
  • Intraocular pressure is determined by the contents of the globe, scleror rigidity and
  • external pressure.
  • The globe contents are determined by aqueous humor production and drainage, blood volume,
  • both arterial and venous and vitrious humor.
  • The vitrious humor volume is relatively fixed, so does not normally affect intraocular
  • pressure.
  • Other factors may increase intraocular pressure from within the globe, such as tumour, hemorrhage
  • or a foreign body.
  • The scleror becomes more rigid with age and is therefore less compliant to changes in
  • globe contents.
  • The global factors include extra-global muscle tone, retro-bulber hematoma, abscess, tumour
  • or anesthetic block.
  • There may also be external pressure from face masks, position, for example, in the prone
  • or lateral positions or from the surgeon.
Q3
How is intraocular pressure normally regulated?
A
  • It would be worth drawing a diagram to show the flow of aqueous humor.
  • It is normally regulated by aqueous humor volume.
  • The aqueous humor is produced in the silvery bodies.
  • This is mainly by active secretion, which is fairly constant.
  • There is also some ultra-filtration, which will be reduced if intraocular pressure is
  • high.
  • The fluid then flows from the posterior chamber through the iris into the anterior chamber.
  • It is then reabsorbed through the canal of Schlem, which is in the angle between the
  • iris and cornea.
  • This canal drains into the episcleral veins.
  • If intraocular pressure rises, the rate of absorption will increase.
  • If the reabsorption is blocked, or episcleroviness pressure is increased, then intraocular pressure
  • will increase.
  • An alternative, less important route of drainage, is via the UV-l-tracked route.
Q4
Why don't these tests need to know about intraocular pressure?
A
  • Remember, it's not only about penetrating eye injury.
  • It's important in penetrating eye injury, as an increase in intraocular pressure can cause
  • expulsion of globe contents.
  • Also, patients with acute or chronically raised intraocular pressure may present for non-upthalmic
  • surgery.
  • Anesthetic drugs and procedures have effects on intraocular pressure.
Q5
Tell me how blood volume affects IOP.
A
  • I would like to see you applying basic physiology to clinical applications.
  • Don't wait to be asked.
  • The most important factor is venous pressure.
  • The coroidal venous plexus drains into the episcleral veins.
  • The pressure within these episcleral veins is determined by central venous pressure.
  • If the central venous pressure is high, draining is reduced, and intraocular blood volume and
  • pressure will increase.
  • If you are trying to control intraocular pressure, then a head up tilt, avoidance of neck
  • ties, a head in the neutral position, and avoidance of coughing and straining will help.
  • Artereal blood flow is determined in the same way as cerebral blood flow.
  • It increases linearly with arterial carbon dioxide tension, increases in hypoxia and high
  • blood pressure.
Q6
How do anaesthetic drugs affect IOP?
A
  • Try to not come out with a random selection.
  • One way to classify them is in the order that you use them.
  • All of the induction agents accept ketamine reduce intraocular pressure.
  • Prophefall reduces intraocular pressure the most.
  • Non-depolarizing muscular blockers decrease extrocular muscle tone and decrease intraocular
  • pressure.
  • Succemethonium transiently increases intraocular pressure.
  • Initially this was thought to be due to extrocular muscle for circulation, but in fact occurs
  • even if these muscles have been detached.
  • Succemethonium provides the best and quickest intubating conditions for rapid sequence induction
  • in penetrating eye injury.
  • It is normally given after an induction agent which decreases intraocular pressure and
  • prevents coughing which may accompany early intubation, which may occur after a non-depolarizing
  • blocker has been given.
  • This may increase intraocular pressure to an even greater extent.
Q7
What about volatile agents?
A
  • All of the volatile anaesthetic agents decrease intraocular pressure.
Q8
What about nitrous oxide?
A
  • Nitrous oxide has little direct effect on intraocular pressure, however it should be avoided if other
  • gases are being injected into the eye by the ophthalmic surgeons.
  • For example, sulfur hexafluoride may be injected in retinal detachment surgery.
  • If nitrous oxide is used, it will diffuse in more easily than nitrogen diffuses out, making
  • the gas bubble bigger.
  • On stopping anaesthesia, the nitrous oxide will diffuse out more quickly than nitrogen
  • diffuses in and the retina may again become detached.
Q9
What about opioids?
A
  • Opioids have little direct effect on intraocular pressure but can decrease the hypertensive
  • response to intubation along with lidocane, beta blockers and clonidine.
Q10
What can be done to reduce IOP acutely?
A
  • For example, if the surgeon says there is protrusion of the orbital contents.
  • Make sure you emphasize that you know this is an emergency.
  • Don't go straight for a manitone.
  • Remember A, B, C.
  • This is an emergency.
  • First, I need to ensure there are normal physiological variables, the patient is well-oxygenated,
  • is normal-capnic and is normal-tensive.
  • I would ensure that venous pressure is not raised by making sure there are no ties around
  • the neck, impeding venous drainage and that the head is in the neutral position.
  • I would also ensure that the patient is not coughing or straining with adequate depth
  • of anesthesia and paralyzing the patient.
  • In the emergency situation, intravenous propyl can be given to reduce the intracular pressure.
  • Propyl is also useful whilst waiting for other agents or techniques to work.
  • Other drugs used in the management of intracular pressure include manitol and acetazolamide.
  • It is important to remember the rebound side effects that manitol may have on the circulation
  • postoperatively and to the electrolyte arrangements which may occur with acetazolamide, especially
  • in the elderly.
  • Regular electrolytes will need to be done if acetazolamide has been given.

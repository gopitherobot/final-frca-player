Day-K selection.
Q1
What are the advantages of day-K surgery?
A
  • There are many advantages of day-K surgery, which is one of the reasons why the NHS plan
  • has set a target of 75% of all elective surgeries being performed on a day-case basis.
  • There are advantages for patients as most prefer day-case rather than overnight stay.
  • In the elderly population, it has been shown to decrease post-operative cognitive dysfunction.
  • It is ideal for children and elderly patients.
  • There are also other patient benefits, not just psychological ones.
  • These include less cross-infection and early mobilisation, reducing the risk of deep vein
  • thrombosis.
  • Staff are easier to obtain due to social hours, and there are increased economic benefits
  • to the trust as there can be an increased efficiency in a well-run day-case service reducing waiting
Q2
lists. Are there any disadvantages that you can think of?
A
  • It is important to assess and have robust guidelines for the selection of patients for
  • day-case surgery.
  • There is also inherently a limitation of some anesthetic techniques such as epidural or
  • indwelling catheter techniques for analgesia postoperatively.
  • There also has to be facility for admission of a proportion of patients due to intra and
  • postoperative complications.
Q3
What surgery can be performed as day-case and what are the important considerations?
A
  • There has to be consideration as to which surgery is appropriate to be performed as day-case.
  • It is important to make sure that any surgery can be done safely as a day-case without compromising
  • patient care.
  • The complication rate should be low with minimal blood loss.
  • There must be adequate control of pain, nausea and vomiting, and the patients must be able
  • to eat and drink and mobilize within a reasonable time frame after the surgery is complete.
Q4
So how are you going to select patients to have day-case surgery?
A
  • There are social and medical factors that affect whether a patient can have day-case surgery
  • or not.
  • Social factors include willingness on the patient's behalf, easy access to a phone and transport
  • back to the hospital.
  • There should be a responsible adult who can stay with the patient for at least the first
  • 24 hours postoperatively and the conditions at home should be compatible with postoperative
  • care, with adequate toilet and bathroom facilities.
  • There should be no more than a 60 minute travelling time back to the hospital should complications
  • occur.
  • Patients should have a telephone number to contact in case of emergencies and be discharged
  • with an advice sheet explaining what to do in case of complications.
  • Social factors are more difficult to quantify and in many circumstances clinical judgment
  • needs to be employed.
  • In general, patients should be relatively fit and well, mobile and not morbidly obese.
  • Patients should be selected according to their physiological status and not their chronological
  • age.
  • Some patients who fall outside guidelines may particularly benefit and should be discussed
  • on a case-by-case basis, for example, people with stable diabetes.
  • Obesity is not an absolute contraindication for day-case surgery, but there are problems
  • with this population.
Q5
What are the problems of treating obese patients in the day-case setting?
A
  • There needs to be appropriate resources and facilities for the management of obese
  • patients, and specialist equipment and staff may not be available.
  • There is an increased risk of respiratory compromise in the obese population, and late
  • complications may develop more frequently, and therefore a longer hospital stay may be
  • appropriate in some circumstances.
  • Obidly obese patients may also provide increased technical challenge to surgeons.
  • Body Mass Index, though not the ideal tool for assessing these patients, does give pre-assessment
  • nurses a guide.
  • Our day-case unit has a body Mass Index cutoff of 35 for all procedures, as there is not
  • the appropriate facility available.

Q7
How else can you select patients?
A
  • Other general factors to consider include no previous problems with surgery or anesthesia,
  • and no significant family history of anesthetic complications.
  • The type of surgery needs to be taken into account, and the likelihood of significant
  • bleeding, and or prolonged and difficult surgery.
  • Newer techniques, such as total intravenous anesthesia, and the use of desflurrain, mean
  • that surgery does not have to be time limited to be performed as a day-case.
  • If there are specialist services needed, such as radiological intervention, then there may
  • be logistical reasons why day-case surgery may not be appropriate.
  • In these cases, surgery should be planned as an inpatient.
  • You have mentioned general and surgical factors.
Q8
What other factors are there that need to be considered when selecting patients for day-case surgery?
A
  • Cardiovascular history should be explored, with particular attention being paid to exercise
  • tolerance and reserve.
  • If there are signs of heart failure, such as ankle edema and disnear, then these patients
  • are not appropriate for day-case surgery.
  • Also, uncontrolled angina or myocardial infarction in the last six months should be an exclusion.
  • Vovular heart disease with symptoms should be excluded for further investigation and
  • quantification.
  • Type attention is controversial and should be controlled preoperatively in a day-case
  • surgery setting for an elective procedure.
  • A general practitioner should be able to re-refer the patient directly to the clinic for treatment
  • once this is controlled.
  • Significant respiratory disease should be searched for and optimised prior to day-case surgery.
  • A history of corticosteroid use or intensive care admission recently would make day-case
  • surgery less appropriate.
  • Diabetics can generally be done if performed first on the list, allowing time for eating
  • and drinking to be established post-procedure.
  • A sliding scale should be able to be performed if needed in the day-surgery unit.
  • Epilepsy is not an absolute contraindication unless there is poor control.
  • Recent transient ischemic attacks or so re-brovascular events in the past six months may exclude
  • day-case surgery.
  • This population group is also more likely to be anti-coagulated and every case should
  • be assessed to see if this could be stopped for surgery.
  • If unable to stop, the patient should be done as an inpatient, allowing for transfer
  • to heparin infusion.
  • Other drugs which would make day-surgery more difficult include monoam in oxidase inhibitors
  • and corticosteroids.
  • Excessive alcohol or drug abuse may also exclude day-surgery.
  • Every case should be assessed individually.
Q9
What are the important principles of anaesthetic management for day-case surgery?
A
  • It is important to perform safe and effective anaesthesia with rapid recovery and few complications.
  • The patients need to recover rapidly, have no cardiovascular or respiratory effects, and
  • be awakened or orientated.
  • They should not have nausea or vomiting and be able to drink fluids.
  • Anti-emetics and the avoidance of nitrous oxide or volatile anaesthetic agents may be vital
  • in certain circumstances.
  • Adequat analgesia is vital and opiate sparing techniques are important.
  • Local anaesthetic techniques and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs should be used if appropriate.
Q10
When should patients be discharged and what criteria should they meet?
A
  • As mentioned before, patients should be cardiovascular stable.
  • There should not be excessive blood loss.
  • There should be adequate respiratory parameters with saturations maintained on room air.
  • Pain and nausea and vomiting should be controlled.
  • Any motorblock from a regional technique should have worn off and there should be no impairment
  • of bladder function.
  • Models should be able to mobilize and have adequate social care at home, including access
  • to a phone, bathroom and a responsible adults are care for them for 24 hours.
  • Information should be given on discharge with a telephone number and advice as to what
  • to do if complications arise.
Q11
Why do patients get admitted overnight following day case surgery?
A
  • There are many reasons why patients get admitted following day surgery.
  • There are surgical reasons such as bleeding or more extensive procedure being performed.
  • There are anaesthetic reasons such as pain, cardiovascular or respiratory instability
  • and nausea and vomiting.
  • If any of the discharge criteria are not achieved, such as being unable to pass urine, then
  • the patient should also be admitted.
  • The commonest reasons for admission are inadequate pain relief and nausea and vomiting.

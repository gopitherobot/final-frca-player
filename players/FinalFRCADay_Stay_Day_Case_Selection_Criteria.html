Day-K selection. What are the advantages of day-K surgery? There are many advantages of day-K surgery, which is one of the reasons why the NHS plan has set a target of 75% of all elective surgeries being performed on a day-case basis. There are advantages for patients as most prefer day-case rather than overnight stay. In the elderly population, it has been shown to decrease post-operative cognitive dysfunction.
0:34
It is ideal for children and elderly patients. There are also other patient benefits, not just psychological ones. These include less cross-infection and early mobilisation, reducing the risk of deep vein thrombosis. Staff are easier to obtain due to social hours, and there are increased economic benefits to the trust as there can be an increased efficiency in a well-run day-case service reducing waiting
1:04
lists. Are there any disadvantages that you can think of? It is important to assess and have robust guidelines for the selection of patients for day-case surgery. There is also inherently a limitation of some anesthetic techniques such as epidural or indwelling catheter techniques for analgesia postoperatively.

There also has to be facility for admission of a proportion of patients due to intra and postoperative complications. What surgery can be performed as day-case and what are the important considerations? There has to be consideration as to which surgery is appropriate to be performed as day-case. It is important to make sure that any surgery can be done safely as a day-case without compromising patient care.
1:59
The complication rate should be low with minimal blood loss. There must be adequate control of pain, nausea and vomiting, and the patients must be able to eat and drink and mobilize within a reasonable time frame after the surgery is complete. So how are you going to select patients to have day-case surgery? There are social and medical factors that affect whether a patient can have day-case surgery or not.
2:29
Social factors include willingness on the patient's behalf, easy access to a phone and transport back to the hospital. There should be a responsible adult who can stay with the patient for at least the first 24 hours postoperatively and the conditions at home should be compatible with postoperative care, with adequate toilet and bathroom facilities. There should be no more than a 60 minute travelling time back to the hospital should complications
2:59
occur. Patients should have a telephone number to contact in case of emergencies and be discharged with an advice sheet explaining what to do in case of complications. Social factors are more difficult to quantify and in many circumstances clinical judgment needs to be employed. In general, patients should be relatively fit and well, mobile and not morbidly obese.
3:29
Patients should be selected according to their physiological status and not their chronological age. Some patients who fall outside guidelines may particularly benefit and should be discussed on a case-by-case basis, for example, people with stable diabetes. Obesity is not an absolute contraindication for day-case surgery, but there are problems with this population.

What are the problems of treating obese patients in the day-case setting? There needs to be appropriate resources and facilities for the management of obese patients, and specialist equipment and staff may not be available. There is an increased risk of respiratory compromise in the obese population, and late complications may develop more frequently, and therefore a longer hospital stay may be appropriate in some circumstances.
4:28
Obidly obese patients may also provide increased technical challenge to surgeons. Body Mass Index, though not the ideal tool for assessing these patients, does give pre-assessment nurses a guide. Our day-case unit has a body Mass Index cutoff of 35 for all procedures, as there is not the appropriate facility available. However, in many units there is no specific limit set, and cases can be assessed on an

Other general factors to consider include no previous problems with surgery or anesthesia, and no significant family history of anesthetic complications. The type of surgery needs to be taken into account, and the likelihood of significant bleeding, and or prolonged and difficult surgery.

What other factors are there that need to be considered when selecting patients for day-case surgery? Cardiovascular history should be explored, with particular attention being paid to exercise tolerance and reserve. If there are signs of heart failure, such as ankle edema and disnear, then these patients are not appropriate for day-case surgery.

Type attention is controversial and should be controlled preoperatively in a day-case surgery setting for an elective procedure. What are the important principles of anaesthetic management for day-case surgery? It is important to perform safe and effective anaesthesia with rapid recovery and few complications. The patients need to recover rapidly, have no cardiovascular or respiratory effects, and be awakened or orientated. They should not have nausea or vomiting and be able to drink fluids. Anti-emetics and the avoidance of nitrous oxide or volatile anaesthetic agents may be vital
8:59
in certain circumstances. Adequat analgesia is vital and opiate sparing techniques are important. Local anaesthetic techniques and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs should be used if appropriate. When should patients be discharged and what criteria should they meet? As mentioned before, patients should be cardiovascular stable. There should not be excessive blood loss.

There should be adequate respiratory parameters with saturations maintained on room air. Pain and nausea and vomiting should be controlled. Any motorblock from a regional technique should have worn off and there should be no impairment of bladder function. Models should be able to mobilize and have adequate social care at home, including access to a phone, bathroom and a responsible adults are care for them for 24 hours.

Information should be given on discharge with a telephone number and advice as to what to do if complications arise. Why do patients get admitted overnight following day case surgery? There are many reasons why patients get admitted following day surgery. There are surgical reasons such as bleeding or more extensive procedure being performed. There are anaesthetic reasons such as pain, cardiovascular or respiratory instability

and nausea and vomiting. If any of the discharge criteria are not achieved, such as being unable to pass urine, then the patient should also be admitted. The commonest reasons for admission are inadequate pain relief and nausea and vomiting.

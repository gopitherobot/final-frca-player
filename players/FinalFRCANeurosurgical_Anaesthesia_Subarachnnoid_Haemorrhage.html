Subarachnoid hemorrhage. A 55-year-old woman presents with sudden onset headache in his drowsy and confused in the emergency department. CT reveals a subarachnoid hemorrhage likely to be from the left anterior cerebral artery aneurysm. You are asked to necessitate the patient for angiographic coiling three days after this presentation.
Q1
What are the important features of the pre-operative assessment for this patient?
A
  • Essentially normal neuronousisia with potential for catastrophic problems.
  • This is a procedure generally done in a physiologically stable patient to prevent a more catastrophic
  • second bleed. In contrast to the open approach to an aneurysm or bleed, the cranium remains
  • closed so that hemorrhage during the procedure is more dangerous.
  • At the pre-operative visit, I would ensure that the patient is not hypotherlemic and
  • that the systolic blood pressure is controlled and is between 120 and 150mm of mercury.
  • I would check for evidence of cardiac ischemia by requesting an electrocardiogram and I'd
  • examine the patient for signs of pulmonary edema. Nymodopene should have been started
  • as prophylaxis against cerebral vasospasm so I'd check that this had been done.
  • I'd also do a brief neurological assessment to both make sure that the clinical grade
  • of the hemorrhage had not deteriorated and that there were no signs of active vasospasm.
  • I would also check for concurrent medical problems that may complicate the anaesthetic,
  • particularly pre-existing hypertension, which is common in those with subarachnoid hemorrhage.

Q4
How would you manage this patient postoperatively? How and where?
A
  • The important points in postoperative management are maintenance of a systolic blood pressure
  • between 140 and 160 millimetres of mercury and regular neurological assessment looking
  • in particular for the occurrence of vasospasm. Some centres will monitor all patients on a
  • neurosurgical high dependency unit during the immediate postoperative period. In any
  • case I would watch out for the complications of subarachnoid hemorrhage.
Q5
Which complications would you be mindful of during the procedure? And how might it affect your anesthetic?
A
  • Intracranial hemorrhage is a possibility during the procedure and may be seen radiologically
  • or may only be noticed when its physiological consequences are seen such as cushing sign
  • or cardiac arrhythmias. If it does occur a decision needs to be made as to whether the
  • procedure should be continued or whether the patient should be moved to the neurosurgical
  • intensive care or whether they need an urgent craniotomy. Ischemia and thrombomboembolic
  • phenomena can occur due to vessel obstruction by the catheter or an injectate, though there
  • is unlikely to be any obvious sign of this until the patient is woken.
Q6
And what complications may occur after the procedure?
A
  • Re-bleeding after subarachnoid hemorrhage occurs in about 20% of patients within two
  • weeks of presentation and 50% within six months, though if the aneurysm has been secured
  • this is dramatically reduced. Raised intracranial pressure can occur in the absence of a further
  • bleed and may be due to impairment of CSF flow or a deem in the tissue surrounding
  • a hematoma. Seasures are also a potential problem both at the time of the bleed and
  • in the ensuing weeks and anti-epileptic medication may be needed lifelong.
  • Groin hematoma can also occur and can be worsened by the use of anticoagulants and it may
  • need surgical repair of the vessel defect.

A

  • and the world federation of neurosurgeon score and radiological such as the Fischer score.
  • Pick one of them and outline it for me.
  • The world federation of neurosurgeon score uses the Glasgow Coma score and the present
  • saw absence of a focal neurological deficit. So GCS-15 is grade 1.
  • GCS-13 to 14 with no deficit is grade 2. GCS-13 to 14 with neurological deficit is grade
  • 3. GCS-7 to 12 is grade 4 and GCS less than 7 is grade 5. The score relates to mortality
  • so that grade 1 patients have only a few percent mortality rate whereas those with grade
  • 5 have nearly 100 percent mortality.
Q8
What can you tell me about thesis bazaam?
A
  • That's a potential consequence of subarachnoid hemorrhage in which arterial vasoconstriction
  • occurs as a result of irritation by subarachnoid blood and is generally more likely to happen
  • in those with higher grade hemorrhage and thus more blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • If it happens it usually doesn't start until three days after the bleed and rarely starts
  • more than two weeks afterwards.
  • The consequences of vasocasm for the patient can include focal neurological deficit or a more
  • generalized picture with reduced level of consciousness.

A

  • of the medical management.
  • This involves induced hypertension of 160 to 200 mm of mercury systolic blood pressure

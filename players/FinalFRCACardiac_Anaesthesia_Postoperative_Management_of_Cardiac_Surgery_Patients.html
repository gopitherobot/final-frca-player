Q5
How would you manage these patients fluid balance?
A
  • Large fluid shifts take place during cardiopulmonary bypass.
  • This leads to an increase in extracellular fluid
  • due to the volume of priming solution given and capillary leakage.
  • Himo dilution occurs.
  • The extracellular fluid load increases with the duration of bypass.
Q6
However, there may be co-existent relative intravascular hypotherlemia.
A
  • This can be worsened by post-operative phaseodilitation
  • with re-warming, ongoing blood loss and a marked diuresis post-bypass.
  • Post-operative fluid replacement therapy is usually a combination of colloid and crystaloid fluids
  • as well as blood products.
  • I would be guided by urinoutput, blood pressure, heart rate and CVP trends
  • as well as blood gas studies whilst monitoring ongoing losses.
  • Otherwise, healthy patients tend to tolerate hemoglobin levels down to 8 grams per litre.
  • The associated reduced viscosity has been shown to improve coronary perfusion
  • as well as cardiac output by reducing left ventricular work.
  • Tell us about problems for electrolyte disturbance as after cardiac surgery.
  • In post-operative cardiac patients, I am mainly concerned about electrolytes like potassium and magnesium
  • since derangement of these can predispose patients to arrhythmias.
  • Potassium tends to be low with increased renal excretion and preoperative use of diuretics.
  • Severe hypocholemia may present as ECG changes like ST depression, T-wave inversion and U-waves.
  • Hypocholemia can predispose to tachy arrhythmias.
  • I would aim to keep serum potassium in the normal range between 4.5 to 5 millimoles per litre
  • and replace it by infusing potassium chloride centrally.
  • Hypocholemia can also occur and in severe cases presents as peaked T-waves,
  • atroventricular block and broadening QRS complexes.
  • Treatment of severe hypercholemia is urgent with calcium chloride, 10 millimoles intravenously
  • and 50 mills of 50% dextrose with 10 units of act rapid added.
  • Hypocholemia is a common problem after cardiopulmonary bypass.
  • It can lead to tachy arrhythmias, decreased cardiac output and prolonged ventilatory support.
  • It should be replaced by slow intravenous infusion of 2 grams of magnesium sulfate.
Q7
How would you manage patients acid-based balance?
A
  • Disturbances in acid-based balance are also common after cardiopulmonary bypass.
  • Careful adjustment of mechanical ventilation is necessary to correct any respiratory component.
  • Metabolic disturbances need treating according to the underlying cause.
  • A combination of fluid replacement therapy, ionotropes and vasodilators is used to optimize tissue perfusion and cardiac output.
  • Hyperglycemia should be corrected and renal impairment managed appropriately.
  • Careful monitoring for other causes such as sepsis is crucial.
Q8
How would you manage postoperative arrhythmias?
A
  • Arhythmias are a common problem after cardiac surgery and require prompt and effective treatment.
  • There is a multitude of possible underlying causes which should be corrected before pharmacological treatment is commenced.
  • Possible reasons for postoperative arrhythmias are myocardial ischemia, hypoxia, hypercarbure and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Drugs can also precipitate rhythm disturbances.
  • Preexisting arrhythmias or pacemaker failure may contribute.
  • There is also a chance of surgical damage to the conducting system.
  • Treatment of arrhythmias can be by physical or pharmacological methods.
Q9
Can you tell us more about physical methods please?
A
  • The physical method of DC-cardiaversion is used to convert superventricular tachycardias, ventricular tachycardias and atrial fibrillation back to sinus rhythm.
  • A DC shock is applied via the fibrillator paddles to the chest.
  • The timing of shock application is synchronized with the R wave on the ECG.
  • The shock energy delivered depends on the underlying rhythm and the type of defibrillator used.
  • Cardiac pacing is another physical method to control arrhythmias.
  • It can be used in the treatment of bradiarrhythmias as well as tachyarrhythmias.
  • Many patients will leave theatre with temporary pacing wires in situ to control transient postoperative rhythm problems.
  • If possible, the sequential nature of atrial and ventricular contraction is maintained.
  • That improves ventricular filling. This can be achieved by atrial or sequential pacing.
Q10
What do you know about drug treatment of arrhythmias?
A
  • Pharmacological methods may sometimes involve the discontinuation of pro-rhythmic agents in the first place.
  • Examples are dopamine or adrenaline.
  • Thereafter, anti-rhythmic drugs can be considered.
  • In the treatment of tachyarrhythmias, the clinical picture needs to be considered.
  • DC-cardiaversion is the method of choice in superventricular and ventricular tachycardias with hemodynamic compromise.
  • In uncompromised patients, amyode-rone is the drug of choice for many tachyarrhythmias.
  • In atrial fibrillation and flutter, the duration of the arrhythmiar is also important.
  • If it has been present for more than 48 hours, the patient should be fully anticoagulated before any cardiac version is attempted.
  • Didoxin or beta blockers can be used for rate control.
  • In superventricular narrow complex tachycardias without compromise, vagal maneuvers such as carotid sinus massage can sometimes be successful in terminating the abnormal rhythm.
  • Failing that, a denazine can be used.
  • Other effective drug treatments include amyode-rone, calcium channel blockers or beta blockers.
  • Uncompromised ventricular tachycardias can be treated with amyode-rone or lignacane.
  • Bradiarhythmias can be treated with cardiac pacing, either internal or external, atropine or isoprenoline.
  • In complex or treatment resistant arrhythmias, expert help from a cardiologist should be sought early.
Q11
How would you manage low cardiac output after cardiac surgery?
A
  • Cardiac output can be low because of pre-existing ischemic damage or because of mycardial stunning after the use of cardiopulmonary bypass.
  • Firstly, I would have to ensure adequate filling guided mainly by urine output and central venous pressures.
  • If response to fluid therapy alone is unsatisfactory, I would like to get an estimate of afterload and cardiac performance.
  • This can be done by transosophageal Doppler or picomegiments.
  • The use of a more invasive pulmonary artery catheter is less common nowadays.
  • They were not shown to improve outcomes but have higher complication rates than other less invasive techniques.
  • I would then choose an appropriate ionotropic or vasopressor agent to augment cardiac performance.
  • Some patients may need intraaortic bloom pumps or other assist devices.
Q12
Can you tell us more about ionotropic agents?
A
  • Dopamine or dobutamine are often chosen as the first-line ionotropic agent.
  • Noradrenlin tends to be the first-line vasopressor agent.
  • Dopamine is a noradrenlin precursor and acts as an agonist at alpha, beta and doper-minergic receptors.
  • It causes mesenteric and renal vasodilitation via doper-minergic receptors at low adoses.
  • Its use at those doses for renal protection has been abandoned.
  • At higher doses, sympathetic effects predominate with positive coronatropia and ionotropia via beta adrenusectors.
  • It also results in vasoconstriction via alpha adrenusectors.
  • Dubutamine is a synthetic beta agonist with mainly beta-1 cardiac effects.
  • It therefore causes positive ionotropia but also a marked tachycardia which can be a limiting factor for its use.
  • Noradrenlin tends to be the first-line vasopressor because of its predominant alpha effects.
  • It also has limited ionotropic activity via cardiac beta-1 receptors.
  • Adrenalin is a potent ionotrop with both alpha and beta adrenergic effects.
  • Beta-1 effects are responsible for an increase in heart rate and myocardial contractility.
  • It also acts on alpha receptors and thereby causes peripheral vasoconstriction.
  • Bronco-dilitation is caused via beta-2 effects.
Q13
What second-line ionotropes do you know about?
A
  • Phosphodiesterase-3 inhibitors such as milrenone or inoximone act by an increase in cyclic AMP.
  • These agents decrease intracellular cyclic AMP degradation via the Phosphodiesterase-3 enzyme.
  • They therefore increase contractility whilst also causing peripheral vasodilitation.
  • Dopexamine is a dopamine analogue with effects on dopamine-ergic and beta-2 receptors but no alpha effects.
  • It is therefore an INO dilator which increases myocardial contractility but decreases systemic vascular resistance.
  • It also dilates the splanknic circulation.
  • Dejuoxin is also used for its ionotropic properties and acts via an increase in intracellular calcium.
Q14
How would you go about managing renal impairment after cardiac surgery?
A
  • Cardiopulmonary bypass decreases renal blood flow and glomerular filtration rate by about 30%.
  • Renal failure is a major contributing factor in perioperative morbidity and mortality and appropriate management of impaired renal function is crucial.
  • In the first instance it is important to maintain renal perfusion pressure throughout the perioperative period.
  • Patients with comorbidities such as pre-existing renal disease, hyper-attention or diabetes will need higher mean arterial pressures than those with no other medical problems.
  • Nephrotoxic drugs such as non-steroidols or amino glycocides should be avoided if possible or doses adjusted carefully according to plasma levels.
  • Fluid management is very important to eliminate any pre-renal component of renal impairment.
  • In established renal failure, loop diuretics such as frusomide are often used to turn oliguric into polyuric renal failure but this does not change the course of the disease.
  • Renal replacement therapy is sometimes necessary to bridge the time until recovery of renal function.
  • Continuous venovinas hemofiltration is usually the method of choice.
  • This should be considered if there is prolonged anuria, fluid overload, a significant metabolic acidosis or accumulation of electrolytes or metabolites.
Q15
What sort of problems might you encounter when trying to wean the patient off mechanical ventilation?
A
  • Ideally, in straightforward cases, the period of mechanical ventilation postoperatively should be kept to a minimum.
  • Cidation and ventilation after cardiac surgery allows for a period of stability.
  • During that time, physiological parameters such as temperature, acid base and fluid balance and electrolytes are corrected as well as significant ongoing bleeding excluded.
  • Once all of these parameters are satisfactory, sedation and ventilation are weaned and the patient is allowed to wake up.
  • Problems with weaning may occur for a variety of reasons.
  • It can be difficult in patients with pre-existing medical problems such as pulmonary disease, pneumonia, sepsis from other sources or left ventricular failure.
  • Surgical complications can also be responsible for instance frenic nerve damage, pneumothorax or plural effusions.
  • The use of cardiopulmonary bypass itself can also contribute to difficulties by causing neurological deficits or acute lung injury.
  • If ventilation is anticipated to be prolonged, many centres will opt for early elective tracheostomy to aid weaning.
Q16
Can it us more about acute lung injury after cardiopulmonary bypass?
A
  • Cardiopulmonary bypass invariably causes a degree of acute lung injury. This is usually mild and clinically insignificant.
  • It is thought to be due to an inflammatory response caused by the exposure of circulating blood to the non-physiological surface of the bypass circuit.
  • About 2% of people will develop acute respiratory distress syndrome which will significantly delay weaning and post-operative recovery.
  • Management is as for ARDS from other causes, with pressure controlled ventilation at low tidal volumes of 6-8 mils per kilo.
  • Peak pressures should be limited to less than 35 centimetres of water.
  • The use of peep is essential and FIO2 should be kept at a minimal level required to achieve satisfactory oxygenation.
  • Permissive hypercapnea may have to be tolerated with these ventilation strategies.
  • Mortality associated with cardiopulmonary bypass-related ARDS is significantly lower than that from sepsis or other major causes of ARDS.

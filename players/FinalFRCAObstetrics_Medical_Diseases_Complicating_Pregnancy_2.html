Medical disease complicating pregnancy, too. You're called to the obstetric ward urgently to see a woman who just had a child delivery but is now bleeding significantly.
Q1
What is your immediate management?
A
  • I would ask a member of staff to call for senior help
  • and alert the hematology and portoring services.
  • It is vital to work in a team with the midwives and obstetricians.
  • Having established a brief history from the midwifery staff,
  • the patient's notes and the obstetricians, I would need to establish how much blood
  • had been lost, how much fluid had already been given, and assess the current
  • hemodynamic status.
  • I would give a hundred percent oxygen, ensure that the airway was patent and
  • establish verbal communication with the patient.
  • The blood pressure, pulse rate and capillary refill would need to be assessed
  • large bore intravenous cannula inserted for further volume resuscitation.
  • I would take blood for a full blood count, year-ear and electrolytes,
  • coagulation studies and cross-match six units of blood.
  • I would initially resuscitate the woman with up to two litres of intravenous
  • crystalloid and then consider using blood.
  • Ideally I would use warmed fluids.
  • I would be in constant communication with the rest of the team to
  • ascertain the extent of the bleeding and the planned treatment.
  • This might include manual stimulation of an atonic uterus, placement of a
  • urinary catheter and a drug such as sintosinone, ergo metrin and carbaprost.
  • The obstetricians diagnose uterine atonine retained products of conception.
  • They are keen to move to theatre and surgically intervene.
  • The woman continues to bleed.
Q2
What are your concerns?
A
  • I would appreciate the urgency of the situation and attempt to move the woman quickly.
  • I would want to continue fluid resuscitation, consider invasive monitoring and have the woman
  • catheterised in theatre.
  • It might be necessary to transfuse fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate and platelets
  • depending on the blood transfusion requirements.
  • Cell salvage and attention to normal fermia would also be considerations.
  • In order to provide anesthesia for removal of the products of conception,
  • the options are a general or regional anesthetic technique.
  • My choice would depend upon the urgency,
  • hemodynamic stability of the patient and potential for coagulopathy.
Q3
What surgical interventions might be considered?
A
  • The obstetricians might only need to manually remove the placenta and stimulate
  • the uterus for the bleeding to subside.
  • If this fails, further options include uterine packing,
  • uterine and internal iliac artery ligation,
  • b-link suture and finally a hysterectomy.
  • In some units, radiological embolisation of pelvic vessels may be available.
  • On your postnatal ward round, you identify a woman who complains of a headache.
  • She had an epidural for labour and an un-event for the journal delivery 24 hours ago.
Q4
What are the possible causes of her headache and how much you differentiate between them?
A
  • There are many causes for a postnatal headache,
  • and it is important to take a thorough history and examine the patient
  • in order to ascertain the possible diagnosis.
  • Investigations would include the white cell count and CRP to exclude sepsis as a cause.
  • The headache might be due to a neurological condition,
  • generalised condition or secondary to an anaesthetic intervention.
  • Neurological conditions include simple headache,
  • migraine, meningitis, benign intercranial hypertension,
  • cortical vein thrombosis and intracranial bleed and tumour.
  • Generalised conditions include preeclampsia, dehydration and stress.
  • With regards to anaesthetic intervention,
  • the woman may have developed a post-dueral puncher headache.
  • The classical features of a post-dueral puncher headache include an onset
  • within 72 hours of neuraxial blockade with a severe frontal or occipital headache.
  • This is worse when sitting, moving suddenly, coughing and straining,
  • and is relieved when supine.
  • Next stiffness may occur as well as photophobia,
Q5
which may mimic raised intracranial pressure.
A
  • Rarely, cranial nerve pauses and even subdueral or intracranial hemorrhage have been reported.
Q6
How would you treat suspect a post-dueral puncher headache?
A
  • I would discuss the possible diagnosis with the woman
  • and advise that she should try and remain well hydrated
  • and take oral anaergetia as tolerated.
  • I would like to exclude some of the serological markers of sepsis
  • by sending off bloods for white cell count and would also monitor her temperature.
  • If the headache persisted, I would consider performing an epidural blood patch.
  • The optimal timing for a blood patch is contentious.
  • I would aim to perform the technique within one to two days of the headache onset
  • and if conservative measures had failed.
  • Prophylectic blood patching for patients with recognised ural tap is controversial
  • as it may be unnecessary, less effective and poses a potential infection risk.
Q7
What is a success rate with an epidural blood patch?
A
  • Most patients will recover completely after an epidural blood patch
  • with a success rate of up to 80% rising to 90% after a second blood patch.
  • The most recent CMAC report, 2003-2005, identified six maternal deaths
  • and much anaesthesia directly contributed.
Q8
Can you describe these?
A
  • Three patients were obese with post-operative respiratory complications.
  • Of these patients, one had an emergency removal of ectopic pregnancy
  • and respiratory distress following extubation with failed re-intubation.
  • One received a second dose of opiate prior to extubation
  • and had respiratory failure in recovery
  • and the third had a fatal asthma attack after a cesarean section performed under regional anaesthesia.
  • The fourth patient received an overdose of intravenous bupivocane.
  • The fifth patient developed a hemophorex after attempted central line insertion
  • for preeclampsia and help.
  • Finally, the sixth patient died two weeks after childbirth
  • whilst having a general anaesthetic to have a septic focus-drain from a kidney.
  • The exact cause of her death was unknown.
Q9
What lessons can be learned from these cases?
A
  • The CMAC report highlighted the need for trained experienced recovery staff
  • and an aesthetic being readily available at all times for this group of patients.
  • There were some issues regarding the experience of trainees and the accessibility of senior support.
  • The report reminded clinicians to consider the respiratory implications of a pez excavator
  • but accepted that the use of ultrasound might not avoid all complications of central line insertion.
  • Finally, the report identified factors that may contribute to the inadvertent intravenous infusion of local anaesthetic drugs,
  • such as the location of storage of these drugs in relation to standard infusion bags.
  • Staff also needed to be aware of where the intra-lippid 20% is located for the treatment of severe local anaesthetic toxicity.
Q10
Did the report make any suggestions regarding the 31 deaths and much anaesthesia contributors?
A
  • Yes, the report identified a failure to recognise serious illness and poor management of hemorrhage,
  • sepsis, preeclampsia and obese women.
Q11
What do the reports say about the challenges now facing obstetrics?
A
  • The CMAC report acknowledged the increase in migrant population as an increasing challenge
  • since women are presenting late with complicated pregnancies, serious underlying medical conditions,
  • and poor general health. Furthermore, vulnerable women with socially complex lives are less likely to seek
  • antenatal care and stay in contact with maternity services.
  • More than 15% of all women who died from direct or indirect causes were morbidly obese.
  • Finally, as old women are now presenting, they have more chronic health problems.
Q12
What were the principal direct causes of death?
A
  • These included thromboembolism, amniotic fluid embolism, sepsis, preeclampsia, hemorrhage, anaesthesia and direct uterine trauma.
  • The CMAC report suggested a national early warning school for identifying women who are unwell.
Q13
What indices they suggest might be measured?
A
  • The report suggested both general and specific indices.
  • The obstetrics specific indices were Lockier, Protein-Urea and Lycor,
  • whilst the general indices included respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, temperature, heart rate, blood pressure,
  • urine output, conscious level and pain score.

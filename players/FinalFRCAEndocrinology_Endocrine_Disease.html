Endocrine disease. What are the presenting features of a pituitary tumour? Pituitary tumours comprise approximately 10-15% of all intracranial tumours. The vast majority are benign adenomas and arise from the anterior portion of the gland. Tumours can actively secrete hormones, most commonly prolactin produced by 30% of tumours, growth hormone produced by 20% and ACTH by approximately 15%.
0:37
Conversely, there can be decreased or absent hormone secretion resulting in the clinical picture of hypopituitarism. Pituitary tumours can produce localized or generalized pressure effects. Locally, pressure may be exerted on the optic chiasm, producing a visual field defect, classically by temporal hemionopia. Generalized signs and symptoms include vomiting, headache, papal edema and cranial nerve pauses. Large adenomas can occasionally interfere with the circulation of cerebrospinal fluid, producing hydrochephalus
1:19
and as a consequence raised intracranial pressure. A proportion of asymptomatic pituitary lesions are incidentally detected during imaging for another indication. What is acromegaly? Acromegaly is a rare endocrine syndrome caused by excess production of growth hormone by the anterior pituitary gland. The annual incidence is 6 to 8 cases per million population. Symptoms and signs result mainly from the overgrowth of soft tissue structures.
1:56
Changes are gradual and include an increase in skull size, course and oily skin, prominent superorbital ridge and large hands and feet. What potential anaesthetic issues is acromegaly present? Acromegaly can pose a problem for the anesthetist, both in terms of the effects of the disease on the airway and also the systemic effects. Preoperatively, any history suggestive of obstructive sleep apnea should be thoroughly explored. This includes a history of snoring, disturbed sleep and daytime tiredness.
2:37
The presence of these features suggests the potential for a difficult airway and the risk of postoperative airway obstruction. Airway problems arise due to the hypertrophy of the soft tissues of the upper respiratory tract, including the tongue. A quarter of patients have enlargement of the thyroid gland which can exacerbate airway compromise. Despite enlargement of the mandible and other facial structures in acromegalyc patients, bag mask ventilation can usually be achieved without difficulty. A large-sized face mask and a farangial airway may be helpful.
3:20
Obtaining a good view at larynxoscopy may be difficult due to an enlarged tongue and epiglottis. A long blade may improve the situation. Passage of an endotracheal tube can be impeded by vocal cord thickening and a reduction in the size of the laryngeal opening. Thura airway assessment in the preoperative period is essential and indirect larynxoscopy may add useful information. A fiber optic technique is appropriate in those cases where difficulty is anticipated. The systemic effects of acromegaly are predominantly cardiac and endocrine.

Acromegaly is an important cause of secondary hypertension and can also result in a schemic heart disease, cardiomyopathy and congestive cardiac failure. A preoperative cardiac assessment in the form of a full history, examination and electrocardiogram should be carried out. Patients with evidence of schemic heart disease or left ventricular failure should be further investigated with an echocardiogram and angiogram if deemed appropriate. Treatment of these conditions should be optimised prior to an elective surgical procedure. The endocrine features are glucose intolerance, diabetes mellitus and thyroid abnormalities. Approximately one quarter of patients with acromegaly have diabetes and will require management of their blood sugars in the perioperative period.

What are the importance clinical features of cushing's disease? Cushing's disease refers to the condition resulting from excess production of adrenal corticotrophic hormone by a pituitary adenoma. It is clinically difficult to distinguish from other causes of high concentrations of cortisol, such as following treatment with corticosteroids, ectopic ACTH production in some near plastic conditions and excess cortisol production by an adrenal tumor. The above conditions are collectively referred to as cushing's syndrome. Cushing's disease is rare and 80% of affected individuals are female. Cushing's disease produces effects in many systems, many of which are of major interest to the anesthetist. Around 80% of individuals are hyper-attensive and this can prove resistant to pharmacological treatment.

Long-standing hyper-attention can result in left ventricular hypertrophy and impairment. Abesity, sleep apnea and gastroesophageal reflux are recognised complications of the condition with implications for airway management. There can also be electrolyte disturbances in the form of hyper-nutremia and hypocholemia and impaired glucose tolerance or Frank diabetes mellitus. What are the important considerations for surgery to remove a pituitary lesion? The principles of anesthesia for surgery to a pituitary lesion are common to those for any neurosurgical procedure. These are to maintain hemodynamic stability, ensure adequate cerebral perfusion, prevent rapid alterations in intra-cranial pressure and provide a stable state of anesthesia which can be rapidly reversed at the end of surgery to allow early neurosurgical assessment.
7:05
In addition, there are some specific issues to consider. Preoperative assessment of the patient entails a full history, examination and appropriate investigations to ascertain any pathological effects of the tumor. As explained before, these would include syndromes of hormone overproduction, symptoms due to raised intra-cranial pressure or problems due to lack of production of pituitary hormones. The patient's past medical and anesthetic history should be fully explored. The preoperative assessment must identify and investigate any comorbidities either arising because of the tumor or that are unrelated. The patient should be optimised in terms of cardiovascular and respiratory function, management of diabetes and hormone replacement if indicated.
8:04
Induction of anesthesia proceeds as for a standard neurosurgical case. Some differences in anesthetic technique may arise as a result of the surgical approach which is to be used. Exposure of the pituitary gland and surrounding structures can be achieved via a transphanoidal approach whereby an incision is made in the nasal septum or by a frontal craniotomy. For a transphanoidal approach, the use of a throat pack prevents contamination of the airway with blood and debris. A vasoconstrictor agent can be applied to the nasal mucuser to reduce bleeding. The patient is positioned in a head-up position in theatre. Acromagallic patients are particularly prone to nerve compression syndromes so special care should be taken with positioning and padding.
8:58
Maintenance of anesthesia can be with either an inhalational or intravenous technique. The aim is to provide stability throughout the surgery with a rapid wake up at the end of the procedure. Adequate analgesia must be provided prior to emergence. Perioperative complications related to pituitary surgery are uncommon. However, the issues to be aware of are hemorrhage, damage to the optic chiasm with consequent visual field defects, cerebral spinal fluid leak and postoperative pan-hypoperiturism.

Postoperative airway complications are unusual but patients with preoperative airway issues such as obstructive sleep apnea should be monitored in a high dependency environment for the first 24 hours. You are asked to assess a 46-year-old lady for an abdominal hysterectomy. She has a diagnosis of Adeson's disease. What do you understand about this condition? Adeson's disease is a clinical syndrome resulting from the destruction of the adrenal cortex. This leads to a deficiency of the adrenal steroid hormones. The majority of cases seen in the UK are due to an autoimmune process and there is an association between Adeson's disease and other autoimmune conditions such as panicious anemia, vitiligo and thyroid disorders.
10:31
Alternative causes include infiltration of the adrenal glands by malignancy or infection, infarction due to a compromised blood supply and hemorrhage into the adrenal tissue. The presenting features of chronic cases are commonly non-specific and include weakness, fatigue, abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and weight loss. Signs include postural hypotension and hyperpigmentation of skin creases. Acute presentations are also seen during which the patient is typically profoundly unwell with hypotension, vomiting and weakness. Characteristic by a chemical abnormalities are seen, namely hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, hypercalcemia and hypoglycemia. How is Adeson's disease treated?
11:31
Patients require a replacement of the adrenal steroid hormones. This is given orally in the form of hydrocortezone and fluid recordazone. Patients should be cancelled regarding the importance of not emitting doses. Intramuscular supplementation may be needed in the event of significant gastrointestinal upset. Adesonian crisis is managed with an airway breathing and circulation approach. Admission to a critical care area is likely to be necessary for ventilatory support, intravenous steroid supplementation, fluid resuscitation and correction of serum electrolytes and glucose. What are the principles of a perioperative management of patients with Adeson's disease?

Regular medication should be continued up to the time of surgery. An intravenous dose of 25 mg of hydrocortezone should be given at the time of induction of anesthesia. Following minus surgery, whether patient is expected to resume oral intake promptly in the post-operative period, the normal medication regime can be reestablished. After more invasive surgical procedures, intravenous steroid supplementation of 50 mg of hydrocortezone at six hourly intervals is appropriate until oral intake is restored. Blood glucose monitoring is important, as is the daily measurement of urea and electrolytes. A 35-year-old male is to undergo surgical resection of a feocromocytoma. What are the principles of his pre-operative optimization? Feocromocytoma is a rare tumor derived from chromophine cells. 90% of tumors are located in the adrenal medulla whilst the remainder occur at other sites in the sympathetic nervous system. Most tumors are benign.

These tumors can arise as an isolated problem or as part of a condition known as multiple endocrine near pleasure. In this condition, medullary carcinoma of the thyroid, parathyroid adenomas, or marphinoid characteristics, coexist with the feocromocytoma depending on the precise sub-tight of multi-endocrine near pleasure. Feocromocytomas secrete cataclylamines and the systemic effects of these substances lead to the symptoms and signs of the condition. Tumors which produce mainly nor epinephrine result in severe hyper-attention which is resistant to pharmacological treatment. Production of epinephrine and dopamine produce symptoms of episodic palpitations, sweating and anxiety. A mixed pattern of cataclylamine secretion and physiological effects often occurs.
14:35
Although surgical resection of the tumor is the definitive treatment for feocromocytoma, the patient requires a period of optimization. This should ideally be undertaken in a centre familiar with dealing with these cases. Optimization is twofold. Firstly, assessment of any end organ damage to the heart or kidneys, and secondly, pharmacological blockade of the sympathetic overactivity. The aim of treatment is to reduce blood pressure and heart rate, prevent tachyridis rhythmias, and oppose nor epinephrine-induced vasoconstriction permitting expansion of the intravascular volume. In this way, the patient's hemodynamic responses both to general anesthesia and surgical manipulation of the tumor are blunted. Sympathetic blockade is achieved with an alpha blocker as the first line agent.
15:37
Alpha blockade was traditionally undertaken with a long-acting, non-selective agent such as phenoxybenzamine. Limitations with using this agent include the finding that it commonly provokes a compensatory tachycardia and that its effects can persist into the post-operative period, producing unwanted hypotension. For these reasons, shorter acting alpha-1 selective agents such as prazocin or doxozocin are used as an alternative. If tachycardia is a problem after alpha blockade, addition of a beta blocker may be necessary. Beta blockade, in the absence of alpha blockade, can theoretically worsen hyper-tension by abolishing beta-2-mediated vasodilitation in skeletal muscle.
16:33
The adequacy of sympathetic blockade can be assessed by ambulatory monitoring demonstrating stability of both heart rate and blood pressure. A postural drop-in blood pressure is also used as a marker of adequate blockade. The patient has now been adequately investigated and optimised. What are the important factors to be considered in your intraoperative and postoperative management of his case? The aims of anesthesia in this case are to provide as much hemodynamic stability as possible with the ability to respond rapidly and appropriately to any cardiovascular changes as they occur. Invasive cardiovascular monitoring, with an arterial line and central venous catheter is mandatory in this case.
17:23
The stress response to surgical incision can be reduced by the use of an epidural or opiates given at high doses. If the latter approach is taken, this may have implications on the patient's respiratory function in the early postoperative period. Surgical manipulation of the tumor can cause significant catacletamine release with consequent instability in heart rate, rhythm or blood pressure. Short-acting intravenous agents such as phentolamine and alpha-blocker or libiterol are useful to control these episodes. Blood pressure usually settles over a period of minutes after the resection of the tumor. The circulation should be supported with generous administration of intravenous fluid.
18:16
Postoperatively, the patient requires ongoing cardiovascular monitoring in addition to management of any instability. This is best undertaken in a critical care environment. Administration of ionotropes or vasopressors may be needed to maintain an adequate blood pressure. In addition to management of cardiovascular parameters, attention must also be paid to maintenance of blood glucose, which may fall following the acute reduction in circulating catacletamine levels.

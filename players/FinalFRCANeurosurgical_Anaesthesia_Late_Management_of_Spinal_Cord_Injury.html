Late management of patients with spinal cord injury. You were asked to anesthetize a patient seven months after complete transection of the cord for the bridesmaid of an ischial tuberosity pressure saw. He gives a history of significant reflux. Please describe your anaesthetic management of the case. I will divide this into pre, peri and post-operative phases. The main anaesthetic issues specific to his spinal injury are the risk of hyperkalemia with succcemethonium secondary to proliferation of astyle-coiline receptors and also the risks of sympathetic dysreflexia. Most anaesthetists will not have direct experience of these patients. Therefore an awareness of the key issues should be sufficient for your answer. By structuring your answer and flagging up the key points at the beginning, your examiner is able to relax, already confident in your approach. Also, if time is short, you have already gained crucial marks.
Q1
What do you mean by the term dys or hyperreflexia?
A
  • This refers to a massive disordered autonomic response to stimuli below the level of the lesion.
  • In the awake patient, there can be headache, sweating and other sympathetic signs
  • such as hornas syndrome.
  • The most dangerous effect is hypertension, which may be severe and could lead to intra cranial hemorrhage.
  • It is more common with high spinal lesions and seems to be caused by both loss of descending inhibition
  • and altered neuronal connections within the distal spinal cord.
  • The most common triggers are from cordal root levels in particular bladder distension.
  • The patient may already be receiving prophylactic agents such as clonidine
  • to try and minimize cardiovascular instability.
  • This is a difficult one.
  • The pathophysiology of dysreflexia is tricky.
  • It should be enough that you are aware of it.
  • Please go on with your anaesthetic management.
  • Preoperatively, as well as the usual equipment and drug checks, I would clarify patient positioning with a surgeon.
  • Surgery may be conducted in the lateral or prone position.
  • I would review the notes looking for any operation since the injury,
  • in particular focusing on any airway difficulties,
  • whether the patient required a tracheostomy at the time of his injury,
  • ventilation difficulties, including the need for post-operative non-invasive or invasive ventilation,
  • and problems with dysreflexia.
  • I would take a full anaesthetic history and examine the patient, again focusing on these areas.
  • If he had spinal fixation, he may have limited neck movement.
  • Contractures may lead to difficulties with positioning, vascular access or surgical access.
  • In addition, I would ask him about muscular spasm, which could interfere with surgery.
  • You have mentioned he had a complete transection of the cord,
  • so I would expect him to have no sensation or motor power below that level.
  • I would clarify this with him and assess any requirement for analgesia.
  • I would also ask him about the symptoms of gastroesophageal reflux.
  • Most spinal cord injured patients have delayed gastric emptying,
  • but this does not usually lead to reflux.
  • I would prescribe antacid and pro-kinetic therapy.
  • With regards to the anaesthetic plan, after clarifying positioning and estimate of duration with a surgeon,
  • I would discuss with the patient the anaesthetic options.
  • These include sedation, width and without analgesia, central neural axial blockade and general anaesthesia.
  • If there were no symptoms and signs of autonomic dysreflexia and he had no sensation,
  • then the simplest approach would be for him to remain awake for the procedure.
Q2
And what if there was a history of dysreflexia?
A
  • If there was a history, or if the risk was unknown, I would suggest central neural axial blockade with a spinal anaesthetic to abolish the risk.
  • Your patient declined central neural axial blockade and asks to have a general.
  • In that case, I would first seek to clarify his concerns and any misconceptions about spinal anaesthesia,
  • for example if he fears worsening of his lesion.
  • Or whether he simply wishes to be asleep and light sedation might be sufficient.
  • If he still insisted on general anaesthesia, I would seek senior anaesthetic advice and explain the increased risk of aspiration with all of its consequences.
  • If consensus was that a general anaesthetic was reasonable, I would go ahead and prepare for that.
  • Given his C5 lesion, unless there was good evidence of previous and eventful operations without any respiratory complications, I would request a high dependency bed for his post-operative care.
Q3
And how would you ennecetize?
A
  • I will consider induction, maintenance, and then monitoring into position.
  • Induction first.
  • With trained assistance and all my usual checks, I would establish large bore intravenous access, which may be difficult with atrophic skin and poor blood flow.
  • I would connect a giving set with crystalloid, the patient may have a reduced blood volume depending on pre-optimization, so I would consider a fluid preload.
  • I would also have prepared an alpha agonist, such as metramanol, anticipating exaggerated hypotension on induction of anaesthesia.
  • As with any induction, I would have atropine in a pre-drawn syringe.
  • If there were any concerns regarding his airway, I would consider an awake fibre optic incubation.
  • Otherwise, I would carry out a modified rapid sequence induction with thio pentone and rockuronium as a rapid onset non-depolarizing alternative to succimethonium.
Q4
Why avoid succimethonium?
A
  • As I have mentioned, in spinal cord injury there is a proliferation of astyle-coline receptors throughout muscle, away from the motor end plate, presumably as a response to the sudden loss of efferent transmission.
  • The use of succimethonium can therefore lead to a massive depolarization with subsequent hyperkalemia and potentially arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.
Q5
Do you know how long the risk lasts?
A
  • I have read various estimates of the time period. Taking a cautious approach, I would avoid succimethonium from 48 hours after the initial event until 9 months later.
Q6
In all circumstances?
A
  • Every case would have to be assessed on a benefit-harm basis.
  • The potential for a hyperkalemic crisis within that time period is unquantifiable and is also treatable with calcium, insulin and so on.
  • It is possible to imagine a situation where an airway crisis leads to the need to use succimethonium despite the risk.
  • In an elective case such as this, it should be avoidable.
  • These types of questions are more about awareness of the issues and a safety-first approach than any one technique.
  • You successfully intubate the patient and after a period of initial hypertension the patient remains stable. You've described induction, please continue.
  • So on to maintenance. I would ensure adequate depth of anesthesia with sever fluorane.
  • I would use remyfentinal to aid in minimizing the risk of muscular spasm or dysreflexia.
  • I would use intermittent positive pressure ventilation to avoid the increased risk of hypoxia or hyperkalemia with his high-cord lesion while being alert for the exaggerated hypotension that may ensue.
Q7
The original question was, how would you enistatise?
A
  • You don't have to go through all the possible methods, just what you would do.
  • Not everyone would use remyfentinal for example. Alternatively, a total intravenous anesthetic is equally appropriate.
  • I would actively warm the patient using forced air blankets and fluid warming as he is likely to have altered thermoregulation.
  • Though not anticipating major blood loss, I would frequently monitor the surgical site and replace any losses immediately as the patient will not have the normal cardiovascular compensatory mechanisms.
  • I would also have a rapid acting anti-hypertensive such as glycerine trinitrate immediately available for any episodes of dysreflexia.
  • As with any patient, I would take care with positioning using padding to protect sites vulnerable to pressure damage. In his case, there is a greater risk as I have already mentioned the skin is likely to be thin and relatively avascular.
  • As well as standard monitoring as per the association of anesthetist guidelines, I would consider using intra arterial blood pressure monitoring.
Q8
Would you use it or not?
A
  • As I do not have experience with these patients and given the concerns with both hypo and hyper-attensive crises, I would use it.
  • In fact, some centres with a large volume of surgery for these patients don't routinely employ intra arterial monitoring.
  • Given you have already explained the risks of cardiovascular instability, it's easier to justify using it.
Q9
Despite all your preventative measures, during surgery his blood pressure rapidly rises to 210 over 140mm of mercury. What would you do?
A
  • This is almost certainly spinal dysreflexia. I would send for senior assistants, ensure the patient is adequately anesthetised, inform the surgeon and ask them to stop.
  • If withdrawing surgical stimulus did not reduce blood pressure, I would increase the depth of anesthesia.
  • If that failed to obviate the hypotensive crisis, I would administer intravenous glycerine trinitrativ available or via metadose or osprey.
  • I would also consider bladder distension and check for a blocked urinary catheter.
  • Intravenous GTN is faster acting and can be titrated to response. In high volume centres where central neural axial block aid is routinely used to obviate dysreflexia, hypotensive crises are rarely encountered.
  • A metadose spray may be kept immediately available in theatre.
Q10
Why use glycerine trinitrate?
A
  • For hypertension is likely to be paroxysmal and therefore any agent must be rapid acting and have a short duration of action. Another alternative is the alpha blocking agent phentolamine.
  • There is a large list of drugs in the literature that have been used to prevent or treat including recipe, doxasicin, niphetamine and clonidine, which treats spasm as well as hypertension, knowing one of these should be enough.
Q11
Having treated that crisis the patient once again stabilises. You are nearing the end of the case and the surgeon informs you he plans to infiltrate with some hupypivocane and adrenaline. What do you think about this?
A
  • I would suggest he doesn't use it. I would explain that the patient has a complete delusion and therefore will not require the analgesia.
  • If there was some concern about sensation then I would insist that he use plain local anaesthetic. The patient has already had one hypotensive crisis and is likely to have increased sensitivity to cataclyclic amines.
  • Cataclyclic amines levels nor adrenaline especially increased during dysreflexic episodes. However they remain lower than levels in normal non-ingered patients so the implication is an increased sensitivity is at least in part responsible for the crises.
  • Most anaesthetists should be able to answer the questions about acute management of the patient with a spinal cord injury so you would be expected to be confident and decisive in your answers here.
  • In contrast the second part of this viability deals with a specific patient group that many anaesthetists will never encounter. Don't worry if you don't know all the details especially the pathophysiology of autonomic dysreflexia.
  • Most of the issues you can work out for example the risk of respiratory compromise. An awareness of the issues surrounding succumbethanium and hypotensive crises should be sufficient.

What are the roles of magnesium? Magnesium is an ion that has a number of therapeutic applications. You need to have detailed accurate knowledge of its actions.

Magnesium is important in maintaining electrical potentials across cell membranes. It is vital in the function of ATP and the synthesis of DNA, RNA and proteins. It affects calcium metabolism and so is often associated with hypocalcemia. Intracellular magnesium also inhibits calcium influx and it is therefore described as a physiological calcium antagonist. With regards to the body systems, magnesium has effects on most systems. It is a cardiovascular depressant which can cause a reduction in cardiac output and vascular tone.

Magnesium will cause skeletal muscle weakness and in theory precipitate respiratory failure. It is an effective bronchodilator. With regards to the genotyuronry system, magnesium is a topolytic and amyled diuretic. Finally, magnesium reduces platelet activity.

How is magnesium stored and how levels controlled? Magnesium is the second most common intracellular cation after potassium. It is distributed 65% in bone and 35% in cells. About one third of dietary magnesium is absorbed in the small intestine. The kidneys control the plasma levels by controlling excretion of magnesium. Normally, filtered magnesium is reabsorbed in the ascending limb of the loop of Henley, with only 1% excreted in the urine.

Magnesium deficiency is due to magnesium loss from diarrhea or due to lack of intake, classically in patients receiving TPN. Raised magnesium levels may be caused by excessive treatment or intake.

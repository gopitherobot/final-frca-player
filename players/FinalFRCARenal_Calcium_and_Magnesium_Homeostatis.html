Calcium magnesium homeostasis.
Q1
How is calcium metabolism controlled?
A
  • This is another physiology by the question.
  • Calcium metabolism is controlled by parathyroid hormone,
  • vitamin D and calcitonin.
  • Parathyroid hormone is secreted in response to low plasma calcium levels.
  • It causes a rise in plasma calcium
  • and a fall in plasma phosphate.
  • The parathyroid hormone increases bone reabsorption,
  • enhances vitamin D activity,
  • and also reduces phosphate reabsorption by the kidney.
  • Vitamin D is produced in the skin in response to sunlight.
  • It is then activated in the liver and then kidney to form 125 dihydroxyvitamine D.
  • This active vitamin D is stimulated by low calcium and phosphate levels
  • as well as by parathyroid hormone.
  • Active vitamin D increases calcium and phosphate levels
  • by increasing gut absorption and bone reabsorption.
  • The active vitamin D has a negative feedback effect on parathyroid hormone levels.
  • Calcitonin reduces plasma calcium and phosphate levels.
Q2
What are normal plasma calcium levels?
A
  • The normal level is 2.2 to 2.6 millimoles per liter.
  • It is important to remember that calcium is 40% bound to albumin
  • and it is the unbound ionized portion which is active.
  • Calcium measurements should therefore be adjusted for the albumin level,
  • thus for every 4 grams per liter of albumin that is below the normal level of 40 grams per liter.
  • 0.1 millimoles per liter of calcium should be added to the initial result.

Q5
What are the actions of calcium?
A
  • Calcium is vital for a number of different systems.
  • In the hematological system, calcium is required for hemostasis.
  • In the musculoskeletal system, it is vital for the structural integrity of bone.
  • It plays a key role in the release of astylecoline at the presynaptic bulb
  • and is also integral to the function of the actin-myosin power stroke in skeletal muscle contraction.
  • Calcium is also important in cardiac and smooth muscle contraction.
  • Finally, in the neurological system, calcium has functions as a neurotransmitter
  • and as a second messenger system.
Q6
What are the features of hypocalcemia?
A
  • Patients may present with tetany, perioral tingling,
  • carpopedal spasm and a depression.
  • Neuromuscular excitability may also be seen
  • and can be elicited by tapping over the facial nerve causing twitching.
  • This is show-stech sign.
  • The ECG findings would be of an increased QT interval.
Q7
How can we treat hypocalcemia?
A
  • Myeltypocalcemia can be treated with oral calcium.
  • Severe symptomatic hypocalcemia should be treated with intravenous calcium gluconate 10% repeated as necessary.

Q11
Can you tell me where most magnesium is located in the body and what the normal plasma concentration is?
A
  • It is predominantly an intracellular ion with an intracellular concentration of 5-20 millimoles per liter.
  • The normal plasma concentration ranges from 0.5 to 1 millimoles per liter.
Q12
What are the roles of magnesium?
A
  • Magnesium is an ion that has a number of therapeutic applications.
  • You need to have detailed accurate knowledge of its actions.
  • Magnesium is important in maintaining electrical potentials across cell membranes.
  • It is vital in the function of ATP and the synthesis of DNA, RNA and proteins.
  • It affects calcium metabolism and so is often associated with hypocalcemia.
  • Intracellular magnesium also inhibits calcium influx and it is therefore described as a physiological calcium antagonist.
  • With regards to the body systems, magnesium has effects on most systems.
  • It is a cardiovascular depressant which can cause a reduction in cardiac output and vascular tone.
  • It has anti-arhythmic effects and inhibits the release of cataclylamines.
  • Magnesium is an anti-convulsant that reduces excitability of nerves and antagonizes calcium at the presynaptic junction.
  • Magnesium will cause skeletal muscle weakness and in theory precipitate respiratory failure.
  • It is an effective bronchodilator.
  • With regards to the genotyuronry system, magnesium is a topolytic and amyled diuretic.
  • Finally, magnesium reduces platelet activity.
Q13
How is magnesium stored and how levels controlled?
A
  • Magnesium is the second most common intracellular cation after potassium.
  • It is distributed 65% in bone and 35% in cells.
  • About one third of dietary magnesium is absorbed in the small intestine.
  • The kidneys control the plasma levels by controlling excretion of magnesium.
  • Normally, filtered magnesium is reabsorbed in the ascending limb of the loop of Henley, with only 1% excreted in the urine.
  • Parathyroid hormone enhances both gut and kidney reabsorption whilst aldosterone increases renal excretion.
Q14
What are the causes of abnormal magnesium levels?
A
  • Magnesium deficiency is due to magnesium loss from diarrhea or due to lack of intake, classically in patients receiving TPN.
  • Raised magnesium levels may be caused by excessive treatment or intake.
Q15
What are the effects of hypermagnesemia?
A
  • Parasthesia, fits, tetanine and arrhythmias have all been observed.
  • It is important to remember that hypocalcemia may also occur.
  • The treatment is replacement of magnesium.
Q16
What about hypermagnesemia?
A
  • The effect of magnesium depends on the plasma level.
  • At plasma concentrations of 4 to 5 millimoles per liter,
  • the patient may lose their tendon reflexes and suffer from muscle weakness.
  • At levels of 7 to 8 millimoles per liter, the patient will have respiratory muscle paralysis,
  • and at levels over 10 millimoles per liter, cardiac arrest will occur.
  • Treatment is aimed at reducing levels.
  • Calcium gluconate will antagonize magnesium whilst dioresis and dialysis are implemented.

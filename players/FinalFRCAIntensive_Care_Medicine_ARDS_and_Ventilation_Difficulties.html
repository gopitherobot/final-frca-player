ARDS and ventilation difficulties. The management of ARDS is a core topic in intensive care and will also be relevant to all who will have to inestitise high risk and critically ill patients. In the vivas, it may be a clinical scenario or follow a discussion of respiratory physiology, for example, the different causes of hypoxia.
Q1
What are the causes of hypoxia? Give me a clinical example of each.
A
  • Hypoxia is an inadequate tissue oxygen supply or use and has four main causes.
  • Hypoxic, anemic, ischemic and cytopathic.
  • Hypoxic hypoxia is a reduction of oxygen uptake by the lung, for example, after a pneumonia.
  • Anemic hypoxia is reduced arterial oxygen content, secondary to loss of hemoglobin,
  • or presence of abnormal hemoglobin, examples are major hemorrhage and carbon monoxide poisoning.
  • Ischemic hypoxia is reduced oxygen delivery, secondary to reduced cardiac output or local
  • vascular occlusion, for example, cardiogenic shock or arterial thromboembolism.
  • Cytopathic hypoxia results from disrupted cellular metabolism, for example, cyanide poisoning
  • of mitochondrial respiratory chain enzymes.
  • Different types may coexist.
  • I'm interested in hypoxic hypoxia.
Q2
How does this occur?
A
  • There are several mechanisms.
  • First, the inspired oxygen concentration may be reduced.
  • Second, overall, minute ventilation may be reduced.
  • Then there are the effects of ventilation perfusion mismatching and of shunt with venous
  • admixture.
  • Finally, reduced diffusing capacity may also contribute in some situations.

Q4
But what about diffusion?
A
  • In most pathological conditions, gas exchange is not diffusion limited, since the alveola
  • capillary barrier is so gas permeable.
  • In practical terms, extra pulmonary factors, such as cardiac output, mixed venous
  • oxygenation and CO2 production, will have a greater effect on arterial gases than any
  • impairment of diffusion.

Q5
Can you explain how hypoxia may arise in terms of VQ mismatching?
A
  • Full saturation of mixed venous blood requires a sufficiently high alveola P02.
  • In poorly ventilated, well-perfused alveoli, there is insufficient oxygen delivered to the
  • alveoli to saturate pulmonary venous blood fully.
  • In cases of trusshunt, where there is no ventilation and perfusion is maintained, no oxygen is
  • taken up into the pulmonary venous blood.
  • So, as the number of units with a low ventilation to perfusion ratio increases, the total oxygen
  • content of pulmonary venous blood will fall.
  • Incidentally, this is also how hypoventilation causes hypoxemia, since alveola oxygen falls
  • and CO2 rises and is also important if dead space volume approaches tidal volume.

A
  • If the dead space increases, then a greater portion of the tidal volume does not participate
  • in gas exchange.

  • rises and also in poorly ventilated alveoli ventilation to perfusion ratio falls.
  • Overall, alveola oxygen content falls and therefore so does pulmonary oxygen uptake.

Q7
Are you aware of any clinical conditions in which dead space fractions relate to mortality?
A
  • As far as I know, there are two where this has been demonstrated.
  • Pulmonary embolism and ARDS.
  • In both, increased dead space is associated with increased mortality.

Q8
What is the generally except definition?
A
  • The generally accepted definition is that of the American European Consensus Conference
  • in 1994.
  • It has four components, an acute onset, bilateral infiltrates on chest x-ray, absence of left
  • atrial hyper tension, and a PF ratio of under 200mm of mercury or 26.7 kilopascals.
Q9
Are there any difficulties with this definition?
A
  • Firstly, there is an element of subjectivity in assessing infiltrates on chest x-rays.
  • Secondly, it can be difficult to exclude left atrial hyper tension clinically and
  • a pulmonary artery occlusion pressure of more than 18 may coexist with ARDS.
  • Finally, the PF ratio may be significantly affected by ventilator settings and some argue
  • the definition should specify standard settings.
Q10
What are the causes?
A
  • There are many.
  • Conveniently considered into categories, pulmonary and extra pulmonary.
  • Extra pulmonary causes are direct insults to the lung, like pneumonia and aspiration,
  • or more rarely smoke inhalation or pulmonary contusion.
  • Extra pulmonary or indirect insults are most commonly sepsis or severe trauma, but also transfusion
  • reactions, burns, pancreatitis or drug reactions.
  • There is a classification help.
  • There is a lot of debate about whether or not pulmonary and extra pulmonary ARDS are different
  • diseases, and I don't think that this has been resolved.
  • For practical purposes, most treat them in the same way.
Q11
What is a pathophysiology?
A
  • There are two stages that may be distinguished histologically.
  • The exudative phase, followed by the fibro-proliferative phase.
  • First, there is an acute inflammatory reaction in the lung, and the alveoli fill with neutrophils,
  • blood, and protein-rich pulmonary edema.
  • There is associated alveola epithelial and capillary endothelial damage, with loss of alveola
  • cells, and structural integrity and compilary thrombosis.
  • Days or weeks later, chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and neovascularization occur.
Q12
So how does ARDS cause hypoxia? Can you relate this to our earlier discussion?
A
  • There is widespread and profound ventilation perfusion mismatching as a result of the inflammatory
  • reaction, which may be superimposed on a direct lung injury.
  • Both shunt and dead space increase, as alveoli and capillaries respectively are occluded.
  • If severe, right ventricular failure and low cardiac output contribute further to venous
  • admixture and hypoxia.

Q17
Moving on, what are the principles of management of ARDS in ICU?
A
  • There is specific and supportive care.
  • Public care includes treating the cause of ARDS and preventing further lung damage with
  • a lung protective ventilator strategy.
  • Fluid balance is important as the art net fluids and catheter study of 2006 suggests that
  • keeping the lung dry is associated with better outcomes.
  • Active care includes deep vein thrombosis and ulcer prophylaxis, control of blood glucose
  • and elevation of the head of the bed.
Q18
Are the specific treatments for ARDS itself? What about fish oil?
A
  • As yet there is no proven therapy for established ARDS.
  • There are two small studies of entral omega-3 fatty acids showing reduced mortality, but
  • other conflicting evidence.
  • Other therapies under investigation include beta agonists to help clear alveolar edema,
  • exogenous-suffactant to replace the endogenous deficit and fibrenelitics or activated protein
  • C to reduce alveolar and capillary thrombosis.
Q19
Anything else?
A
  • I'm sure I remember giving steroids in my ICU days.
  • Again, there is conflicting evidence and considerable controversy regarding steroids
  • in ARDS.
  • If given at moderate doses within the first week or ten days and tapered slowly, there
  • appear to be benefits in gas exchange and reduced time on the ventilator.
  • Given later, any benefit is outweighed by more neuromuscular problems and increased
  • mortality.
Q20
What is lung protective ventilation?
A
  • It is a ventilation strategy that is based on the premise that ventilation causes injury
  • to the sick lung in a number of ways.
  • The major causes of injury are volutroma, barotroma, atelectotroma and biotroma.
Q21
What do these terms mean?
A
  • In ARDS, not all of the lung is equally involved in the pathological process.
  • Essentially, excessive tidal volume and inflation pressure over-distant healthy alveoli, hence
  • volume and barotroma, and affected lung is subjected to cyclical opening and closing,
  • hence a tolectotroma.
  • The damage to the alveola capillary unit leads to increased permeability, release of
  • inflammatory mediators and translocation of pathogens, hence biotroma.
Q22

A
  • The general principle is to open the lung and keep it open while avoiding excessively
  • high tidal volumes and pressures.
  • The landmark trial is the ARDS net trial of 2000, which used targets of 6 mil per kilo
  • ideal body weight for tidal volume and plateau pressures of less than 30 centimetres of water.
  • The exact role of positive end-expiratory pressure and recruitment maneuvers are more
  • open for discussion, but most would set people at 10 to 15 centimetres of water to begin
  • with, depending on the degree of hypoxia.

A

Q27
What is the mortality associated with ARDS?
A
  • In recent randomized control trials, the mortality has been around 25 to 30%, but community
  • surveys show mortality of about 35 to 40%.
  • This may reflect the difficulty of translating research findings into clinical practice.
Q28
What are the predictors of outcome?
A
  • The age of the patient and the number of non-pulmonary organ failures are the best predictors.
  • Elderly patients with shock do badly, as do those with hepatic failure.
  • Surprisingly, the initial PF ratio is not predictive unless it is under 50 or fails to improve
  • during the first week.
Q29
What is the mode of death in ARDS?
A
  • Well, it is rarely intractable severe hypoxia.
  • Death is usually due to other organ failures and sepsis.
Q30
What is function like in survivors of ARDS?
A
  • There are persistent problems in many survivors.
  • Lung function deficits are present in most, although this is rarely a problem in day-to-day
  • life and returned to nearly normal within the first year.
  • Cognitive and neuromuscular complications can be frequent and severe and are more likely
  • to prevent return to work or precipitate loss of independence.
  • Doctors do have a reduced health-related quality of life.

As offergeolatresia, diaphragmatic hernia, and exonphalus. These are pediatric surgical problems which many final FRCA candidates may not have encountered in their training. You are unlikely to encounter questions on more than one of these problems within a viber. A structured understanding of each problem and the main anaesthetic considerations relating to it will ensure you tackle a potentially difficult question well. You are on call for pediatric emergencies and are asked to assess a term neonate with a Zoffegyolatresia.
0:35
What do you understand about this condition? A Zoffegyolatresia and Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula are part of a spectrum of conditions where there is defective embryological development of the Tricio and Zoffegos. The incidence is approximately one in 3,500 live births and many cases are diagnosed antinatally. Can you describe the anatomical abnormality in more detail? There are several patterns of abnormal anatomy.
1:06
It is worth being able to sketch these as it makes remembering and explaining the differences easier. The most common type involves a blind ending of a Zoffegos with a fistula between the distal portion of the Zoffegos and the Tricio. This occurs in approximately 80% of cases. What would you look for in your pre-operative assessment of this infant? It is important to look for other congenital abnormalities because 50% of infants with a Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula will have associated problems. It is particularly important to look for evidence of congenital cardiac problems.
1:50
The baby may have been born prematurely, raising the possibility of lung disease, retinal problems and impaired blood sugar regulation. What is the Vacturl Association? If you are doing particularly well up to this point, this may be asked. This is an acronym used to describe a group of congenital problems which can occur together. An affected infant has vertebral, anorectal, cardiac, Tricio a Zoffegyol, renal and limb abnormalities. How does Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula present?
2:32
The baby may have repeated episodes of coughing, choking and cyanosis. This is exacerbated by attempts at feeding. There is resistance to passage of an oragastric tube. How should the infant be managed pre-operatively? The child cannot take oral fluids or nutrition, so intravenous fluids should be administered, taking care to avoid hypoglycemia.
3:03
A specialized suction tube, called a replogal tube, can be used to prevent pooling of secretions in the upper part of the esophagus. This works by letting air in to avoid high negative pressures sucking the esophageal mucusa into the catheter. What is the optimum timing of corrective surgery after birth? In an otherwise uncomplicated case of Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula or a Zoffegyol atrizia, surgery should be carried out within 24 hours of delivery because accumulation of fluid in the upper esophagus leads to a risk of aspiration. How would you induce anesthesia in this patient?
3:51
The tube in the upper pouch should be suctioned following attachment to the saturation probe. Inhalational induction with Civa flurain is carried out. The Tricio is intubated with a size 3 or 3.5mm tube. Vigorous bag and mask ventilation is avoided because this can force air through the fistula from the Tricio to the distal esophagus and stomach. This can splint the diaphragm and impair ventilation. The infant is allowed to begin to breathe spontaneously on the tube, at which point the tube can be removed to permit examination of the airway with a rigid broncuscope.
4:31
The exact anatomy of the Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula can be established. The endotricial tube can then be replaced and positioned so that the Tricio a Zoffegyol fistula is occluded. In this way, the escape of gas from the Tricio to the distal esophagus and stomach during positive pressure ventilation is minimized. Once the tube position is correct, a neuromuscular blocking agent can be administered and gentle hand ventilation commenced. What are the intraoperative concerns? These relate to positioning, monitoring, temperature regulation and maintenance of reliable intravenous access.
5:16
The infant is usually positioned right lateral and strict attention must be paid to pressure areas. Invasive monitoring with arterial and central venous lines is appropriate. Active warming methods must be used. How will you manage the child postoperatively? Most infants remain ventilated postoperatively and are cared for on the neonatal or pediatric intensive care unit. Appropriate sedation and analgesia should be ensured. What can you tell me about congenital diaphragmatic hernia?
5:56
Congenital diaphragmatic hernia occurs in approximately one in every three to four thousand live births. The diaphragmatic defect is most commonly left-sided and associated with a variable degree of lung hypoplasia. 40% of patients have significant associated congenital abnormalities of which cardiac anomalies are most common. Continental data suggests that abnormal lung development begins at an early stage of embryonic development before the diaphragm begins to form. Subsequent diaphragmatic development is disturbed leading to the observed anatomical defect. Herniation of abdominal contents through the defect in the later stages of fetal development exerts a pressure effect further impairing lung development.
6:48
The affected lung has poorly developed airways, reduced numbers of type 2 numerocytes and abnormal highly reactive pulmonary vasculature. All affected infants have some degree of pulmonary hypertension. Is surgical correction of the defect an emergency procedure? No, the condition of the child should be optimised prior to considering a surgical procedure. Surgery does not improve gas exchange because of the associated pulmonary hypertension and lung hypoplasia. How should the patient be optimised prior to theatre?
7:29
Delivery should take place as close as possible to term to maximise lung development. Bag and mask ventilation should be minimised to prevent distension of the intra-theracic bowel. Intubation and ventilation with close attention to avoiding baritourma should follow. The bowel lying within the chest cavity can be decompressed within a gastric tube. The child should be transferred to a pediatric critical care area for invasive monitoring and further stabilisation. Appropriate investigations include arterial blood gases, chest x-ray and echocardiogram.
8:10
The principles of ventilating the infant are to achieve adequate gas exchange whilst preventing eye-atrogenic injury to the lung. In spiritary pressures are limited and permissive hyper-capnia is used. Inhalled nitric oxide may be trialled in patients with significant pulmonary hypertension, although the evidence of an improved outcome in this condition is so far lacking. Surgery is generally delayed for 24 to 48 hours to allow a fall in pulmonary resistance. What is exomphalus?
8:48
Exomphalus is a congenital defect of the abdominal wall. There is herniation of abdominal contents with a covering sac through a midline defect. It is caused by failure of the gut to migrate into the abdominal cavity during fetal development. How does gastroskycis differ? Gastrocysis is also an anterior abdominal wall defect, allowing herniation of intra-dominal contents. It does not occur in the midline and there is no covering sac over the exposed bowel.
9:26
Exomphalus is commonly associated with other congenital problems, particularly cardiac defects, whilst gastrocysis is usually an isolated problem. What are the problems associated with the two conditions? There is significant loss of heat and moisture by evaporation from the exposed abdominal contents. The bowel can be damaged by the drying effect and is a potential root for the entry of infection. What is the management?
10:01
The exposed bowel should be initially covered with a non-porus material such as cling film. Strict attention should be given to estimating and replacing fluid losses and to maintenance of normothermia. Primary surgical closure is attempted once the infant is adequately resuscitated. Staged closure may be necessary if the abdominal contents cannot be reduced without compromising respiratory function.

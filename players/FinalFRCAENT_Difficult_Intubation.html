Prediction of a difficult track hill intubation and the management of intubation difficulties.
Q1
In your day to day practice, how do you attempt to predict a difficult track hill intubation?
A
  • Remember to classify your answer. A great way to approach this question is
  • History, Examination and Investigations. There are a lot of different aspects to remember here.
  • It may help to jot things down on a piece of paper.
  • There are many factors that might contribute to a difficult intubation.
  • I therefore approach airway assessment in a systematic way.
  • I take a full medical and anaesthetic history from the patient,
  • paying particular attention to previous airway management.
  • The patient may have been told that they are difficult to intubate
  • and may even have been given a card, letter or medical hurt bracelet.
  • They may have medical conditions which are associated with difficult intubation
  • such as rheumatoid arthritis or ankylosing spondylitis.
  • I check previous anaesthetic charts for airway difficulties and
  • Kormak and Lehane gradings, bearing in mind that these may have changed over time.
  • I then examine the patient. On general examination,
  • findings that are associated with difficult intubation are obesity, pregnancy,
  • large breasts, a short thick neck, goiter, hematoma or other mass in the neck,
  • micro or macrognathy, prominent upper incisors, high arched palate, large tongue,

Q3
Can you tell me how you'd perform this test?
A
  • This involves visually inspecting the pharyngeal structures
  • with the patient sitting, with the mouth fully open, tone protruded and without
  • fination. In a class one view, the soft palate, uvula and tonsular pillars
  • are visible. In class two, the soft palate and the base of the uvula, in class three,
  • the soft palate only, and in class four, just the hard palate.
  • This test yields many false positive results and 50% false negative results.
Q4
What are the bedside assessments do you perform?
A
  • Another bedside test is the thyrimental distance, which is the distance in centimeters
  • from the notch of the thyroid cartilage to the chin with the head fully extended.
  • A distance of less than seven centimeters is associated with difficult
  • intubation.
  • I also always assess mouth opening. Less than three centimeters is abnormal in an
  • adult. Mouth opening can be affected by acute local pain and
  • inflammation, so it's important to try to assess whether pain is the only
  • limiting factor, in which case mouth opening can often improve on induction of
  • anesthesia.
  • I also test jaw protrusion. The patient should be able to protrude the lower
  • incisors in front of the upper incisors. I also assess neck extension at the
  • Atlanta-Excipital joint. A range of movement of 35 degrees or more is normal.
  • This may be limited by arthritis and collosing spondylitis, injury or surgery
  • to the cervical spine, a rigid collar or the need for manual inline stabilization.

Q10
Can you outline a method for awake intubation?
A
  • I gain informed consent and ensure minimum monitoring standards are in place.
  • The procedure requires a skilled assistant and ideally a second anacetist.
  • I would secure intravenous access and have an intravenous infusion running.
  • I give glycopyralate 200 micrograms intravenously as an anti-silogog
  • and then spray both nostrils with a vasoconstrictor, usually xylomatazoline spray.
  • I have found a low dose remyfentinal infusion to be useful for its sedative and antitussive properties.
  • I run this at a rate that results in a respiratory rate of 8 to 10 breaths per minute.
  • There are many ways to provide local anesthesia for the airway
  • but the method I prefer is to atomise 2% lidocaine with a 3-way tap attached to a long 20 gauge cannula
  • and oxygen tubing with oxygen running at 2 litres per minute as the driving gas.
  • I then atomise 1 mil of 2% lidocaine on inspiration every 1 to 2 minutes
  • until there is an obvious change in the patient's voice.
Q11
What is the maximum dose of lidocaine that you would use?
A
  • I use a maximum dose of 8 milligrams per kilogram, which is recommended by the British thoracic society guidelines for flexible bronchoscopy.
Q12
What can the needs to do to prepare for the unanticipated failed intubation?
A
  • This question is designed to assess your safety as an inistist.
  • The examiner wants to establish that if this potentially life threatening situation happens to you,
  • you will approach it in a calm and measured way with patient safety as your prime concern.
  • About half of difficult intubations are not predicted and so anistatists should prepare for all intubations as if failure could occur.
  • On a basic level, we should ensure that we are familiar with all of the airway equipment and techniques that can be used during a difficult intubation.
  • We should all know exactly what equipment is available on the difficult intubation trolley and where it is.
  • A trolley should be present in all operating theatre suites, intensive care units and emergency departments.
  • Difficult intubation is a rare occurrence, so we need to practice the rescue techniques, for example on mannequins or in simulators.
  • We should also be familiar with a difficult intubation algorithm.
Q13
Which difficult intubation algorithm do you use?
A
  • I use the difficult airway society guidelines. These are well regarded, evidence-based guidelines, which give separate, structured approaches to a difficult intubation during routine and rapid sequence induction.
  • I always have a clear plan A and plan B, which I communicate to my assistant before any intubation attempt.
  • The guidelines emphasize optimization of the initial intubation attempt.
  • Summoning senior anesthetic help as soon as possible, avoiding repeated attempts at larynxoscopy and maintaining oxygenation above all else.
  • You perform a rapid sequence induction of anesthesia and a patient in whom you would not predict difficulty and obtain a callmack and lehane grade 4 view on larynxoscopy.
Q14
How do you approach this situation?
A
  • You should have a difficult airway algorithm such as the difficult airway society guideline or the one used in your own hospital committed to memory.
  • Firstly, I would ensure oxygenation at all times and if oxygen saturation start to fall, I would abandon intubation attempts and ventilate the patient with a face mask and airway adjuncts.
Q15
Let us assume that the oxygen saturation is maintained in the first instance. How would you proceed?
A
  • Initially, I would attempt to optimize the initial view at larynxoscopy. I would ensure that the head was in the correct position and try external laryngeal manipulation like backward, upward, right, wood pressure.
  • I may try an alternative larynxoscope blade such as the McCoy or a straight blade. If I obtained a view of any laryngeal structures, I would attempt to pass a gammelastic bougie either under direct vision or blindly into the midline.
  • If I was successful, I may feel clicks as the bougie passes down the trachea and hold up as the tip of the bougie reaches the carina.
Q16
Unfortunately, none of your maneuvers allowed you to intubate. What should you do next?
A
  • I would avoid repeated attempts at laryngeoscopy and would not give a second dose of muscle relaxant. After three attempts at intubation or if oxygen saturation began to fall, I would abandon attempts at intubation.
  • I would call for senior help and concentrate all of my efforts on oxygenating the patient. I would allow the patient to awake unless surgery is immediately life threatening. Initially, I would attempt to face mask ventilation with airway adjuncts such as an auro-pharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway and a two-person technique if necessary.
  • If oxygen saturation falls below 90% using these techniques, I would attempt to insert a laryngeal mask airway. This may involve reducing crycoyd pressure both for insertion and subsequent ventilation, but this is acceptable in the circumstances.
Q17
Despite your best efforts, you are unable to ventilate the patient with the bag valve mask or the laryngeal mask airway. Your patient is sign-osed with falling oxygen saturations. No senior help is available. Now what will you do?
A
  • Don't mess about here. You are all the way down the Plan D of the Difficult Airway Society guidelines that can't intubate, can't ventilate scenario. You have to perform a crycothyroidosomy.
  • The patient requires a crycothyroidotomy, which I could either achieve with a cannula or a surgical technique. I could use either a standard intravenous cannula or a specialized crycothyroidotomy cannula.
Q18
What oxygen supply can you connect these two?
A
  • These cannuli require high pressure ventilation, which means wall oxygen at 400 kilopascals.
  • This involves either connecting oxygen tubing to a wall flow meter turned to 15 litres per minute, or using a specialized high pressure ventilator, such as the Sanders or Manuject.
  • The common gas outlet of the anaesthetic machine cannot be used as there is a pressure relief valve, which will vent at 4 kilopascals, which is insufficient for high pressure ventilation.
  • I would need to allow a long time for exploration, which needs to occur through the upper airway to avoid volume trauma.
Q19
What is your alternative to needle techniques?
A
  • The other option is a surgical technique, which I could perform with either a kit that employs a celldinger technique, or by incising the skin and crycothyroid membrane using a scalpel, then dilating the orifice in order to insert a standard endotracheal tube
  • of approximately size 6mm of internal diameter.
  • You've saved the patient's life. Well done.

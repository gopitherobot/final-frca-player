A generic 5-letter code to describe the functions of pacemakers has been devised. The first letter indicates the chamber being paced, A for atrium, V for ventricle and D for dual. The second letter indicates the chamber being sensed, A for atrium, V for ventricle, D for dual and O for none. The third letter indicates the response to sensing, I is inhibited, T for triggered. The fourth letter indicates programmability, O equals none, C equals communicating, P equals simple programmability, M equals multi-programmability, R equals rate modulation. The fifth letter indicates anti-tackicaridia functions, O equals none, P is for pacing, S for shocks and D for dual, for example pacing and shocks.

What is electromagnetic interference? Electromagnetic interference refers to any electromagnetic radiation with the potential to affect implantable devices. The most important source of potentially damaging electromagnetic interference in an operating theatre environment is surgical diaphragm. Other hospital sources include magnetic resonance imaging, cardiaversion or cardiac defibrillation, lythotrypsy and transcutaneous nerve stimulation. What are the potential adverse effects of electromagnetic interference and patients with permanent pacemakers? Possible responses to external electromagnetic interference include inappropriate inhibition or triggering of impulses, reprogramming the pacemaker to a backup mode, which may be a synchronous or permanent damage to the pacemaker itself. Is there any safety advantage of using one type of diaphragm over the other in this situation? Wherever possible, surgical diaphragm should be avoided altogether. However, where surgical diaphragm is deemed essential, the use of bipolar diaphragm is preferred as the chance of interference with pacemaker function is less in this mode.

In an elective setting, what additional equipment and expertise is required whenever diaphragm is used in a patient with a permanent pacemaker? Clear guidance on this topic has been provided by the Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency. If surgical diaphragm cannot be avoided, the patient's cardiac follow-up centre should usually be contacted for advice. If the surgical procedure is remote from the pacemaker site and the device has been checked within the last three months, then the risk of malfunction is minimal. If the procedure will be close to the implant, then the risk of diothermia-induced malfunction is increased. In these instances, the patient's follow-up clinic should be asked to advise whether support from a cardiac pacing physiologist will be required before, during and after the surgical procedure.

Steps taken may include programming a pacemaker to minimise inappropriate inhibition secondary to electromagnetic interference, disabling shock delivery functions and rate response functions that use the minute ventilation or sleep modes of physiological pacing, and reactivation of functions and confirmation of device functioning after surgery. This must include a defibrillator and access to alternative pacing methods such as temporary external and transvenous pacing. How does advice above differ for emergency procedures?

If diothermia is necessary, what steps must be taken to minimise the chance of pacemaker malfunction? Bi-poladirethermia should be used in preference to monopolar diothermia whenever possible. If monopolar diothermia is unavoidable, then it's use should be limited to short bursts, keeping the energy level as low as possible. The return plate should be positioned so that the current pathway between the diothermia electrode and the return plate is as far away as possible from the pacemaker and its leads.

In operations involving either mono or bipolar diothermia, the cables attached to the diothermia equipment should be kept away from the pacemaker. How is pacemaker failure suspected? The consequences of pacemaker failure depend upon the underlying cardiac rhythm. This highlights the importance of knowing the original indication for insertion of a pacemaker. Clearly, if the original indication was ventricular standstill, then pacemaker failure is potentially lethal. Pacemaker failure results from loss of electrical capture, and in the perioperative period is most commonly caused by electromagnetic interference.

As electromagnetic interference also interferes with ECG recording, recognition of pacemaker failure during electromagnetic interference relies on monitoring the plethysmograph trace, arterial line trace, or patient's pulse. If this is not done, then pacemaker failure may initially go unnoticed. How should pacemaker failure be treated? If cardiac arrest has occurred, then all sources of electromagnetic interference should be stopped immediately. If pacemaker failure was due to oversensing, stopping the electromagnetic interference storm may help. If cardiac arrest persists, advanced cardiac life support, including chest compressions, should be commenced immediately.

Describe the anaesthetic management of a patient with an ICD undergoing surgery. Detailed pre-anesthetic assessment is required. The center that implanted the ICD should be contacted, the indication for insertion, and details regarding delivery of countershocks should be obtained. The device should be interrogated preoperatively and the effect of magnet applications should be recorded. Many of these patients will have severe cardiovascular disease, including left ventricular impairment. As for patients with pacemakers undergoing anesthesia and surgery, 12 lead ECG, serum electrolytes, and possibly chest x-ray should be performed preoperatively. Electrolite abnormalities should be corrected preoperatively and anti-arhythmic and anti-faliomedicines should be continued.

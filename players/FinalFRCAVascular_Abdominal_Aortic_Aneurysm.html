Abdominal aortic aneurysm repair. Anastasia for abdominal aortic aneurysm is a big topic and may be a question in an SAQ or VIVA. It is important you understand the physiological consequences of aortic cross-clamping and the likely complications of the surgery, whether it be emergency or elective. A man presents the emergency department with collapse, back pain and confusion. He has a history of hypertension and is a lifelong smoker. He has a heart rate of a hundred and a systolic blood pressure of 90. There is a pulsatile expanding mass palpable in his epicastrium.
Q1
What is an abdominal aortic aneurysm?
A
  • An aortic aneurysm is a dilatation in the wall of the aorta greater than 3cm in diameter.
  • They develop as a result of disruption and degeneration of elastin and collagen fibers
  • in the aortic wall over time, chronic inflammation of the vessel wall or smooth muscle cell loss.
  • The commonest cause is atherosclerosis but they also occur in patients with marphans
  • syndrome, tuberculosis, salmonella and tachyrosus disease.
  • The aneurysms are usually asymptomatic when they are small but expand over time and the
  • risk of spontaneous rupture increases exponentially with increasing diameter.
Q2
What are the risk factors for developing an aortic aneurysm?
A
  • Aneurysms most commonly occur in males, over 65s and cigarette smokers.
  • Smokers are four times more likely to have an aneurysm than non-smokers.
  • A positive family history of aneurysms is also a risk factor.
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysms occur in 10% of all men over 65 and in 3% of women over 65.
Q3
What are the treatment options available?
A
  • Treatment can be surgical or medical management.
  • Medical management is aimed at preventing the expansion of the aneurysm.
  • Drugs such as statins, indomethasin and tetracyclins
  • prevent expansion in animals, however none have been shown to be effective in humans
  • and surgical management is the definitive treatment.
  • Elective surgery has much lower morbidity and mortality than emergency surgery for a ruptured
  • aneurysm, so screening is advocated and elective surgery performed when aneurysm diameter is 5.5
  • centimetres. The elective mortality of having an aortic aneurysm repair is 7%.

A

  • Elective repairs may be performed by an open procedure or endovascular stenting.
  • Conservative management for ruptured aneurysms is an option if the patient is unlikely to survive

  • Symptom control and family liaison are crucial in such cases.
Q5
What kind of abilities would you expect in patients presenting with a AAA?
A
  • These patients are commonly arteriopaths, suffering from coronary artery disease,
  • hyper-tension, peripheral vascular disease and possible cerebrovascular disease.
  • There is a high chance that they are smokers and have COPD.
  • They may have diabetes and suffer from renal failure.
  • Exercise tolerance should be recorded. If the patient can walk up one flight of stairs
  • without symptoms, this demonstrates an exercise capacity equal to or greater than 4 m.
  • Patients achieving 4 m or more have improved outcomes as mets can be used to provide an indication
  • of an individual's functional capacity, which is more formally measured by VO2 max.
  • An anaerobic threshold or VO2 max of less than 11 mls per kilo per minute predicts
  • high perioperative risk, although this is not routinely tested in most centres.
Q6
Do you know any scoring systems for risk assessment?
A
  • General scoring systems such as Apache 2 and POSM scoring can be employed to estimate risk,
  • but are not accurate at predicting outcome in ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm patients.
  • Goldman and Dezky analysis both look at cardiac risk factors and provide a score equating to
  • relative risk of major adverse cardiac events. The Hardman Index for ruptured aneurysms
  • has a score of 0 to 5 with one point being awarded for age greater than 76,
  • creatinine over 190 micromoles per litre, hemoglobin less than 9 grams per desolita,
  • ischemic ECG and history of loss of consciousness, a score of 2 or more gives a predicted mortality of 80%.
  • There is also the Glasgow aneurysms score which can be applied to elective and emergency
  • aneurysm repairs. The Glasgow aneurysm score adds age in years to other variables with allocated
  • scores. These include shock, myocardial disease, cerebrovascular disease and renal disease.
  • Scores of 84 or above indicate a mortality of 65%. However, all scoring systems have limitations
  • and need to be used as a tool to supplement clinical decisions.

Q8
What other equipment would you need available before anesthetising this patient?
A
  • Ideally, you would want a rapid infuser, a cell salvage system and access to the hemicube.
  • You may also consider cardiac output monitoring. An example of a rapid infuser device is a level
  • one infuser. This allows fluid to be infused under pressure from an automated pressure chamber,
  • while simultaneously preparing a second bag for infusion. The giving sets are wide bore,
  • and unite into one set which is also warmed to an appropriate temperature.
  • The advantage of this device is that fluid may be rapidly pressurised and administered
  • with no interruption during bag changes. It allows the highest flow rate for intravenous fluid
  • administration. The cell saver should be used from the start of the procedure to maximise the
  • volume of autologous blood that may be returned to the patient. The salvaged blood is spun down,
  • washed, and resuspended in normal saline, then returned to the patient, so reducing the need for
  • donor blood transfusions. It is important to note that salvaged blood should not be administered
  • under pressure as this may cause hemolosis. It is important to remember that cell salvaged
  • blood contains red blood cells only, and in cases of massive blood loss, fresh frozen plasma,
  • platelets and cryoprecipitate should be administered as appropriate.

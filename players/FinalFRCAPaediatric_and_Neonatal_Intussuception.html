Q1
Intusception. What is intusception?
A
  • Intusception is caused by the small bowel telescoping as if it was swallowing itself.
  • This usually occurs at the Ileocicle junction.
  • Intusception can lead to venous congestion, bowel edema and intestinal obstruction.
Q2
What age group tend to present with this condition?
A
  • Intusception occurs predominantly in infants and young children.
  • 50% of cases occur in infants and only 10% of cases occur in children older than 5.
Q3
Is intusception seen more in boys or girls?
A
  • The condition is seen more often in boys.
Q4
What are the causes of intusception?
A
  • 90% of cases are idiopathic.
  • Intusception is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in the first year of life.
  • In children over one, intusception may be the first presentation for a number of pathologies,
  • such as Mechel's diverticulum, intestinal polyp, lymphoma, hemalitic uremic syndrome,
  • an oxygen line preparer or poidsiega's syndrome.
Q5
Can intusception recur?
A
  • Yes, the recurrence rate is 5%.

  • An 8 month old boy has been admitted to your hospital.
  • His mother says that he has been intimately crying and pulling his legs up to his chest for the past two days.
  • She found a red-current jelly-like stool in his last nappy.
  • He is vomit-occasionally and his notices nappies have not been wet the last few times she changed them.
  • Your examination reveals a quiet, pale baby.
  • His heart rate is 150 beats per minute and capillary refill time is 4 seconds.
  • His abdomen appears to standard and the infant cries when you examine it.
Q6
How might an infant with intusception present?
A
  • The classical features are of paroxysmal abdominal pain and blood and mucus in the stool.
  • This is sometimes described as having red-current jelly appearance.
  • The infant has episodes of inconsolable crying and drawing up its legs.
  • Vomiting and dehydration may be evident, potentially leading to shock.
  • Abaddominal distension can cause respiratory compromise.
  • Infection, bowel infarction, bleeding and perforation may all occur.
  • On examination, a sausage-shaped mass may be palpable in the right side of the abdomen.
  • The abdomen may be distended with guarding or rigidity.
  • The child may be profoundly shocked.
Q7
What would be your differential diagnosis for this history?
A
  • The infant is most likely to be suffering from a gastrointestinal condition
  • intersception with dehydration and possible sepsis would be my main diagnosis.
  • I would also consider gastroenteritis.
Q8
How do diagnosed interception?
A
  • Diagnosis is made from the history and examination and then investigations.
  • The investigation of choice is an abdominal ultrasound.
  • An air-enema can be diagnostic as well as curative in up to 70% of cases.
  • This intervention is contraindicated if a patient is in shock or has peritonitis or has perforated.
  • An abdominal X-ray may aid the confirmation of bowel obstruction and possible perforation.
Q9
How would you treat interception of air-enema has failed?
A
  • The patient will need either a laparotomy or laparoscopy.
  • Any necrotic bowel found will need to be removed.
Q10
Would you advise air-enema or surgical intervention in this case?
A
  • This infant has evidence of shock because he has tachycardia, increased capillary refill time
  • and reduced urine output and conscious level.
  • The infant should be resuscitated as a matter of urgency and an emergency laparotomy should then be performed.
Q11
How would you resuscitate this infant and prepare them for theatre?
A
  • Try and describe the resuscitation as you would actually do it.
  • This will hopefully help you remember everything.
  • Resuscitation should either be performed in a high dependency unit or in the anesthetic room or recovery area prior to theatre.
  • I would obtain intravenous access and send bloods for full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests and cross-match.
  • I would regularly monitor the vital signs of the infant, including the respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill, urine output and conscious level.
  • Fluid resuscitation is vital.
  • I would give fluid boluses of normal saline, 20 ml per kilo body weight to restore the circulating volume.
  • After each bolus, I would reassess the infant to see whether further fluid was required.
  • My reassessment would consist of reviewing the observations of the infant and looking at the capillary refill time and conscious level.
  • The blood pressure might be maintained due to tachycardia and coexisting pain.
  • In addition to resuscitation fluids, I would give maintenance fluids calculated from the weight of the patient.
  • Heartmon solution can be used to provide this.
  • If the blood results revealed any severe abnormalities in the urea and electrolytes, then I would start correcting these prior to theatre.
  • I would place a nasogastric tube, aspirate the stomach contents and then place the tube on free drainage.
  • If the infant's respiration was inadequate due to either the diaphragmatic splinting from abdominal distension or the effects of severe shock, I would consider intubating and ventilating the infant.
  • This could be done in theatre.
Q12
How would you induce anesthesia in this infant?
A
  • I would ennecetatize this infant with the support of a consultant pediatric anesthetist.
  • A general anesthetic with endotracheal intubation and deventilation is required.
  • Prior to induction, I would ensure that the infant has been adequately resuscitated.
  • I would suction the NG tube and preoxygenate the infant.
  • I would obtain monitoring as per the AAGBI standards and have all of my drugs for resuscitation and induction immediately available.
  • The options for induction of general anesthesia are a gas or intravenous induction.
  • I would prefer a rapid sequence induction with crycoid pressure.
  • I would use thio pentone to induce the infant and then give muscle relaxant.
  • This could be either succumbethonium or an adequate dose of non-depolarizing muscle relaxant such as rockeronium.
  • I would then intubate the infant with a straight blade and pass an uncuffed endotracheal tube.
  • I would check the position of the tube by osculating the chest, observing the capnography trace and checking the length at the lips.
  • Provided the tube was inserted easily and not too tight, I would not require a leak around the tube.
  • I would then allow crycoid pressure to be released and secure the tube in place.
Q13
What about maintenance?
A
  • I would maintain anesthesia using an inhalational agent.
  • Personally, I would use CIVA fluorane.
  • I would then hand ventilate the infant before connecting to either an air's T-piece or a pediatric circle system using pressure-controlled ventilation.
  • Hand ventilation throughout the operation would not be ideal as it is difficult to maintain regular ventilation with stable blood gases and capnography is unreliable.
Q14
What would you give for pain relief?
A
  • I would give a combination of paracetamol, local anaesthetic techniques and opioids for analgesia.
  • Paracetamol can be given intravenously and rectally.
  • The intravenous doses for an infant under 10 kilos are 7.5 mg per kilogram, 4 to 6 hourly, up to a total of 30 mg per kilogram per day.
  • The rectal doses are 40 mg per kilogram loading dose, followed by 20 mg per kilogram, 4 to 6 hourly, to a maximum of 90 mg per kilogram per day.
  • Oral paracetamol, 20 mg, 6 hourly, may be commenced once or all fluids are tolerated.
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatories may be an option provided the infant is adequately hydrated with good renal perfusion and no evidence of sepsis.
  • The dose of oral ibuprofen is 5 mg per kilogram, 4 times a day.
  • Dioclet phenac may be given orally or rectally at a dose of 1 mg per kilogram, 3 times daily, to a maximum of 150 mg per day.
  • Opioids can be used intra and postoperatively. Morphine can be used in judicious boluses or by infusion.
  • It is preferable to fentanyl due to its greater water solubility. It is less lipid soluble and so less cumulative and more predictable.
  • Due to the risk of postoperative respiratory depression, the infant should be carefully monitored.
  • Local anaesthetic techniques such as wound infiltration, epidural or spinal anaesthesia may be used.
  • Wound infiltration is effective and often used. The dose of bupivocane is 2 mg per kilogram.
  • I would also consider a regional technique. Epidural or spinal blockade may be performed but they may be difficult to insert and require expertise in their postoperative management on the ward.
  • A spinal would also be relatively short acting. Contra indications to regional techniques include sepsis and coagulopathy.
Q15
What are your concerns during the operation?
A
  • My main concerns during the operation can be divided into general considerations for pediatric patients and specific concerns regarding the laparotomy.
  • Specifically, I would be vigilant of any surgical complications or hemorrhage. I would aim to maintain good communication with the surgeon.
  • Generally, I would want to closely monitor the infant and avoid excessive heat loss, give adequate doses of analgesics and anaesthetic agents and give appropriate fluids taking into account the fluid losses during surgery.
  • I would aim to maintain the oxygen saturation and tidal carbon dioxide, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature and urine output all within normal levels throughout the operation.
  • I would also check the blood sugar levels of the infant to avoid hypoglycemia.
  • This infant is at risk of hypothermia during the operation due to heat loss from the skin, aided by peripheral vasodilitation and the surgical site.
  • I would aim to increase the ambient temperature of the operating and anaesthetic rooms prior to and during surgery. I would use a heated mattress.
  • I would use a bear hugger and cover the infant's head in order to avoid further heat loss from the skin.
  • Bubble wrap and plastic drapes can also be used. I would warm the fluids given to the patient and use a heat and moisture exchanger to reduce the heat loss from the respiratory system.
  • Use of a circle system also provides warm, fully humidified gas and is therefore superior to a T.P. system.
Q16
How would you approach intraoperative fluid management?
A
  • I would try and consider fluids in terms of pre-existing deficits, maintenance requirements and ongoing losses.
  • I would resuscitate the infant prior to induction of anaesthesia thus addressing the pre-existing deficit.
  • With regards to maintenance fluids, I would calculate this from the weight of the infant.
  • 4 mils per kilo is given for the first 10 kilos of body weight of the infant, followed by 2 mils per kilo for the second 10 kilo body weight and then 1 mil per kilo for each following 10 kilos.
  • I would use Hartman's solution as a continuous infusion, although this may need to be changed depending upon the blood results.
  • A glucose solution may also be required depending on the blood sugar measurements.
  • The ongoing losses would be mainly due to the operation. Blood loss should be closely monitored.
  • As a general guide, approximately 10 mils per kilo per hour of fluid will be required to compensate for evaporation during a laparotomy.
  • This can be replaced as 0.9% saline or Hartman's solution.
  • Colloid may also be required during fluid therapy due to capillary leak.
  • If the infant is septic, bolus is totaling 40 mils per kilo are not unusual.
  • If significant blood loss occurs, then I would give blood and consider the need for clotting factors or fresh frozen plasma.
  • Or be a post-operative plan for this patient.
  • If the infant requires ongoing ventilation due to respiratory compromise, septic shock, blood loss or surgical complications, then they will need to be nursed in a pediatric intensive care unit.
  • Otherwise, I would want the patient to be nursed on a high dependency unit in order to closely monitor the vital signs and pain levels of the infant.
  • Respiratory depression may occur due to the effects of opioid analgesics.
  • Regular blood tests will be necessary to check the full blood count and urea and electrolytes.
  • Intravenous fluids should be continued until the infant is able to feed.
  • Blood transfusion may also be required.
